# 🦐 Krill, Whales, And Anti-Krill  
**First created:** 2026-05-31 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-31  
*A public metaphor map for undersea drones, submarines, cables, hostile sensors, and the hidden sea layer.*  

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

“Krill, whales, and anti-krill” is not official defence language.

It is a public map.

The point is not to rename military systems. The point is to help people understand the shape of the undersea system without needing classified detail, procurement acronyms, or a naval glossary large enough to stun a haddock.

The hidden sea layer is difficult to explain because it joins things that usually sit in separate public boxes:

* submarines;
* undersea cables;
* autonomous drones;
* seabed sensors;
* AI-assisted analysis;
* hostile-state probing;
* AUKUS;
* deterrence;
* cable repair;
* ports, shipyards, and offshore energy.

The metaphor gives those pieces a simple shape:

> **Whales** are the big strategic assets that must remain hard to find.
> **Krill** are the small distributed systems that help watch, protect, and support the hidden sea layer.
> **Anti-krill** is the hostile version: sensors, mappers, trackers, interferers, and systems that try to make our whales visible or our cables vulnerable.
> **The reef** is the infrastructure everything lives on.

It is slightly ridiculous.

That is why it works.

---

## 🐋 Whales

Whales are the big things.

In this map, whales include:

* deterrent submarines;
* attack submarines;
* major naval vessels;
* high-value maritime platforms;
* strategic assets that matter because they are hard to find, hard to track, or hard to threaten confidently.

The whale does not need to be magically invisible.

It needs the sea around it to remain difficult enough to read that an adversary cannot confidently find, classify, track, target, and neutralise it.

That uncertainty matters.

Deterrence does not require fairy dust. It requires doubt in the adversary’s mind.

If the ocean becomes easier to read, the whale does not instantly become useless. But the protective darkness around it starts needing more active defence.

That is where krill enters.

---

## 🦐 Krill

Krill are the small distributed systems.

They are not glamorous in the same way as a submarine, a carrier, or a missile system. That is fine. Not everything important has to look dramatic on a recruitment poster.

In this map, krill can mean:

* undersea drones;
* autonomous or semi-autonomous underwater vehicles;
* seabed sensors;
* inspection systems;
* cable-watchers;
* anomaly-detection tools;
* maritime data feeds;
* AI-assisted monitoring;
* systems that help detect, map, classify, or understand what is happening below the waterline.

Krill help make the hidden sea layer legible to us.

They can inspect infrastructure. They can watch cable routes. They can help detect suspicious change. They can extend awareness into places humans cannot continuously watch. They can reduce risk to crewed vessels. They can help build a more persistent picture of a difficult environment.

On their own, they may look small.

Systemically, they are the layer that helps keep the whales hidden, the cables noticed, and the reef alive.

Not glamorous.

Vital.

Very annoying to adversaries.

Excellent.

---

## 🕷️ Anti-Krill

Anti-krill is the hostile version of the distributed layer.

It is not one scary robot.

It is the ecology of systems that could make the sea more readable to an adversary, or make civilian infrastructure easier to threaten.

Anti-krill can include:

* hostile seabed mappers;
* loitering drones;
* cable interferers;
* undersea trackers;
* passive sensors;
* active sensing systems;
* spoofing tools;
* acoustic, magnetic, wake, or pattern-detection support;
* cyber compromise of maritime or cable-support systems;
* systems that help an adversary narrow the search space around submarines, cables, ports, routes, or chokepoints.

The danger is not only attack.

The danger is also mapping.

A hostile actor may gain leverage by learning where things are, how systems behave, where repair is difficult, which routes matter, which assets are sensitive, and which public stories become strangely hard to explain.

Anti-krill tries to make the hidden sea layer less hidden for them and more fragile for us.

That is the problem.

---

## 🪸 The Reef

The reef is what everyone lives on without noticing.

In this map, the reef includes:

* undersea cables;
* cable landing stations;
* ports;
* shipyards;
* offshore energy;
* repair vessels;
* maritime chokepoints;
* logistics routes;
* the commercial and public infrastructure that keeps civilian life functioning.

The reef is not only “defence.”

It is finance, cloud services, government communication, media, business continuity, civil society, military and diplomatic coordination, and ordinary daily life.

Most people only notice the reef when something breaks.

That is why it needs a metaphor before the emergency.

If the reef is invisible to the public, the public cannot understand why krill matter or why whales need protecting.

---

## 🌊 Ocean Opacity

Ocean opacity is the hinge.

The ocean is hard to read. That hiddenness can protect us and expose us at the same time.

It protects us because submarines benefit from uncertainty. A deterrent submarine is safer when an adversary cannot confidently know where it is, track it continuously, or target it reliably.

It exposes us because cable incidents can be ambiguous. A cable disruption may be an accident, a storm, a dragged anchor, poor maintenance, a grey-zone act, a proxy operation, or something else entirely. The harder the sea is to read, the harder attribution becomes.

So the goal is not simply:

> make the ocean transparent.

That would create its own dangers.

The goal is more subtle:

> keep enough darkness around our whales, while improving our ability to detect threats to the reef.

Friendly krill helps us understand what we need to understand.

Hostile anti-krill tries to understand what we need to keep uncertain.

That is the contest.

That is Krill Wars.

## 🛰️ Where AUKUS Fits

AUKUS fits because it is not only a submarine deal.

The submarine story matters, especially for Australia. But the advanced-capability side of AUKUS also points toward autonomy, AI, cyber, electronic warfare, undersea systems, shared payloads, standards, exercises, and allied interoperability.

Publicly, that means:

> AUKUS is helping allies build the undersea technology layer around the whales and the reef.

Or, in this metaphor:

> AUKUS is partly about building friendly krill before adversary anti-krill makes the hidden sea layer too readable.

That does not mean every AUKUS system is a tiny drone.

It means the direction of travel is clear: the undersea domain is becoming more layered, more technical, more autonomous, more data-heavy, and more strategically contested.

The point is not only to build bigger whales.

The point is to protect the sea around them.

## 🧮 Why AI Belongs Here

AI belongs here because humans cannot constantly watch, classify, interpret, and cross-reference every undersea signal, cable route, maritime anomaly, acoustic pattern, infrastructure warning, cyber cue, and vessel movement by hand.

That does not mean “give the machine the keys to the sea.”

It means AI-assisted systems can be useful when pointed at the right problem:

* cable anomaly detection;
* seabed monitoring;
* maritime awareness;
* infrastructure protection;
* hostile-system detection;
* pattern analysis;
* triage for human review.

The political distinction matters.

AI pointed at ordinary civilians can become a suspicion machine.

AI pointed at undersea infrastructure protection can be legitimate defensive capability.

That is the “krill, not Doris” rule.

Do not waste strategic compute building better ways to treat ordinary life as suspicious.

Use serious compute for cables, ports, cyber defence, maritime awareness, hostile-state interference, and the systems that keep civilian life functioning.

## 🗞️ Why The Metaphor Helps Media

Official language has a job.

It serves policy, procurement, classification, and specialist coordination.

But official language is often terrible at public memory.

“AUKUS Pillar II payloads and enabling systems” may be correct.

It is also emotionally dead.

Most people will not remember it, search it, explain it to a friend, or connect it to cables, submarines, AI, Australia, China, Russia, recruitment, and the physical internet.

“Krill, whales, and anti-krill” gives the public a structure.

Use the metaphor beside the official term, not instead of it:

> AUKUS Pillar II — undersea drones, cable defence, seabed sensing, and the krill layer around the whales.

That lets specialists keep the official language while normal people get an entry point.

The media does not need to make defence childish.

It does need to stop making the cool part unreadable.

---

## 🧿 What This Metaphor Must Not Do

A metaphor is a bridge.

It is not evidence.

It must not be used to:

* imply that “krill” is an official defence category;
* imply that “anti-krill” identifies a specific actor without evidence;
* imply exact capabilities;
* imply Trident is already compromised;
* imply that all underwater drones are defensive;
* imply that all secrecy is suspicious;
* imply that all undersea activity is hostile;
* collapse Russia, China, Iran, proxies, criminals, commercial actors, and accidents into one blob;
* replace careful attribution with vibes;
* excuse reckless disclosure of operational detail.

This matters.

The metaphor is useful because it makes the system memorable.

It becomes dangerous if it starts pretending to know more than it knows.

Keep the map.

Do not invent the treasure.
