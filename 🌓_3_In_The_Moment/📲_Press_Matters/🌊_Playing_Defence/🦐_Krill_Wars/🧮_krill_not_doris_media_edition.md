# 🧮 Krill Not Doris — Media Edition  
**First created:** 2026-05-31 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-31  
*A plain-language media explainer on why strategic compute should protect cables, ports, cyber systems, and the undersea layer — not routine civilian life.*  

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

**Krill Not Doris** is not an anti-technology slogan.

It is a mission-allocation test.

A serious maritime democracy needs data centres, AI, sensors, modelling, cyber capability, autonomous systems, and high-end analysis. That is not the argument.

The argument is where that capacity should point.

It can point at real strategic infrastructure:

* undersea cables;
* ports;
* hospitals;
* grids;
* cloud systems;
* cyber defence;
* offshore energy;
* maritime routes;
* hostile-state interference;
* undersea drones and sensors.

Or it can point at ordinary civilians living ordinary lives.

That second route is where things become stupid, brittle, coercive, and politically poisonous.

Doris is the ordinary civilian.

Doris is not the submarine.  

---

## 🦐 What “Krill Not Doris” Means

In this media pack:

**Krill** means the strategic infrastructure layer: undersea drones, cable monitoring, port resilience, cyber defence, maritime awareness, seabed sensing, anomaly detection, and the systems that help protect the hidden sea layer.

**Doris** means ordinary civilian life being over-monitored, scored, nudged, profiled, or treated as suspicious by default.

The point is not “Doris bad.”

Doris is fine.

Doris is doing her shopping, managing her bills, ringing the GP, arguing with a utility company, buying biscuits, forgetting a password, and trying to get through the week without becoming a dashboard event.

Doris is not the threat surface.

The state should not build a telescope and point it through the kitchen window while the cables lie in the sea.

---

## 🌊 What Strategic Compute Should Be For

Strategic compute should be aimed at serious strategic problems.

For a coastal state, that includes:

* undersea domain awareness;
* cable anomaly detection;
* cable-route resilience;
* port security and continuity;
* maritime cyber defence;
* hostile-state interference mapping;
* offshore energy protection;
* UUV autonomy testing;
* anti-submarine support;
* emergency communications modelling;
* hospital and grid resilience;
* disaster response;
* cloud and data-centre continuity;
* critical supplier mapping;
* public communication during infrastructure stress.

These are not decorative problems.

They are the systems ordinary people live inside.

If cables fail, ports stall, hospitals lose connectivity, cyber systems are degraded, or hostile actors gain leverage over critical infrastructure, ordinary life is affected very quickly.

That is where serious data capability belongs.

Not because technology is magic.

Because the threat surface is real.

---

## 🧍 What It Should Not Default To

Strategic compute should not default to routine suspicion of ordinary civilian life.

The failure mode is familiar:

* behavioural scoring;
* dragnet suspicion architecture;
* keyword panic;
* overfitting to past patterns;
* bias laundering through technical language;
* institutional deskilling;
* procurement capture;
* political temptation;
* chilled public behaviour;
* legitimacy loss;
* endless expansion pressure.

Once a system starts treating ordinary life as a risk object, it rarely knows where to stop.

Everything becomes a signal.

Shopping becomes a signal.

Travel becomes a signal.

Debt becomes a signal.

Speech becomes a signal.

Stress becomes a signal.

Protest becomes a signal.

Curiosity becomes a signal.

That is not strategic seriousness.

That is administrative appetite with a threat label on it.

If everything is monitored as suspicious, the state has not become safer. It has become easier to distract.

---

## 🧿 Legitimacy Is Infrastructure

Public trust is not soft.

It is infrastructure.

During a cable outage, cyber incident, infrastructure disruption, hostile information operation, or grey-zone event, the state needs public cooperation. People need to believe the warning. They need to understand the risk. They need to know what to do. They need enough confidence in institutions not to panic, disengage, or assume every official message is manipulation.

That trust is damaged when “national security technology” becomes associated with watching ordinary civilians.

If the public learns that data capability means being scored, nudged, profiled, or treated as pre-suspicious, the state weakens the consent base it needs for real resilience.

People are more likely to accept serious national data infrastructure when it is visibly aimed at:

* cables;
* ports;
* grids;
* hospitals;
* maritime safety;
* hostile interference;
* cyber defence;
* emergency response;
* infrastructure continuity.

They are less likely to accept it when it looks like the state built a machine for watching Doris make tea.

That is not only a civil-liberties point.

It is a defence point.

Legitimacy is part of resilience.

## 🛰️ Why AUKUS Makes This Concrete

AUKUS undersea drones make the issue concrete.

They give the public a clear example of AI, autonomy, sensors, data, and drones being used where the case is easier to understand:

> hard-to-watch infrastructure, hostile-state exposure, cable defence, seabed awareness, and the underwater systems that support national resilience.

This is where the robots should be.

Undersea drones and AI-assisted systems can help inspect, monitor, classify, detect, map, and protect spaces humans cannot continuously watch.

That does not make every system safe.

It does not remove the need for law, oversight, environmental responsibility, procurement scrutiny, or clear limits.

But it does make the basic public case much stronger than most domestic surveillance use cases.

There is a difference between:

> using AI to help detect threats to the physical internet

and:

> using AI to treat ordinary civilians as suspicion objects.

That difference should be said clearly.

## 🦾 AI Here Is Not AI Everywhere

The phrase “AI” is too broad to settle the argument.

The question is not only what the tool is.

The question is:

* what is it pointed at?
* who controls it?
* who is affected by it?
* what happens when it is wrong?
* can it be challenged?
* is it supporting human judgement or replacing it?
* is it protecting infrastructure or managing people?
* does it preserve democratic consent or corrode it?

A rough public map:

| Use case                                      | Public legitimacy concern                                                   |
| --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| AI watching benefit claimants at scale        | High risk of coercion, suspicion, chilling, and error harm                  |
| AI scoring protest behaviour                  | Democratic danger; risk of lawful activity being treated as threat          |
| AI monitoring ordinary civilian behaviour     | High risk of dragnet normalisation and legitimacy loss                      |
| AI detecting cable anomalies                  | Legitimate infrastructure defence if bounded and governed                   |
| AI supporting maritime cyber defence          | Legitimate if accountable, proportionate, and lawfully constrained          |
| AI assisting undersea monitoring              | Legitimate if tied to clear defensive purpose, oversight, and safety limits |
| AI supporting disaster or hospital resilience | Strong public-interest case if transparent and accountable                  |

The problem is not the tool in isolation.

The problem is target, power, oversight, and consequence.

## 🎖️ Recruitment And Skills

This also matters for recruitment.

Young people are constantly told that AI, drones, data, robotics, and cyber are the future.

Then the public often sees those technologies used in creepy, exploitative, coercive, or stupid ways.

That is not exactly inspiring.

The undersea layer offers a better story:

> Come build the systems that protect cables, ports, ships, cyber networks, hospitals, grids, and undersea infrastructure — not creepy dashboards for watching your neighbours.

That opens defence to people who may not come through traditional martial imagery.

Some people come through engineering.

Some come through robotics.

Some come through cyber.

Some come through logistics.

Some come through environmental monitoring.

Some come through maritime law.

Some come through “hang on, the internet is in the sea and someone has to protect it?”

Good.

That is a real route into public service.

## ⚖️ Governance Rules

Krill needs governance too.

“Better than watching Doris” is not the same as “no oversight required.”

A serious model needs:

* clear lawful basis;
* explicit mission limits;
* human accountability;
* auditability;
* procurement scrutiny;
* environmental responsibility;
* limits on autonomous escalation;
* transparent public-purpose framing;
* separation between infrastructure protection and domestic population management;
* review of bias, error, and misuse risks;
* democratic scrutiny of dual-use capabilities;
* rules against mission creep from cable defence into civilian monitoring.

Undersea systems are not harmless because they are underwater.

AI is not virtuous because it has been pointed away from Doris.

Drones are not automatically good because they protect cables.

The argument is more disciplined than that:

> This is a stronger, more legitimate mission for strategic compute — and precisely because it matters, it needs adult governance.

## 🧭 Plain Map

> **Krill** = strategic infrastructure protection.
> Undersea drones, sensors, cable monitoring, cyber defence, maritime awareness, and resilience systems.

> **Doris** = ordinary civilian life.
> Shopping, healthcare, speech, stress, protest, travel, family, work, and daily admin should not be treated as suspicious by default.

> **Compute** = scarce national capacity.
> Data centres, AI, modelling, analytics, cyber capability, engineering talent, money, energy, legitimacy, and public patience.

> **The choice** = point the machine at the real threat surface, not the administratively convenient one.

> **The rule** = cables, ports, cyber, hospitals, grids, hostile interference, maritime resilience, and undersea systems before routine civilian scoring.
