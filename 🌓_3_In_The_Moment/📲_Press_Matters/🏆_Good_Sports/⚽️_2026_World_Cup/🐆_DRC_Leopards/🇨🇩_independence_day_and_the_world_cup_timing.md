## 📰 Reporting Standard  

Do not write this moment as if DR Congo appeared from nowhere.

Do not treat Independence Day as trivia.

Do not turn the team into a symbolic burden.

Do not make the players carry the entire history of colonialism.

Do not erase the timing either.

Better phrasing:

```text id="276hpt"
DR Congo’s World Cup visibility arrives beside the country’s Independence Day, sharpening the public frame around national memory, diaspora pride, and the right to appear on one’s own terms.

The timing does not make the match less about football. It makes the football more publicly resonant.

The Leopards’ visibility interrupts a familiar international pattern in which DR Congo is seen primarily through crisis, extraction, or humanitarian need.

Coverage should recognise the timing without turning the team into a history lesson.
```

The standard is balance.

Football stays football.

The public map gets larger.

## 🛑 What Bad Reporting Does  

Bad reporting either ignores the timing completely or turns it into heavy symbolic theatre.

Bad routes:

```text id="rxz9a0"
DR Congo are the war-torn outsiders.
The Leopards carry the hopes of a troubled nation.
The match is a redemption story for Congo.
The team are playing for independence.
The players represent suffering.
The country is finally visible because England are playing them.
```

That is not good enough.

The team are not props for Western feeling.

The players are not a humanitarian explainer.

The country does not become real because England notices it.

The timing matters because Congo is already real.

The world’s attention is the late arrival.
