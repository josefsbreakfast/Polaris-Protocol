# 📰 How Not To Report Congo  
**First created:** 2026-07-01 | **Last updated:** 2026-07-02  
*Press Matters rule sheet on DR Congo, World Cup coverage, extraction shorthand, crisis framing, racialised fan panic, atrocity reduction, cultural laziness, and how to report Congo without turning Congolese people into scenery for someone else’s story.*

Because bad coverage is not neutral.

## 📰 Core Frame  

DR Congo is often reported through a narrow set of keywords:

```text id="lazy-keywords"
war
rape
minerals
rebels
poverty
chaos
corruption
refugees
cobalt
coltan
crisis
tribes
```

Those words may sometimes point toward real issues.

But when they become the whole map, they stop informing.

They start reducing.

The problem is not that journalists mention conflict or mining.

The problem is when Congo is allowed to appear only as:

```text id="bad-appearance"
wound
mine
warning
burden
mystery
spectacle
failure
supply zone
humanitarian backdrop
```

A country is not a content category.

## 🐆 The World Cup Problem  

World Cup coverage creates a specific risk.

The DRC Leopards make Congo visible through:

```text id="world-cup-visibility"
football
teamwork
supporters
style
music
chants
dance
diaspora pride
public joy
national memory
```

Bad media may immediately drag that back into:

```text id="world-cup-reduction"
war-torn nation
conflict-ravaged Congo
minerals and misery
African chaos
raw passion
feral support
hope amid horror
football fairytale from hell
```

That is not depth.

That is extraction with adjectives.

The Leopards are not there to decorate Western guilt.

They are there to play football.

## 🚫 Do Not Use Congo As Misery Wallpaper  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-misery-wallpaper"
In a country known for war, rape, and minerals, football offers a rare smile.
```

Better:

```text id="better-misery-wallpaper"
DR Congo’s World Cup visibility comes alongside serious issues including conflict in the east, displacement, resource politics, and unequal global attention. Coverage should hold those realities without reducing Congolese players and supporters to symbols of suffering.
```

The difference matters.

The first line uses suffering as atmosphere.

The second line preserves context and personhood.

---

## ⛏️ Do Not Move Straight From Congo To Cobalt  

Bad order:

```text id="bad-cobalt-order"
DR Congo is rich in cobalt. Its football team has also reached the World Cup.
```

Better order:

```text id="better-cobalt-order"
DR Congo is a country of people, football, music, language, style, politics, grief, humour, and public joy. It is also central to global mineral supply chains through cobalt, copper, coltan, and other resources.
```

Order is not cosmetic.

Order tells the reader what matters first.

People first.

Minerals second.

---

## 🪖 Do Not Call It Chaos When There Is Governance  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-chaos"
Eastern Congo is chaotic and lawless.
```

Better:

```text id="better-chaos"
Eastern Congo is shaped by armed groups, state weakness, regional security narratives, foreign interference, mineral routes, displacement, and conflict governance.
```

“Chaos” is lazy because it hides structure.

Ask:

```text id="governance-questions"
Who controls?
Who taxes?
Who moves?
Who arms?
Who denies?
Who profits?
Who is displaced?
Who is protected?
Who is made invisible?
```

If you can answer those questions, it is not just chaos.

---

## 🧲 Do Not Say Resources Cause War  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-resources-war"
Congo is unstable because it has resources.
```

Better:

```text id="better-resources-war"
Resource wealth can become destabilising when global demand, weak accountability, foreign pressure, armed finance, insecure land rights, elite capture, and unequal processing power turn minerals into pressure points.
```

Do not call the people cursed.

Follow the pressure.

Follow the value.

Follow the gate.

---

## 🇨🇩 Do Not Make Congo A Symbol Before It Is A Country  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-symbol-country"
Congo symbolises Africa’s tragedy.
```

No.

Congo is not Africa’s tragedy mascot.

Better:

```text id="better-symbol-country"
DR Congo is a large, diverse, politically significant country with its own histories, languages, regions, cultures, institutions, conflicts, and public life. It should not be used as shorthand for an entire continent’s suffering.
```

A country is not a metaphor until it has first been allowed to be real.

---

## 🌍 Do Not Confuse The Congos  

Do not use:

```text id="bad-congo-naming"
Congo
```

carelessly where precision matters.

Clarify:

```text id="congo-clarity"
Democratic Republic of the Congo
DR Congo
DRC
Congo-Kinshasa
Republic of the Congo
Congo-Brazzaville
```

Do not mix them.

Do not treat the name confusion as the country’s fault.

The web is messy because colonial history, language politics, platform metadata, and Western search habits are messy.

See: [🏷️ Naming RDC DRC Zaire And Searchability](./🏷️_naming_rdc_drc_zaire_and_searchability.md)

## 🗣️ Do Not Treat French As The Whole House  

Bad assumption:

```text id="bad-french"
Congo is Francophone, so French-language framing explains it.
```

Better:

```text id="better-french"
French is one doorway. It is not the house.
```

DR Congo also moves through:

```text id="language-stack"
Lingala
Swahili
Tshiluba
Kikongo
French
diaspora languages
football language
music language
platform language
gesture
style
dance
```

If coverage treats Paris as culture and Kinshasa as colour, the map is still colonial.

---

## 🎶 Do Not Hear Noise Where There Is Language  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-noise"
The Congolese fans created a wall of noise.
```

Better:

```text id="better-noise"
Congolese supporters brought coordinated chants, rhythm, dance, and national songs into the stadium.
```

Where possible, name the chant.

Translate carefully.

Ask people who know.

Do not turn Lingala, rhythm, or unfamiliar supporter culture into menace because you cannot be bothered to learn.

---

## 💃🏿 Do Not Treat Dance As Disorder  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-dance"
Wild scenes erupted among Congolese fans.
```

Better:

```text id="better-dance"
Congolese supporters celebrated with high-energy dancing and chanting.
```

A dance is not a riot.

A chant is not a threat.

A loud crowd is not disorder.

A police line is not proof of danger.

Black joy does not require a safety warning.

---

## 🖤 Do Not Animalise The Leopards  

The team nickname is **Les Léopards**.

That does not give media permission to write like this:

```text id="bad-animalise"
the Leopards prowl
feral energy
jungle atmosphere
untamed passion
raw African power
England must cage the Leopards
```

No.

Use the nickname with care.

The Leopards are a football team.

Congolese supporters are human beings.

Do not let a team name become a racialised permission slip.

---

## 🛑 Do Not Preload Fan Panic  

Bad coverage pattern:

```text id="fan-panic-pattern"
Find a clip.
Remove context.
Add anxious caption.
Use racialised adjectives.
Treat unfamiliar joy as threat.
Ask police for comment.
Call it tension.
Let the pile-on do the rest.
```

Do not do this.

One clip is not a crowd.

One incident is not a people.

One caption is not context.

One confrontation is not national character.

Do not let racism wear a steward’s jacket.

---

## 🧾 Do Not Turn Every Legal Issue Into Certainty Theatre  

For live legal and political matters, use careful categories:

```text id="legal-care"
alleged
reported
documented
contested
under investigation
subject to legal proceedings
provisional measures
no final ruling
official denial
UN expert reporting
human-rights reporting
```

Bad line:

```text id="bad-legal-certainty"
Everyone knows exactly who did everything.
```

Also bad:

```text id="bad-legal-minimisation"
Nothing can be said until a final judgment exists.
```

Better:

```text id="better-legal-balance"
The public record includes allegations, denials, UN reporting, legal action, and documented harms. Coverage should distinguish settled facts, credible reporting, contested claims, and unresolved legal questions.
```

That is how you avoid both propaganda and laundering.

## 👑 Do Not Flatten Lumumba  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-lumumba"
Patrice Lumumba, a Cold War figure, was killed in murky circumstances.
```

Better:

```text id="better-lumumba"
Patrice Lumumba, Congo’s first prime minister after independence, was killed in January 1961 after being removed from power, detained, and transferred to Katanga. Belgian responsibility and documented CIA plotting are central to the public record, while the exact operational chain to the final killing should be stated carefully.
```

Do not use “murky” to hide power.

Do not use shorthand so loose that the whole point can be dismissed.

Precision protects memory.

---

## 🗽 Do Not Make Visa Exclusion A Vibes-Only Claim  

For Lumumba Vea, do not write:

```text id="bad-visa"
America banned him because it feared Lumumba.
```

without evidence.

Better:

```text id="better-visa"
Lumumba Vea’s reported visa exclusion carried symbolic force because he is publicly associated with Patrice Lumumba, anti-colonial memory, and Congolese national visibility. The administrative motive should be reported carefully and not inflated beyond the evidence.
```

Symbolic meaning matters.

Motive still needs proof.

Hold both.

---

## 🔗 Do Not Treat Supply Chains As Background  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-supply"
Congo supplies minerals for the modern world.
```

Better:

```text id="better-supply"
DR Congo’s mineral wealth sits inside global technology, energy, military, and infrastructure supply chains. The central question is not only what Congo supplies, but who profits, who is harmed, who controls processing, and whether Congolese communities benefit from the value under their land.
```

Supply chains are not neutral.

They are moral routes.

---

## 🧍 Do Not Make Congolese People Responsible For The Systems Exploiting Them  

Bad implication:

```text id="bad-responsibility"
Congo is corrupt, violent, mineral-rich, and unstable.
```

Better:

```text id="better-responsibility"
Congolese people live within systems shaped by local actors, foreign states, armed groups, corporate interests, colonial history, regional politics, global demand, and unequal accountability.
```

The people are not the profiteers.

The country is not the mine.

The team is not the supply chain.

---

## 📱 Do Not Make Platform Absence Look Natural  

Bad assumption:

```text id="bad-platform"
If Congo content is not showing up, maybe there is not much of it.
```

Better:

```text id="better-platform"
If DR Congo’s music, chants, fashion, football humour, fan edits, and diaspora commentary are hard to find during a World Cup moment, that may reflect platform routing, language friction, colonial metadata, and search failure — not cultural absence.
```

Congo is not invisible.

Congo is misrouted.

---

## 🏟️ Do Not Force Football To Redeem Suffering  

Bad line:

```text id="bad-redemption"
Football gives a broken country hope.
```

Better:

```text id="better-redemption"
Football is one public space where Congolese people can appear through skill, style, humour, national pride, and collective joy, even while serious political and humanitarian crises remain live.
```

Football does not redeem state failure.

Football does not heal displacement.

Football does not solve extraction.

It gives people another way to appear.

That matters without making it magical.

---

## 📸 Do Not Photograph Pain Better Than Joy  

A reporting pattern to avoid:

```text id="pain-photo-pattern"
High-resolution suffering.
Low-resolution culture.
Expert quotes for conflict.
No names for artists.
Maps for minerals.
No translations for chants.
Long history for war.
One sentence for joy.
```

That is not balance.

That is hierarchy.

If you can explain coltan, you can translate a chant.

If you can name an armed group, you can name a musician.

If you can map a mine, you can map a supporter culture.

## ✅ Better Reporting Checklist  

Before publishing, ask:

```text id="better-reporting-checklist"
1. Have I named Congolese people as people, not only victims?
2. Have I separated DR Congo from Republic of the Congo where needed?
3. Have I avoided lazy “chaos” language?
4. Have I avoided animalising the Leopards?
5. Have I translated or checked chants before describing them?
6. Have I separated atmosphere from disorder?
7. Have I avoided using suffering as colour?
8. Have I placed minerals after people, not before them?
9. Have I distinguished allegation, denial, documentation, and legal finding?
10. Have I avoided turning footballers into symbols of every national wound?
11. Have I included Congolese voices where possible?
12. Have I avoided making Western guilt the centre?
13. Have I named joy with the same seriousness as pain?
14. Have I explained supply chains without blaming civilians?
15. Have I asked who profits from the frame I am using?
```

If the answer is no, rewrite.

## 🧠 What This Node Is Not Claiming  

This node does **not** claim:

```text id="not-claiming"
journalists should ignore war
journalists should ignore mining
journalists should ignore sexual violence
journalists should ignore M23 / Rwanda-DRC tensions
journalists should ignore supply chains
journalists should write only joyful stories
all criticism of DR Congo coverage is censorship
all mistakes are malicious
football coverage must become a legal briefing
```

The claim is narrower:

Coverage of DR Congo must stop reducing Congolese people to crisis, mineral, war, spectacle, or Western conscience prompt. Good reporting can hold conflict, extraction, colonial history, legal complexity, football, music, dance, style, humour, and public joy without letting any one frame erase the people.

## 🧭 Press Matters Rule  

Do not make Congo small.

Ask:

```text id="press-rule"
Who is being reduced?
Who is being centred?
Who is being named?
Who is being made scenery?
Who is being treated as evidence?
Who is being allowed joy?
Who is being allowed complexity?
Who profits from the shorthand?
```

Report conflict without making Congo only conflict.

Report minerals without making Congo only mine.

Report football without making Congo only fairytale.

Report joy without erasing grief.

Report grief without stealing joy.
