This is a search-aid note.

Not a lyrics archive.  
Not a fluent Lingala guide.  
Not a musicology dissertation.  
Not a definitive translation file.  
Not a claim to own or explain Congolese supporter culture from outside it.

This note does one job:

It helps journalists, editors, researchers, civic readers, and non-Lingala-speaking viewers move from **sound** to **search** without flattening Congolese supporter language into noise, spectacle, or “colour.”

The working languages are mainly:

```text id="kwm6rf"

Lingala

football slang
diaspora platform language
music hooks
chant fragments
```

The point is not to pretend fluency.

The point is to stop the search path breaking.

---

## 🎧 Why This Node Exists  

A viewer can hear the chant.

They can feel the rhythm.

They can recognise that something is happening.

But if they cannot spell the words, identify the language, or know which search terms to try, the story becomes harder to find.

That is not a small issue.

It is part of the same colonial metadata problem this folder is tracking.

A culture should not have to pass through English to become searchable.

A chant should not have to become “noise” because a British journalist cannot type it.

A song should not become “vibes” because a platform cannot route it properly.

A democratic internet should help people move from sound to source.

Too often, it leaves them with caricature.

## 🎶 Working Phrase Bank  

### `Allez les Léopards`

**Language:** French  
**Working gloss:** Go Leopards / Come on, Leopards  
**Use:** Safe general supporter phrase.  
**Search use:** Strong.  

```text id="48cwql"
"Allez les Léopards"
"Allez les Léopards" RDC
"Allez les Léopards" Coupe du Monde
"Allez les Léopards" World Cup
```

**Notes:**  
This is the most accessible search phrase for French-speaking coverage and fan material.

Low translation risk.

Good starting point.

---

### `Bendele likolo`

**Language:** Lingala  
**Working gloss:** Flag up / raise the flag / flag high  
**Use:** National pride, supporter slogan, celebratory caption.  
**Search use:** Strong, but needs context checking.  

```text id="5k9was"
"Bendele likolo"
"Bendele likolo" RDC
"Bendele likolo" Léopards
"Bendele likolo" Coupe du Monde
```

**Notes:**  
This phrase matters because it routes football into national pride.

Do not over-translate it into stiff English.

The emotional register is likely closer to flag raised / flag high / the flag is up than a literal bureaucratic phrase.

Needs fluent-speaker checking.

---

### `Bendele ekweya te`

**Language:** Lingala  
**Working gloss:** The flag must not fall / the flag does not fall  
**Use:** National pride, resilience, defiance.  
**Search use:** Useful, but context-dependent.  

```text id="r2kkjn"
"Bendele ekweya te"
"Bendele ekweya te" RDC
"Bendele ekweya te" Léopards
"Bendele ekweya te" Congo
```

**Notes:**  
Potentially strong for Independence Day / World Cup timing.

Do not flatten into generic patriotism too quickly.

Check whether it appears in songs, captions, chants, or broader political/cultural use.

---

### `Fimbu`

**Language:** Lingala / Congolese football-cultural usage  
**Working gloss:** commonly associated with “whip” / *fimbu* celebration language  
**Use:** Goal celebration, dance reference, supporter hype, Les Léopards football culture.  
**Search use:** Very strong.  

```text id="mmnnsn"
"Fimbu"
"Fimbu" RDC
"Fimbu" Léopards
"Fimbu" football RDC
"Fimbu" Coupe du Monde
"Fimbu chicotte"
"Fimbu na fimbu"
```

**Notes:**  
This needs care.

Do not translate it as a flat violent word and then treat the celebration as aggression.

Football language often transforms words through performance, humour, rhythm, collective meaning, and celebration.

The point is not “Congolese fans are invoking violence.”

The point is that a term has travelled through music, dance, and team celebration into supporter culture.

Context first.

Translation second.

Moral panic never.

---

### `Fimbu na fimbu`

**Language:** Lingala / football-cultural usage  
**Working gloss:** fimbu after fimbu / fimbu again and again / repeated fimbu  
**Use:** Celebration phrase, hype caption, fan expression.  
**Search use:** Strong.  

```text id="e1qbyh"
"Fimbu na fimbu"
"Fimbu na fimbu" RDC
"Fimbu na fimbu" Léopards
"Fimbu na fimbu" football
```

**Notes:**  
Likely better handled as performance language than literal translation.

Needs fluent and cultural-context checking.

Potential bridge to dance/music node.

See: [💃🏿 Dance Politics Body Language And Congolese Joy](./💃🏿_dance_politics_body_language_and_congolese_joy.md)

---

### `Fimbu chicotte`

**Language:** Lingala / French-colonial residue / football-cultural usage  
**Working gloss:** fimbu / chicotte reference; needs careful context  
**Use:** Dance / celebration / football reference.  
**Search use:** Useful but sensitive.  

```text id="k1enl8"
"Fimbu chicotte"
"Fimbu chicotte" RDC
"Fimbu chicotte" Léopards
"Fimbu chicotte" football
```

**Notes:**  
This one needs extra care because “chicotte” carries colonial punishment / whip associations.

Do not overclaim.

Do not sanitise either.

This could be a place where football celebration, music language, colonial residue, humour, and embodied performance sit close together.

Needs Congolese-authored explanation before any strong interpretive claim.

---

### `Toko kende`

**Language:** Lingala  
**Working gloss:** We will go / we are going / let’s go  
**Use:** Hype phrase, forward motion, supporter energy.  
**Search use:** Medium to strong.  

```text id="lqujl4"
"Toko kende"
"Toko kende" RDC
"Toko kende" Léopards
"Toko kende" Coupe du Monde
```

**Notes:**  
Could appear across non-football contexts too.

Search with team terms to reduce drift.

---

### `Oyo biso`

**Language:** Lingala  
**Working gloss:** This is us / this is ours / this is who we are  
**Use:** Identity, pride, caption language, possible supporter usage.  
**Search use:** Medium.  

```text id="zbbssj"
"Oyo biso"
"Oyo biso" RDC
"Oyo biso" Léopards
"Oyo biso" Congo
```

**Notes:**  
Context-dependent.

Do not force into a fixed slogan without examples.

Useful as a possible doorway into fan captions and identity language.

## 📰 How Journalists Should Use This  

Use this note to ask better questions.

Do not use it to perform expertise you do not have.

A useful journalist approach:

```text id="j7uw71"
I heard this phrase.
I think it may be Lingala / French / Congolese French.
These are the possible spellings I have found.
This is the rough working gloss.
I have checked it with a fluent speaker / Congolese source where possible.
Here is the song / chant / creator / context.
```

A bad journalist approach:

```text id="8unbjz"
The crowd made tribal noise.
The fans brought colour.
The chant roughly means something aggressive.
The dancing was primitive / wild / sexual / chaotic.
The language was incomprehensible, so we ignored it.
```

The first version treats people as authors.

The second treats them as scenery.

Do the first one.

## 🧿 What This Node Is Not Claiming  

This node does **not** claim:

```text id="zfuynv"
these are the only chants
these translations are final
all phrases are used in the same way
all uses are football-specific
all clips use standard spelling
all captions are accurate
all platform results are reliable
```

The claim is narrower:

There are enough identifiable phrase fragments to prevent lazy erasure.

If a journalist or researcher can learn European football chants, transfer-market nicknames, military acronyms, Westminster factions, and Silicon Valley product names, they can spend ten minutes trying to hear Congolese supporters properly.

The language is not the problem.

The standard is.
