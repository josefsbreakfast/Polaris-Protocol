# 🛠️ What MPs And Journalists Should Ask  
First created: 2026-06-01 | Last updated: 2026-06-01  
A media-pack checklist for asking better questions about sensitive communications, redactions, WhatsApp, official email, bridge traffic, platform custody, and information markets.

## 🛰️ Orientation

This is the practical checklist node.

The point of Visible Ink is not to make everyone panic.

It is to help MPs, journalists, editors, researchers, policy staff, and civic readers ask better questions.

Not just:

> What did the message say?

But:

> Why was that message in that container?

Not just:

> Who is the villain?

But:

> What system made too many actors plausible?

Not just:

> What is under the redaction?

But:

> Was the material protected properly before it had to be redacted?

The aim is not theatre.

The aim is a fix.

## 📦 The Container Questions

Start with the container.

Before arguing about the contents, ask whether the route made sense.

1. What kind of material was being handled?
2. Was it ordinary admin, political gossip, diary coordination, public-line drafting, sensitive state business, diplomatic handling, procurement material, vetting, policing, or national-security-adjacent material?
3. Was the material sent through ordinary email, WhatsApp, secure portal, controlled document workspace, classified or approved transfer route, or another system?
4. Was email used as the payload or only as a pointer?
5. Was WhatsApp used for quick coordination or substantive decision-making?
6. Was the material attached, forwarded, quoted, summarised, screenshotted, exported, or copied into another channel?
7. Was the route appropriate for the sensitivity of the material?
8. Would the same route be permitted today?
9. What rule now prevents recurrence?

Core test:

> Was this the right container for this material?

## 🧱 Official Email Questions

Do not stop at:

> Was this sent from a government address?

That is too shallow.

Ask:

1. Was government email being treated as a secure container rather than a distribution tool?
2. Which departments, private offices, advisers, legal teams, press teams, embassies, or contractors were copied in?
3. Did the email cross Cabinet Office, FCDO, MOD, MOJ, No. 10, embassy, or other organisational seams?
4. Were attachments sent directly?
5. Were attachments later forwarded, quoted, downloaded, or summarised?
6. Was access limited to the smallest necessary circle?
7. Were delegated mailboxes, shared inboxes, mobile devices, archives, backups, or search indexes relevant?
8. Was there an audit trail showing who accessed the actual material?
9. Could weaker edge contacts reveal timing, travel, meeting windows, or urgency?
10. Was a secure portal or controlled workspace available instead?

Core test:

> A government email address is not a bunker.

## 📱 WhatsApp Questions

Do not treat every WhatsApp as a scandal.

Do not treat WhatsApp as a secure audit trail either.

Ask:

1. Was WhatsApp used for low-risk coordination or for substantive state business?
2. Did the chat carry, summarise, forward, imply, or substitute for sensitive material?
3. Was the same substance captured in an official record?
4. Were messages preserved complete and in context?
5. Did any participant change phones, lose access, delete messages, use disappearing messages, or provide only partial exports?
6. Were screenshots used as evidence instead of a proper record?
7. Did the chat involve bridge-sensitive, procurement-sensitive, policing, vetting, diplomatic, or national-security-adjacent material?
8. Was the platform environment appropriate for the sensitivity?
9. Could the chat reveal relationship warmth, timing, access, pressure, travel, or movement from formal to informal channels?
10. What rule now stops WhatsApp becoming the workplace?

Core test:

> WhatsApp can point to the package. It should not be the package.

## 📡 Redaction Questions

Redaction may be legitimate.

That does not end the scrutiny.

Ask:

1. What category of sensitivity justified the redaction or withholding?
2. Was it national security, international relations, policing, vetting, legal privilege, procurement sensitivity, commercial sensitivity, personal privacy, allied confidence, or operational security?
3. What communication route originally carried the material?
4. Was the sensitive content sent directly, or held in a secure system?
5. Was email used as payload or pointer?
6. Was WhatsApp involved?
7. Did the material cross departments, embassies, contractors, private offices, platforms, or jurisdictions?
8. Who had access then?
9. Who can verify access now?
10. Was onward forwarding controlled?
11. Were attachments downloaded, quoted, summarised, screenshotted, or copied elsewhere?
12. Was the handling reviewed after redaction became necessary?
13. Can a closed oversight body see the handling log?
14. Would the same route be permitted today?

Core test:

> Redaction protects what the public can see. It does not prove the original communication was secure.

## 🌉 Bridge Traffic Questions

If the material sits between London and Washington, or between the UK and another ally, ask bridge questions.

1. Was the communication domestic, allied, or bridge-sensitive?
2. Which UK and US offices, departments, embassies, private offices, advisers, or contractors were involved?
3. Did the route expose alignment, disagreement, timing, anxiety, or allied confidence?
4. Was the material connected to Chagos, Diego Garcia, Ukraine, sanctions, nuclear-policy-adjacent material, defence procurement, strategic industry, or allied handling?
5. Did the route reveal pre-brief or post-brief traffic around travel, meetings, votes, deadlines, announcements, or treaty movement?
6. Did one side’s weak route expose the other side’s position?
7. Did the material cross jurisdictions or platform dependencies?
8. Was access limited to the smallest necessary circle?
9. Was there an audit trail?
10. Could partial knowledge of timing, alignment, disagreement, or uncertainty have commercial or strategic value?
11. What rule protects future bridge traffic?

Core test:

> Do not ask only what the UK knew or what the US knew. Ask what the bridge revealed.

## 👻 Bridge Figure Questions

Highly connected people need network-risk questions.

Ask:

1. Was the person a high-connectivity political, diplomatic, commercial, or media bridge figure?
2. What rooms did they connect?
3. Did they connect ministers, advisers, diplomats, donors, journalists, business contacts, former officials, current officials, party figures, or private offices?
4. Were they treated as a network-risk figure?
5. Were they given security guidance appropriate to their network position?
6. Did they use personal devices or informal channels for state-adjacent business?
7. Did their communications reveal timing, travel, meetings, alignment, disagreement, pressure, warmth, or access?
8. Could they show where the secret travelled without holding the secret themselves?
9. Could a commercial actor benefit from their relationship map?
10. Would the same figure be handled differently today?

Core test:

> Do not ask only whether the bridge figure held the secret. Ask whether the bridge figure showed where the secret travelled.

## 📱 Device Exposure Questions

A political phone is not just a phone.

Ask:

1. Did the device carry official-adjacent, diplomatic, procurement, bridge-sensitive, journalistic, political, commercial, or personal material in one place?
2. Were personal and official channels separated?
3. Were linked devices, cloud backups, message exports, screenshots, or authentication apps relevant?
4. Were WhatsApp, email, private email, cloud services, or app notifications involved?
5. Did the device reveal timing, travel, meetings, pressure windows, or movement between formal and informal channels?
6. Was there any sign of unauthorised access or unusual account activity?
7. Was there a bounded inspection process with privacy safeguards?
8. Was unrelated private, privileged, journalistic, family, or medical material protected?
9. Was relevant state material migrated into the official record?
10. What rule protects high-connectivity figures in future?

Core test:

> Do not ask only what was on the phone. Ask what the phone exposed.

## 🌐 Jurisdiction And Platform Questions

Do not ask only whether the platform is trusted.

Ask whether the state had custody.

1. What platform, cloud, device, or provider environment carried the material?
2. Which jurisdiction or legal regime applied to that environment?
3. Was the material held in a sovereign or approved government custody system?
4. Was email or WhatsApp used as pointer or payload?
5. Were backups, linked devices, metadata, notification previews, or account recovery routes relevant?
6. Was the provider subject to foreign lawful-access processes?
7. Did the route cross allies, contractors, embassies, cloud systems, or private vendors?
8. Was the material later redacted, withheld, or retained for security reasons?
9. If so, was the original platform route reviewed?
10. Could future data-sharing, defence integration, platform policy, or legal changes affect retained material?
11. Could commercial actors benefit from early or partial knowledge?
12. Would the same route be permitted today?
13. What rule prevents sensitive material being placed in the wrong jurisdictional pipe?

Core test:

> Trust is not custody.

## 🛰️ Information Broker Questions

Do not ask only which state wanted it.

Ask who could profit from it.

1. Could the material have commercial value even without being a state secret?
2. Could partial knowledge of timing, alignment, hesitation, or procurement direction create advantage?
3. Were contractors, consultants, lobbyists, donors, advisers, commercial actors, or reputation firms close to the route?
4. Could a broker package the signal as political risk, due diligence, litigation support, reputation intelligence, or market insight?
5. Could a journalist receive material without knowing who shaped the timing?
6. Could a state benefit without collecting directly?
7. Could a commercial actor benefit without committing a clean offence?
8. Was the value in the content, the timing, the relationship map, or the direction of travel?
9. Could the route reveal bridge-sensitive material even where content was redacted?
10. What oversight exists for information brokers, contractors, and commercial-intelligence markets?
11. What rule prevents sensitive state material becoming market intelligence?

Core test:

> Do not only look for the spy. Look for the buyer.

## 💰 Information Arms Race Questions

If the story is being fed by leaks, screenshots, partial files, or timed disclosure, ask market questions.

1. Who benefits from this becoming available now?
2. Who had access?
3. Who shaped the timing?
4. Could the timing serve a broker, faction, commercial actor, donor network, litigation actor, platform route, or foreign interest?
5. Does publication reveal wrongdoing, route failure, process failure, or only embarrassment?
6. Are journalists being asked to report public-interest material or feed a screenshot cycle?
7. Could publishing further detail expose current routes or lower-level staff?
8. Is the press reporting the infrastructure or only the personality scandal?
9. Is this public-interest disclosure, reputation warfare, commercial positioning, foreign-influence activity, legal-pressure strategy, internal party conflict, or several of these at once?
10. What market made the material available?

Core test:

> Journalism cannot only be a downstream customer of the information arms race.

## ⚖️ Intent-Free Risk Questions

Do not wait for proof of malicious intent before fixing weak routes.

Ask:

1. What risk existed regardless of motive?
2. Could the material have value to states, allies, companies, brokers, contractors, journalists, or political actors?
3. Could the same route be risky even if everyone acted lawfully in their own frame?
4. Could a platform, contractor, company, or state say it complied with the law while still creating UK custody concerns?
5. Could a bridge figure reveal the network without meaning to?
6. Could device exposure happen without complicity?
7. Could non-cooperation delay discovery of third-party compromise?
8. Could a commercial actor have profit motive rather than hostile intent?
9. Could attribution delay itself create harm?
10. What rule prevents recurrence even if nobody intended harm?

Core test:

> Intent matters. But intent is not the same as risk.

## 📰 Journalism Questions

For journalists and editors, the practical standard is:

1. What is the public-interest reason for publishing this detail?
2. Does this detail show wrongdoing, process failure, route failure, or merely embarrassment?
3. Are we reporting the message but missing the container?
4. Are we reporting the route without printing the attack surface?
5. Could publication expose current routes, lower-level staff, or live vulnerabilities?
6. Could the timing serve a broker, faction, commercial actor, or foreign interest?
7. Have we separated what is known from what is inferred?
8. Are we asking for handling assurance rather than demanding dangerous disclosure?
9. Are we helping readers understand the system, or just feeding a screenshot cycle?
10. Are we escalating private-message warfare?
11. What is the minimum detail needed to make the public-interest point?

Core test:

> The test is not whether the story has bite. The test is whether the bite lands on the right thing.

## 🏛️ Parliamentary Questions

For MPs and committees, the practical standard is:

1. Was the route appropriate for the sensitivity?
2. Was email used as pointer or payload?
3. Was WhatsApp used for substantive state business?
4. Did material cross departments, embassies, platforms, contractors, or jurisdictions?
5. Did bridge-sensitive topics receive appropriate handling?
6. What assurance exists for redacted material?
7. Was there an audit trail?
8. Were high-connectivity figures treated as network risks?
9. Was device exposure reviewed without public rummaging?
10. Could commercial actors benefit from partial knowledge?
11. Could an ally, platform, contractor, or jurisdiction have access conditions Parliament cannot inspect?
12. Would the same handling be permitted today?
13. What rule prevents recurrence?

Core test:

> The aim is not theatre. The aim is a fix.

## 🧭 Public / Business / Personal Questions

This is not only an MP problem.

The same basic hygiene applies to businesses, charities, universities, law firms, campaign groups, schools, councils, families, and ordinary people.

Ask:

1. Is this the right channel?
2. Who can see it?
3. Who can forward it?
4. Where is it stored?
5. What happens if the phone is lost?
6. What happens if someone leaves the organisation?
7. What happens if a screenshot is taken?
8. What happens if the platform changes its rules?
9. What happens if the material becomes sensitive later?
10. Who could benefit from knowing this earlier than everyone else?
11. Would this hurt someone, expose someone, advantage someone, or become difficult to explain later?
12. Is the easiest channel really the right container?

Core test:

> Most people do not need spy-grade security. They do need basic container discipline.

## 📛 Core Lines

Do not ask only what the message said.

Ask why the message was in that container.

Do not ask only whether the email was official.

Ask whether the route was appropriate.

Do not ask only what was redacted.

Ask what route made redaction necessary.

Do not ask only what the UK knew or what the US knew.

Ask what the bridge revealed.

Do not ask only whether the bridge figure held the secret.

Ask whether the bridge figure showed where the secret travelled.

Do not ask only what was on the phone.

Ask what the phone exposed.

Do not ask only whether the platform was trusted.

Ask whether the state had custody.

Do not only look for the spy.

Look for the buyer.

Do not ask only who leaked this.

Ask who benefits from this being available, and what market made it available.

Intent matters. But intent is not the same as risk.

The test is not whether the story has bite. The test is whether the bite lands on the right thing.

The aim is not theatre. The aim is a fix.

Use the right container.
