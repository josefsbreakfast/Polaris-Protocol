# 🛰️ Info Brokers Sans States  
First created: 2026-06-01 | Last updated: 2026-06-01  
A media-pack node on why political communications risk is not only about hostile states, but about information markets, brokers, contractors, commercial intelligence, and deniable intermediaries.

## 🛰️ Orientation

It is easy to frame political communications risk as a hostile-state problem.

Russia.  
China.  
Iran.  
North Korea.  
A named adversary.  
A familiar villain.

That frame is sometimes right.

It is not enough.

The target is information.

And information does not only move through states.

It can move through commercial-intelligence firms, private investigators, lobbyists, reputation managers, litigation actors, contractors, platform-adjacent insiders, journalists, donor networks, investors, cyber-criminal groups, consultants, political fixers, and brokers selling useful signals onward.

The question is not only:

> Which state wanted this?

The better question is:

> Who could buy, broker, infer, monetise, or time this signal?

Do not only look for the spy.

Look for the buyer.

## 🧿 The Actor Is Not Always The Client

The person who collects information may not be the person who benefits from it.

That is the basic broker problem.

A contractor may collect.

A broker may package.

A consultant may summarise.

A journalist may receive.

A company may buy.

A political actor may use.

A state may benefit.

A donor may pressure.

A litigation actor may time.

A platform may comply with a lawful request.

A government department may mishandle the original route.

These actors do not need to share one motive.

They do not need to know each other.

They do not need to operate inside one command structure.

The harm can be distributed.

So can the responsibility.

## 🏭 Procurement Interest Is Enough

High-sensitivity material does not only attract foreign intelligence services.

It can attract commercial interest.

If correspondence touches defence procurement, nuclear-policy-adjacent material, strategic industry, allied handling, sanctions, Chagos, Ukraine, steel, infrastructure, technology, appointments, or market-sensitive policy, the useful information may not be the full secret.

It may be the direction of travel.

Who is worried.  
Who is favoured.  
Who is delayed.  
Which department is pushing.  
Which ally is hesitant.  
Which contractor may benefit.  
Which legal obstacle is being managed.  
Which procurement route is softening.  
Which public line differs from private expectation.  
Which option is becoming more likely.

That information can matter to companies, investors, suppliers, lobbyists, consultants, litigation actors, commercial-intelligence firms, competitors, and reputation managers.

You do not need a spy story for the route to matter.

Procurement interest is enough.

## 💼 Respectable Language Can Hide The Market

Information brokerage does not always advertise itself as surveillance.

It may be sold as:

- political risk;
- due diligence;
- litigation support;
- reputation protection;
- market intelligence;
- geopolitical advisory;
- stakeholder mapping;
- threat monitoring;
- strategic insight;
- open-source intelligence;
- commercial intelligence;
- compliance intelligence;
- or source reporting.

Some of this work is legitimate.

Some of it is useful.

Some of it is lawful.

Some of it is necessary.

But the same market can also contain aggressive collection, intrusive surveillance, unlawful hacking, deniable middlemen, targeted leaks, pressure campaigns, and material obtained through routes the buyer does not want to inspect too closely.

That is why the grey market is hard to regulate.

It can look respectable at the front desk while something much rougher is happening out the back.

## 🧱 The Grey Market Problem

The grey market is dangerous because it thrives on plausible deniability.

A client may say:

> I only asked for insight.

A consultant may say:

> I only provided analysis.

A broker may say:

> I only received material from a source.

A company may say:

> I only bought political-risk advice.

A state may say:

> We only received reporting.

A journalist may say:

> I only received a leak.

Each statement may contain some truth.

That is the point.

Information can be collected, laundered, repackaged, summarised, resold, and timed until the original route becomes hard to see.

By the time the damage is visible, everyone can point to the person before them in the chain.

## 🕸️ Partial Knowledge Is Still Valuable

A broker does not need the full file.

A contractor does not need the whole policy.

A commercial actor does not need the final decision.

A state does not need every email.

Sometimes partial knowledge is enough.

The useful signal may be:

- timing;
- alignment;
- disagreement;
- hesitation;
- urgency;
- relationship warmth;
- a sudden new department in the chain;
- a trip being prepared;
- a meeting being moved;
- an ally becoming nervous;
- a legal team entering the discussion;
- a procurement route changing shape;
- a redacted topic appearing repeatedly;
- or a private line not matching the public line.

That is enough to create advantage.

It can tell someone when to lobby, leak, litigate, invest, delay, pressure, brief, smear, bid, withdraw, or wait.

The prize is not always the secret.

Sometimes the prize is knowing when the system is moving.

## 🌉 Bridge Traffic Is Market-Relevant

Bridge traffic is especially valuable to information markets.

UK–US material, embassy-to-capital readouts, Chagos, Diego Garcia, Ukraine, sanctions, procurement, nuclear-policy-adjacent material, strategic industry, and allied handling do not only interest foreign states.

They can interest:

- defence contractors;
- technology firms;
- infrastructure companies;
- investors;
- insurers;
- lobbyists;
- litigation actors;
- reputation firms;
- political donors;
- commercial intelligence firms;
- and competitors in allied countries.

The bridge reveals alignment.

The bridge reveals wobble.

The bridge reveals where pressure may work.

That has market value.

A redaction may hide the content from the public.

The timing and route may still reveal enough for someone else to profit.

## 👻 Bridge Figures As Market Sensors

Highly connected people can become valuable market sensors.

They do not need to act maliciously.

They may simply know many people.

They may keep lines warm.

They may hear anxiety early.

They may know when a policy is moving.

They may know which department is worried.

They may know which ally is hesitating.

They may know who has access.

They may know when a public line is not quite the private expectation.

That is why bridge figures are useful politically.

It is also why they can become useful commercially.

A person who can hold a network together can reveal the network’s shape.

Sociability is infrastructure.

## 📱 Device And Platform Layers

Information brokers do not always need direct access to the main government system.

They may exploit weaker edges.

A personal phone.  
A WhatsApp group.  
A cloud backup.  
A linked device.  
A private email.  
A consultant’s inbox.  
A journalist’s tip line.  
A lobbyist’s phone.  
A donor network.  
A contractor’s shared drive.  
A meeting calendar.  
A platform notification.  
A legal disclosure process.  
A partial screenshot.

Each fragment may look small.

Together, they can form a map.

That map can then be sold as insight.

This is why “government email is secure” is not the end of the story.

The information market does not need to enter only through the front door.

## 🧨 Opportunistic Collection

Not every collector begins by looking for the exact material they later sell.

Some actors look for anything useful.

They probe systems.

They scrape public records.

They monitor accounts.

They buy datasets.

They watch travel.

They follow litigation.

They collect leaks.

They gather screenshots.

They notice exposed files.

They test weak devices.

They pick up fragments.

Then they work out who might pay.

Sometimes the market begins after the collection, not before it.

That makes loose communications even more dangerous.

If sensitive material travels through ordinary routes, it may be picked up by someone who was not originally targeting that exact file.

They find it because it was there.

Then the information moves.

## ⚖️ Legal Harm Before Clear Crime

This is why the law often struggles.

The harm may be real before the offence is obvious.

A person may lawfully gather public data.

A company may lawfully buy due-diligence intelligence.

A consultant may lawfully speak to contacts.

A journalist may lawfully receive a leak.

A platform may lawfully respond to a request.

A researcher may lawfully map public information.

A lobbyist may lawfully ask what is happening.

But the combined effect may still be coercive, destabilising, commercially unfair, or strategically damaging.

The law often looks for a clean offence.

The threat may look like a market.

## 📰 Why Journalism Should Care

Journalists should care because the press can become part of the information market without intending to.

That does not mean journalists should stop reporting.

It means they should ask harder questions about source pathways, timing, incentive, and downstream effects.

A leak can be public-interest journalism.

A leak can also be reputation warfare.

A leak can be commercial positioning.

A leak can be foreign-influence activity.

A leak can be legal-pressure strategy.

A leak can be Westminster gossip.

A leak can be several of these at once.

The better question is not only:

> Is this interesting?

It is also:

> Who benefits from this becoming available now, and what market made it available?

Journalism cannot only be a downstream customer of the information arms race.

## 🧿 Not Everything Is Sinister

This node is not an argument for paranoia.

Not every consultant is dirty.

Not every journalist is a pawn.

Not every investor is corrupt.

Not every lobbyist is operating for a foreign power.

Not every private intelligence firm is unlawful.

Not every leak is hostile.

Not every commercial actor is doing something wrong.

The point is threat discrimination.

Some information movement is normal.

Some information movement is public-interest disclosure.

Some information movement is legitimate research.

Some information movement is lawful intelligence work.

Some information movement is commercial analysis.

Some information movement is predatory, abusive, deniable, or destabilising.

The system needs enough literacy to tell the difference.

Do not panic at every buzzy thing.

Do learn what is buzzing.

## 🛠️ What MPs Should Ask

The practical questions are straightforward:

1. Could the material have commercial value even without being a state secret?
2. Could partial knowledge of timing, alignment, hesitation, or procurement direction create advantage?
3. Were contractors, consultants, lobbyists, donors, advisers, or commercial actors close to the route?
4. Was the material handled through email, WhatsApp, personal devices, cloud systems, or secure portals?
5. Was access limited to the smallest necessary circle?
6. Were attachments, screenshots, summaries, or informal readouts created?
7. Could the route reveal bridge-sensitive material even where content was redacted?
8. Could a commercial-intelligence actor package the signal as political risk or due diligence?
9. Could a journalist receive material without knowing who shaped the timing?
10. Could a state benefit without collecting directly?
11. What oversight exists for information brokers, contractors, and commercial-intelligence markets?
12. What rule prevents sensitive state material becoming market intelligence?

Do not ask only which state wanted it.

Ask who could profit from it.
