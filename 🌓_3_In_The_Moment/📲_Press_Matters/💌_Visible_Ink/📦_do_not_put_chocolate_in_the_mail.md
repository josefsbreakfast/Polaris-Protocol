# 📦 Do Not Put Chocolate In The Mail  
First created: 2026-06-01 | Last updated: 2026-06-01  
A plain-language media-pack node on why sensitive state material needs the right container, not just a convenient channel.

## 🛰️ Orientation

The issue is not that government communicates.

Government has to communicate.

Officials brief ministers.  
Departments coordinate.  
Embassies report back.  
Journalists ask questions.  
Parliament scrutinises.  
Lawyers test risk.  
Diplomats prepare lines.  
Political actors keep networks warm.

The issue is not movement.

The issue is handling.

Some material can travel through ordinary routes.

Some material needs specialist handling.

The problem opened by the Mandelson files is not simply that emails and WhatsApps exist. It is that some material appears to have travelled through ordinary working channels when the sensitivity of the subject should have triggered a better container.

The question is not:

> Can this be sent?

The question is:

> Was this the right way to send it?

## 🍫 Chocolate In August

The simplest analogy is chocolate in the mail.

Chocolate is not dangerous.

Chocolate is not forbidden.

Chocolate can absolutely be sent to someone.

But if someone puts a bar of chocolate in ordinary post during a heatwave, with no thought about packaging, temperature, timing, or delivery conditions, they should not act shocked when it arrives as expensive brown soup.

That is not the postman’s fault.

That is a handling failure.

Government communication has the same problem.

An email system can carry a message.

That does not mean email was the right container for that message.

WhatsApp can carry words.

That does not mean WhatsApp was the right container for those words.

Some information melts if you send it through the wrong route.

## 📮 Ordinary Post Is Fine For Ordinary Things

Ordinary delivery is fine for ordinary things.

Birthday cards.  
Books.  
Forms.  
Routine letters.  
Low-risk documents.  
Public notices.  
Mildly boring parcels that do not leak, melt, break, explode, rot, or create a scene in the sorting office.

Email is the same.

A lot of email is fine.

Meeting moved to 3pm.  
Please confirm attendance.  
Here is the public line.  
Thanks for your note.  
Can someone check the diary?  
Happy birthday.  
Please see the published document.

Fine.

That is normal office traffic.

The problem is pretending that because email is fine for ordinary things, it must be fine for everything.

It is not.

A diary note is not a nuclear-policy briefing.

A birthday message is not a vetting concern.

A political nudge is not a police-sensitive document.

A public line is not a strategic-industry signal.

A routine admin message is not the same as diplomatic material that still needs national-security redactions one or two years later.

## 📦 Package For The Risk

Everyone understands this in ordinary life.

Fragile things need padding.

Valuable things need tracking.

Hazardous things need specialist handling.

Meltable things need temperature awareness.

Confidential things need privacy.

Dangerous things may not belong in ordinary delivery at all.

This is not complicated.

It is basic packaging.

Government information needs the same common sense.

Some material can go by ordinary email.

Some material should only be referenced by email, with the actual document sitting in a secure portal.

Some material needs controlled access, audit logs, limited onward sharing, identity checks, version control, expiry rules, and a clear record of who opened what and when.

The question is not:

> Can email carry this?

Of course email can carry it.

The question is:

> Was email the right packaging for this material?

## 📩 Email Is Ordinary Delivery

Email is useful because it is easy.

That is also why it is risky.

It is fast, familiar, searchable, forwardable, and available across offices, departments, devices, embassies, private offices, legal teams, press teams, advisers, and officials.

That convenience creates exposure.

Email can create:

- sender copies;
- recipient copies;
- forwarded copies;
- quoted chains;
- attachments;
- mailbox archives;
- search indexes;
- mobile-device traces;
- delegated access;
- backups;
- metadata;
- disclosure problems;
- and uncertainty about who has seen, saved, copied, forwarded, or retained the material.

For ordinary messages, that may be acceptable.

For high-risk state material, it may not be.

A government email address is not specialist packaging.

A sensitive marking is not specialist packaging.

A redaction later is not specialist packaging.

## 📱 WhatsApp Is Not A Diplomatic Courier

WhatsApp has its place.

It can be useful for quick contact:

> Are you free?  
> Please call me.  
> Meeting moved.  
> Can you check the secure system?

That does not make it a proper container for sensitive state business.

If the content touches national security, police investigations, vetting, diplomatic handling, strategic industry, sanctions, Chagos, Ukraine, nuclear policy, allied communications, or high-risk appointments, then the proper question is:

> Why was this not handled through a secure route?

WhatsApp can point to the package.

It should not be the package.

## 🧨 Redaction Is Not Packaging

Redaction may be necessary.

Some things should not be public.

Some material may properly be withheld because of national security, international relations, policing, vetting, legal privilege, commercial sensitivity, or personal privacy.

But redaction happens after the material has already travelled.

It protects the public copy.

It does not prove the original route was safe.

Blacking out the label on a damaged parcel does not mean the parcel was packed properly.

If a message is still too sensitive to publish one or two years later, MPs and journalists are entitled to ask why it travelled through that route in the first place.

The question is not only:

> Did the Government redact the dangerous parts before publication?

The better question is:

> Why was dangerous material placed in that container at all?

## 🛠️ Better Containers Exist

This is not a demand for magical security.

Better containers already exist.

Secure portals.  
Controlled document workspaces.  
Classified or approved transfer routes.  
Access logs.  
Identity checks.  
Document-level permissions.  
Download controls.  
Version control.  
Expiry controls.  
Minimal access circles.  
Separated passwords.  
Clear custody rules.

Most large organisations understand some version of this.

Banks use secure portals.  
Law firms use secure data rooms.  
Hospitals use controlled record systems.  
Courts use restricted filing systems.  
Schools handle safeguarding records differently from lunch-menu emails.

Government should not be less disciplined than people sending mortgage paperwork, medical records, or exam scripts.

The standard is not perfect safety.

The standard is using the right container for the risk.

## 🧿 The Systems Point

This is not mainly a blame node.

It is a design node.

The visible problem is not only that one person may have made one bad judgement.

The wider problem is that the working culture appears to have treated ordinary channels as normal containers for material that may have needed specialist handling.

That is how systems fail.

Not always through one dramatic breach.

Sometimes through ordinary people doing ordinary office things with material that was not ordinary.

Someone attaches the briefing note.

Someone forwards the chain.

Someone adds another department.

Someone summarises the sensitive bit on WhatsApp.

Someone changes phone.

Someone cannot retrieve the old message.

Someone redacts the public copy later.

Everyone says they were following normal practice.

That may even be true.

That is the problem.

## 📛 Core Lines

Do not put chocolate in the mail in August and act shocked when it melts.

Do not put eggs in an envelope and blame the postman.

Do not send fragile, hazardous, or high-value material without specialist handling.

Email is ordinary delivery.

WhatsApp is not a diplomatic courier.

Redaction is not packaging.

A government email address is not specialist handling.

The question is not whether email can carry it.

The question is whether email was the right packaging for this material.

Use the right container.
