## 🛰️ Orientation

This node records the known public facts around the dispute between Palantir, the Metropolitan Police Service, and the Mayor’s Office for Policing and Crime.

It is deliberately narrower than the rest of the cluster.

This is the evidential floor.

No mascot.

No hidden answer.

No narrative leap dressed as a timetable.

The purpose is to separate:

- what has been publicly reported;
- what has been publicly claimed by named actors;
- what appears in public documents or official records;
- what remains disputed;
- and what should not yet be treated as fact.

Because this is an active legal and procurement issue, this node should be updated cautiously.

If a claim cannot be sourced, dated, and attributed, it does not belong here as fact.

---

## 🧭 Evidence Handling Rule

This node uses five working labels.

### Sourceable Fact

A claim supported by a public source, document, official statement, procurement record, court record, parliamentary or assembly record, or named reporting.

### Public Claim

A claim made publicly by an actor in the dispute, but not necessarily accepted as true by the other actors.

### Document Trail

A claim attached to a public report, agenda paper, mayoral question, procurement document, published correspondence, or legal filing.

### Disputed / Contested

A claim where public actors disagree about the facts, the legal significance, the process, or the framing.

### Open Question

A question that may be answerable through reporting, FOI, assembly questioning, legal disclosure, audit, procurement review, or later court material.

## 📌 Current Public Timeline

### 2025–2026: Earlier Palantir Use By The Met

The Metropolitan Police had already appointed Palantir on a smaller contract before the larger disputed contract.

Public reporting describes this earlier contract as worth less than £500,000.

The reported purpose was to use AI to help identify corruption, criminality, or concerning staff behaviour inside the force.

This matters because the later disputed contract was not the first contact point between the Met and Palantir.

It was an attempted expansion.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting.  
**Source anchor:** Guardian reporting, 21–22 May 2026.

---

### April–May 2026: Public Reporting On Proposed Expansion

Public reporting described the Met as seeking a larger Palantir contract connected to AI-supported intelligence analysis and criminal investigations.

The proposed system was reported as Unified Operational Analytics, or UOA.

Reported functions included automating aspects of intelligence analysis, supporting criminal investigations, and processing or analysing large volumes of policing data.

Public reporting also connected the proposed capability to the Met’s wider attempt to manage budget and staffing pressures through technology-enabled efficiency.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting and later board-report references.  
**Source anchor:** Guardian reporting, Local Government Lawyer reporting, London Policing Board papers as reported.

---

### 20–21 May 2026: MOPAC Refuses Approval

The Mayor’s Office for Policing and Crime refused approval for the proposed Palantir contract.

Public reporting gives the refusal date as 20 or 21 May 2026, depending on whether referring to the decision, letter, or public announcement.

The Deputy Mayor for Policing and Crime, Kaya Comer-Schwartz, was reported as writing to the Met Commissioner to inform him of the refusal.

MOPAC’s stated concern was not that the Met could never use advanced technology.

The stated concern was that the procurement process had not adequately ensured or demonstrated value for money.

MOPAC also raised concerns that the Met had only engaged seriously with one supplier: Palantir.

Public reporting also states that the Met did not present its procurement strategy to the Deputy Mayor for approval as required.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting; official/public-record references should be checked directly where accessible.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 22 May 2026; Guardian, 21 May 2026; London Assembly / Mayor’s Question records.

---

### Contract Value

The proposed contract was publicly reported as worth approximately £50 million.

More specifically, reporting gives the value as:

- £25.3 million in 2026/27;
- with an optional one-year extension of £24.8 million in 2027/28.

That gives the dispute its rough public shorthand: the blocked or contested £50 million Met-Palantir deal.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 22 May 2026 and 12 June 2026.

---

### Met Response

The Metropolitan Police publicly described MOPAC’s decision as disappointing.

The Met’s public position was that it needed to modernise and use advanced technology to respond to organised criminals, hostile states, budget pressures, and operational demand.

The Met said procurement had been conducted using a Crown Commercial Service framework.

The Met also linked the blocked contract to its wider financial position, including a reported £125 million funding shortfall and potential workforce reductions.

Public reporting records Met warnings that without new technology delivered at pace, it may have to make further tough choices affecting officer numbers and service delivery.

**Evidence status:** public claim by the Met, reported in public sources.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 22 May 2026; Guardian, 21 May 2026; London Policing Board reporting.

---

### Palantir Response

Palantir publicly criticised the decision.

Louis Mosley, Palantir’s UK and Europe head, accused the Mayor of putting politics above public safety.

Public reporting records Palantir arguing that its software had already helped other police forces and public bodies.

Palantir also questioned why it was being singled out when other major technology companies also work with controversial state or defence clients.

**Evidence status:** public claim by Palantir, reported in public sources.  
**Source anchor:** Guardian, 22 May 2026.

---

### MOPAC / City Hall Position

City Hall’s publicly reported position was that the issue was procurement discipline, value for money, and proper approval.

The Mayor’s office said robust processes are especially important for large public contracts under tight budgetary constraints.

Public reporting also records City Hall concerns about public money going to companies whose work may be seen as contrary to London’s values.

That values point sits alongside, but should not be collapsed into, the procurement-process point.

They are related public arguments, not automatically the same legal issue.

**Evidence status:** public claim by City Hall / MOPAC, reported in public sources.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 22 May 2026; Guardian, 21–22 May 2026.

---

### Future Bidding

Public reporting stated that there did not appear to be a block on Palantir bidding for a future contract.

MOPAC was reported as wanting to work with the Met on a new procurement plan.

That matters because the public record does not support the simple claim that Palantir was permanently banned from Met procurement.

The narrower public claim is that this proposed contract was refused approval.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting.  
**Source anchor:** Guardian, 21 May 2026.

---

### June 2026: Legal Escalation

In June 2026, Palantir was reported to have sent MOPAC a pre-action letter.

The letter was reported as signalling a legal challenge to the refusal of the proposed contract.

Public reporting framed this as Palantir moving toward litigation against the Mayor’s Office for Policing and Crime over the blocked Met AI contract.

At this stage, the public fact is the reported legal escalation.

The strength of the legal case is not yet a public fact.

**Evidence status:** sourceable public reporting of legal escalation.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 12 June 2026; Times reporting as referenced by other outlets.

---

### 11 June 2026: London Policing Board Context

Public reporting on the London Policing Board meeting of 11 June 2026 stated that the Met Commissioner linked the collapse or delay of the UOA programme to the Met’s 2026/27 budget and operational targets.

The reported position was that UOA had been expected to help offset staffing reductions through efficiency gains.

Reporting stated that the Met had expected UOA to cover the equivalent of 500 posts within a wider 1,150-post reduction plan.

The Commissioner was also reported as saying the force would need to review and reset operational targets downward.

**Evidence status:** reported public-board/document trail; official papers should be preserved where accessible.  
**Source anchor:** Local Government Lawyer, 12 June 2026; London Policing Board papers as reported; Financial Times reporting.

## 🧾 Actor Positions In The Public Record

### Palantir

Palantir’s public position appears to include:

- the decision was politically influenced or politicised;
- public safety may be harmed by blocking the contract;
- its software has operational value for policing;
- it has relevant experience with UK public bodies and police forces;
- it should not be singled out compared with other major technology suppliers;
- and the refusal may be legally challengeable.

**Status:** public claim, not established fact.

---

### Metropolitan Police

The Met’s public position appears to include:

- the force needs advanced technology to maintain service under budget pressure;
- the procurement was conducted through a Crown Commercial Service framework;
- the proposed capability was important to efficiency and operational performance;
- the refusal creates budget, workforce, and service-delivery consequences;
- and the Met wants the issue resolved swiftly.

**Status:** public claim, not established fact.

---

### MOPAC / City Hall

MOPAC and City Hall’s public position appears to include:

- the process did not adequately ensure or demonstrate value for money;
- the Met had only engaged seriously with one potential supplier;
- the procurement strategy was not presented for required approval;
- correct processes must be followed before large contracts are awarded;
- public trust and democratic oversight matter in policing technology procurement;
- and the decision was not, on their account, a rejection of modern policing technology as such.

**Status:** public claim, not established fact.

## ⚖️ Points Currently In Dispute

The public record suggests several live disputes.

### Procurement Adequacy

Was the process sufficiently competitive, fair, and value-for-money compliant?

### Approval Route

Was the procurement strategy presented to MOPAC in the required way and at the required time?

### Framework Use

Was reliance on a Crown Commercial Service framework enough to satisfy the relevant procurement and oversight requirements?

### Public Safety Consequences

Would blocking the contract meaningfully harm public safety, officer numbers, or operational capability?

### Political Motivation

Was the refusal driven by procurement concerns, values concerns, political pressure, or some mixture of these?

### Vendor Lock-In

Did the earlier smaller contract create a pathway toward supplier dependency or a single-supplier expansion?

### Future Procurement

Can the Met rerun or restructure the procurement in a way that allows Palantir and other suppliers to compete?

### Legal Strength

Does Palantir have a strong legal case, or is the pre-action letter primarily a pressure move?

This node does not answer those questions.

It records that they are now the questions.

## 🚩 Claims Not Yet Treated As Facts

This node does not currently treat the following as established facts:

- that MOPAC acted unlawfully;
- that the Met acted unlawfully;
- that Palantir’s legal case is strong;
- that Palantir’s legal case is weak;
- that the refusal was only about Palestine, Israel, or US politics;
- that the refusal was only about procurement;
- that the Met could not have found another supplier;
- that UOA would definitely have saved the stated number of posts;
- that public safety would definitely have been harmed without the contract;
- that the earlier smaller contract was deliberately structured to avoid scrutiny;
- that Palantir was permanently barred from future Met contracts;
- or that any actor’s public framing gives the whole story.

Those may be questions for reporting.

They are not the evidential floor.

## 🧭 Immediate Reporting Questions Raised By The Public Record

The public record now supports asking:

- What procurement route did the Met use?
- Which Crown Commercial Service framework was relied on?
- What suppliers were available through that route?
- Was Palantir the only supplier considered, or the only supplier fully engaged?
- Who approved the procurement strategy inside the Met?
- When was MOPAC first informed of the procurement strategy?
- What exactly did MOPAC require before approval?
- What records exist of disagreement between the Met and MOPAC before refusal?
- What operational benefits did the Met claim UOA would provide?
- What evidence supports the claim that UOA would offset 500 posts?
- What risks were identified in DPIA, equality, ethics, procurement, or operational-governance processes?
- What role did the earlier sub-£500,000 contract play in shaping the later proposed contract?
- What data categories would UOA process?
- Would the system process personal data, sensitive personal data, criminal-offence data, or officer/staff data?
- What human review, audit, appeal, and error-correction mechanisms were proposed?
- What parts of the record are being withheld because of national security, policing capability, commercial confidentiality, or legal privilege?
- What exactly is Palantir asking MOPAC to do now?

These are questions the public record already justifies.

They are not allegations.

That is the point.
