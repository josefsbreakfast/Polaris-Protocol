README.md

Orientation for the whole cluster.

This is not a pro-Palantir argument.

It is not a simple anti-Palantir argument either.

The stranger possibility is that a controversial private contractor, acting from commercial self-interest, may force disclosure around public-sector governance failures that ordinary citizens, harmed complainants, wrongly arrested people, women failed by stalking systems, bereaved families, and disabled citizens often cannot afford to expose.

The tulpa moves for tulpa reasons.

The public may still get light through the crack.

Core line:

The point of this cluster is not to replace reporting. The point is to make reporting harder to avoid.

⸻

📌 Known Public Facts First

Strict sourceable timeline only.

Hold here:

* Met pilot / proposed contract;
* approximate contract value;
* MOPAC veto;
* Palantir legal escalation / pre-action posture;
* stated reasons by MOPAC;
* stated public-safety / officer-number claims by the Met or Palantir;
* DPIA / ethics / procurement references;
* Louis Mosley public statements;
* exact dates of reporting;
* exact wording differences between “Palantir” and “Palantir UK.”

Rule:

Facts first. Interpretation second. Vibes in a separate room with tea.

⸻

🧭 How To Read This Without Picking A Mascot

Nobody needs to be the hero.

Palantir can be self-interested and still raise useful questions.

MOPAC can be right and still have awkward paperwork.

The Met can have operational need and weak governance.

A contractor can be commercially aggressive and still expose public-sector fragility.

A public authority can be democratically responsible and still use process language defensively.

Core line:

The public does not need a mascot. The public needs the paperwork.

⸻

🧭 Journalists You Ask

Press Matters doctrine.

Polaris does not claim the hidden answer.

It routes questions.

Use this node to set the standard:

* ask the public bodies;
* ask the company;
* ask the mayoral office;
* ask the lawyers;
* ask procurement specialists;
* ask data-protection specialists;
* ask policing-accountability specialists;
* ask local-government lawyers;
* ask who benefits if the story is kept narrow.

Core line:

We are not claiming the hidden answer. We are showing where reporting should dig.

⸻

🕰️ Why Now? The Timing Question

Why escalate now?

Timing as signal, not proof.

This node should hold the “why this moment?” analysis:

* Was escalation prompted by the veto itself?
* Was there a legal deadline?
* Was there a reputational deadline?
* Was there investor pressure?
* Was there a wider exposure timetable?
* Was there a Mandelson / Global Counsel / lobbying / disclosure atmosphere nearby?
* Was this about one contract, or about pre-framing the company before other accountability windows opened?

Core line:

Timing is not proof. Timing is where the question starts.

⸻

Core line:

The public-interest question is what falls out of their pockets while they are fighting.

If the public record is incomplete because material sits behind locked doors, the litigation posture gets more dangerous.

Core line:

The problem with demanding the folder is that someone may eventually mention the locked cabinet.

⸻

🕳️ Why This Is Not Just About Palantir

Wider synthesis.

This is about:

* outsourcing;
* policing tech;
* public-sector procurement;
* vendor dependency;
* local democratic oversight;
* FOI literacy;
* legal pressure;
* citizen journalism;
* public bodies defending process;
* private contractors forcing accountability;
* state/vendor boundary collapse.

Core line:

The Palantir dispute is a doorway, not the whole building.

⸻

🗞️ Why Mainstream Press Keeps Missing The Shape

News-shape problem.

The press likes:

* named company;
* named mayor;
* £50m contract;
* police cuts line;
* lawsuit threat.

It struggles with:

* procurement weather;
* legal-risk ecology;
* disclosure sequencing;
* foreign influence;
* hidden layers;
* citizen audit trails;
* public-sector defensiveness;
* “paperwork about the paperwork.”

Core line:

The press reports the event. The archive tracks the shape.
