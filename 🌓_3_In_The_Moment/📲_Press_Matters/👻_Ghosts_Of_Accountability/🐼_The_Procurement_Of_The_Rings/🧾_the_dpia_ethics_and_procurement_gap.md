# 🧾 The DPIA, Ethics, And Procurement Gap  
**First created:** 2026-06-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-06-23  
*A useful tool is not the same as a lawfully adopted tool; the gap is where governance either lives or quietly pretends it was invited.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

This node examines the gap between tool usefulness and lawful adoption.

The Met may have had a real operational need.

Palantir may have had a useful system.

MOPAC may have had legitimate oversight concerns.

None of those points answers the full governance question.

A policing-technology system can be useful and still be weakly procured.

It can be effective and still be insufficiently governed.

It can identify real risk and still create new risks for workers, suspects, victims, witnesses, communities, and the wider public.

That is why the DPIA, ethics, and procurement layers matter.

They are not decorative compliance confetti.

They are the difference between:

This tool can do something.

And:

This tool may lawfully, proportionately, accountably, and safely be embedded inside coercive public infrastructure.

Those are not the same sentence.

They should not be allowed to elope.

---

## 🧭 The Core Distinction

This cluster needs to keep one distinction clean.

### Tool Usefulness

A tool may be useful if it:

- speeds analysis;
- connects fragmented data;
- supports investigations;
- helps identify patterns;
- improves productivity;
- supports internal standards work;
- flags corruption or misconduct risk;
- or helps officers and staff manage operational demand.

### Lawful Adoption

A tool is lawfully adopted only if the public body can show:

- lawful basis;
- necessity;
- proportionality;
- procurement compliance;
- data-protection compliance;
- information-governance controls;
- ethical review;
- equality and bias assessment;
- staff and public transparency where required;
- auditability;
- human review;
- challenge and correction routes;
- retention rules;
- access controls;
- supplier accountability;
- and democratic oversight.

The mistake is treating usefulness as if it automatically answers adoption.

It does not.

A scalpel is useful.

That does not mean anyone may perform surgery in a car park because the queue is long.

## 🧾 What A DPIA Is Doing Here

A Data Protection Impact Assessment is not a magic shield.

It is also not a bureaucratic garnish.

In this context, a DPIA should help identify and manage risks created by processing personal data, especially where the processing may be high risk.

For a policing analytics system, the DPIA questions may include:

- what personal data is processed;
- what criminal-offence data is processed;
- what special-category data is processed;
- what staff data is processed;
- what inferred or derived data is created;
- what datasets are linked;
- what purposes are claimed;
- what lawful basis is relied on;
- what necessity case is made;
- what proportionality case is made;
- what risks to individuals are identified;
- what mitigations are proposed;
- what residual risks remain;
- who signs off those risks;
- and whether the system should proceed at all.

A DPIA that merely describes the tool after the procurement path is already politically committed is not enough.

The DPIA should be part of the decision.

Not a napkin tucked under the wobbly table after dinner.

---

## ⚖️ DPIA Before Or After The Commitment?

The timing of the DPIA matters.

A late DPIA can become a compliance theatre prop.

The governance question is:

Was the DPIA used to decide whether and how the system should be procured, piloted, expanded, or refused?

Or was it used to tidy up a direction already chosen?

For this cluster, the important questions are:

- when was the DPIA started?
- when was it completed?
- who authored it?
- who reviewed it?
- was the DPO involved?
- was it updated between pilot and larger contract?
- did it cover the earlier pilot?
- did it cover the proposed larger UOA system?
- did it cover staff data?
- did it cover criminal-offence data?
- did it cover data linkage?
- did it cover inferred risk scoring or pattern detection?
- did it identify high residual risk?
- did any unresolved risk require escalation;
- and did MOPAC see it before the refusal?

If the DPIA happened after the relationship had already become operationally or politically committed, that matters.

Not because late paperwork proves illegality.

Because late paperwork often shows where governance stopped being steering and became upholstery.

## 🛠️ Staff Data And Worker Transparency

The staff-data issue deserves its own gate.

If earlier Palantir work or the proposed system involved internal standards, corruption detection, misconduct, criminality, concerning behaviour, vetting, complaints, deployment, or staff-risk analysis, then worker-facing governance becomes central.

The questions are not soft.

They are structural.

What were staff told?

Were staff given privacy information?

Were unions or staff associations briefed?

What categories of staff data were processed?

Were conduct records, complaints, vetting data, disciplinary histories, intelligence markers, sickness records, protected-characteristic data, or inferred risk indicators included?

Could outputs affect:

- vetting;
- discipline;
- deployment;
- promotion;
- internal reputation;
- professional trust;
- misconduct investigation;
- management attention;
- or future career prospects?

Were there human-review requirements?

Were there audit logs?

Could workers challenge or correct outputs?

Who checked false positives?

Who checked bias?

Who checked whether staff became evidence for a future procurement case without meaningful notice?

This is not a defence of bad officers.

It is a defence of lawful worker governance.

A system designed to find internal harm can still harm workers if it is built badly.

The broom does not become ethical because the room is dirty.

## 🧪 Pilot Governance

A pilot should not be treated as governance-free because it is smaller.

A smaller contract can still process sensitive data.

A limited deployment can still shape future dependency.

A pilot can still affect workers, communities, investigations, or internal decision-making.

The pilot questions are:

- what was the pilot’s purpose?
- what data did it use?
- what decisions did it support?
- who had access?
- who was affected?
- what outputs were produced?
- were outputs used operationally?
- were outputs used for staff management or standards work?
- what was the evaluation method?
- were results independently reviewed?
- was the DPIA limited to the pilot or future expansion?
- was the pilot used as evidence for the larger contract?
- and did pilot use create continuity pressure that made Palantir appear necessary?

A pilot is not automatically harmless because it is below a financial threshold.

The data does not know how much the invoice was.

## 🧿 Ethics Review

Ethics review is not the same as data protection.

A system can satisfy a narrow data-protection checklist and still create ethical problems.

For policing analytics, ethical questions may include:

- should this kind of tool be used at all?
- what problem is it solving?
- who benefits?
- who carries the risk?
- who is most likely to be misclassified?
- what happens when the system is wrong?
- who can challenge it?
- how are vulnerable people affected?
- how are communities affected?
- how are staff affected?
- does it amplify historic bias?
- does it create dependency on a private supplier?
- does it make coercive power less visible?
- does it make oversight harder because the system is technically complex?
- and does it shift accountability from decision-makers to software?

Ethics is where the system has to answer:

Even if we can do this, should we?

And if yes:

Under what limits?

The answer cannot be “the dashboard is shiny.”

That is how institutions end up worshipping a spreadsheet with mood lighting.

## 🏛️ Procurement Governance

Procurement is not a separate universe from ethics and data protection.

For sensitive policing technology, procurement should ask governance questions too.

Not just:

Is this supplier available?

But:

- is this procurement route appropriate?
- were alternatives tested?
- was market engagement adequate?
- was value for money evidenced?
- were exit costs considered?
- was vendor lock-in assessed?
- were implementation costs included?
- were governance and audit costs included?
- were data-protection and ethics requirements built into the tender?
- were staff and public transparency obligations costed?
- were model, data, and audit requirements specified?
- were contractual remedies available if safeguards failed?
- and did the contract allow meaningful oversight of the supplier?

A procurement process that treats governance as an afterthought is weak even if the tool works.

A tool that works by creating dependency is not necessarily good value.

A cheap-looking contract can become expensive once the public body cannot leave.

The ring may be priced attractively.

The curse is often in the maintenance schedule.

---

## 🔮 Supplier Accountability

If Palantir’s system was useful, the supplier-accountability questions become sharper.

A useful supplier can become embedded.

An embedded supplier can become hard to replace.

A hard-to-replace supplier can reshape public-sector choices.

That means the contract should answer:

- what data can the supplier access?
- what data can the supplier retain?
- what outputs can the supplier see?
- what audit rights does the public body have?
- what subcontractors or affiliates are involved?
- where is data hosted?
- what happens on exit?
- can the public body port data, logic, configurations, and audit records?
- can the supplier use learning from the deployment elsewhere?
- are there restrictions on secondary use?
- who controls system configuration?
- who controls updates?
- who can explain outputs?
- who is liable when the system fails?
- and who is accountable when human decision-makers rely on it too heavily?

Supplier accountability is not anti-supplier.

It is pro-public power knowing where its own nervous system has gone.

The crystal ball may be clever.

It should still have a plug someone else can reach.

## 🐼 Policing Context

Policing data is not ordinary administrative data.

It may involve:

- suspects;
- victims;
- witnesses;
- children;
- vulnerable adults;
- protesters;
- staff;
- officers;
- complainants;
- intelligence subjects;
- people never charged;
- people wrongly flagged;
- people linked by association;
- and communities already subject to disproportionate attention.

That makes the governance threshold higher.

The Met’s operational needs may be real.

The sensitivity of the data makes weak governance less excusable, not more.

A system entering policing does not become acceptable because policing is hard.

Policing is hard.

That is why the safeguards matter.

The panda car does not get to remove the brakes because the road is difficult.

## 🏛️ MOPAC’s Governance Gate

MOPAC’s role should not be reduced to political approval or refusal.

Its oversight function should ask whether the governance package was good enough.

That package may include:

- procurement route;
- value-for-money assessment;
- operational-need assessment;
- DPIA;
- equality assessment;
- ethics review;
- staff-data safeguards;
- public-trust assessment;
- supplier accountability terms;
- audit rights;
- exit plan;
- implementation plan;
- risk register;
- and post-deployment review arrangements.

If MOPAC refused approval because the package was inadequate, it should be able to show which parts failed.

If MOPAC refused because Palantir was controversial but the governance package was otherwise adequate, that is a different case.

If MOPAC did not have the full package before refusing, that is also important.

Oversight with teeth needs a jawbone.

Preferably not one borrowed from a press release.

## 💍 The Contract Gap

The contract is where these layers should meet.

A good contract should not simply buy the software.

It should encode governance.

That means it should address:

- permitted purposes;
- prohibited uses;
- data categories;
- staff-data handling;
- access controls;
- audit logs;
- accuracy monitoring;
- bias monitoring;
- human review;
- challenge and correction;
- deletion and retention;
- supplier access;
- subcontracting;
- security;
- breach handling;
- reporting obligations;
- oversight access;
- exit rights;
- knowledge transfer;
- and consequences for misuse or non-compliance.

If these controls are absent, vague, or left to later operational practice, then the procurement gap becomes real.

The public body has not just bought a tool.

It has bought an argument for later.

Those arguments are usually more expensive than they look.

## 🪞 Trust Is Not A Vibe

Public trust is often invoked lazily.

This node treats trust as a governance output, not a mood.

Public trust depends on whether the system can be explained, justified, challenged, corrected, audited, limited, and stopped.

That means trust requires:

- clear purpose;
- public explanation where possible;
- lawful basis;
- proportionality;
- equality review;
- staff and public transparency;
- independent oversight;
- meaningful human accountability;
- complaint routes;
- error correction;
- and visible consequences for misuse.

A public body cannot simply say:

Trust us.

A supplier cannot simply say:

Trust the tool.

An oversight body cannot simply say:

Trust our refusal.

Trust is built from records.

The records are the relationship.

Everything else is lighting.

---

## 🚩 What This Node Does Not Claim

This node does not claim:

- that no DPIA existed;
- that any DPIA was inadequate;
- that Palantir’s system was unlawful;
- that the Met unlawfully processed staff data;
- that MOPAC correctly identified every governance gap;
- that the contract should have been refused;
- or that a useful policing analytics system can never be adopted lawfully.

This node claims something narrower.

A useful tool can still sit on the wrong side of the DPIA, ethics, procurement, trust, and governance gap.

If the actors want their public claims believed, they need to show the adoption route.

Not just the tool.

The ring may work.

That is not the same as being allowed to wear it.

## 🔎 Questions This Node Routes Forward

This node routes the following questions:

- Was there a DPIA for the pilot?
- Was there a DPIA for the proposed larger contract?
- When was the DPIA started and completed?
- Did the DPIA cover staff data?
- Did it cover criminal-offence data, special-category data, or inferred data?
- Was the DPO involved?
- Were high residual risks identified?
- Was ethics review completed?
- Was equality or bias assessment completed?
- Were staff, unions, or representative bodies informed?
- Did procurement documents include data-protection and ethics requirements?
- Were audit, challenge, correction, and human-review routes specified?
- Did the contract include exit and anti-lock-in provisions?
- Did MOPAC see the full governance package before refusing approval?
- Did Palantir’s legal challenge make any missing governance records more relevant?
- What can be published, what must be redacted, and what needs independent oversight?

This is the governance gap.

Mind it.
