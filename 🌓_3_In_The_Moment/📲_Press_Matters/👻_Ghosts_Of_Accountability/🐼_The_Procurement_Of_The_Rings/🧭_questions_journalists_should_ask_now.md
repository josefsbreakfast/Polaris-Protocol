# 🧭 Questions Journalists Should Ask Now  
**First created:** 2026-06-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-06-23  
*A practical question bank for reporters, auditors, lawyers, unions, procurement specialists, and policing-accountability people before the cupboard closes.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

This is the final practical node in the **Procurement Of The Rings** cluster.

It converts the analysis into questions.

Not conclusions.

Questions.

Because this dispute is not only about whether Palantir, the Met, or MOPAC is right.

It is about what records exist, who holds them, what they show, what is hidden, what can be lawfully disclosed, what needs independent review, and what might disappear behind settlement, privilege, confidentiality, national security, policing capability, or administrative fog.

The core instruction is simple:

Ask now.

Before the litigation settles.

Before the procurement is rerouted.

Before the press line hardens.

Before the sensitive-material claim becomes a duvet.

Before the diligent little goblin holding the receipt is escorted out of the room by someone from Legal.

---

## 🧭 The Reporting Rule

Do not ask:

Who is the hero?

Ask:

What document would prove that?

Do not ask:

Who has the best line?

Ask:

Which record makes that line testable?

Do not ask:

Is Palantir good or bad?

Ask:

Which legal entity, contract, dataset, public function, procurement route, and risk network are being discussed?

Do not ask:

Was MOPAC political?

Ask:

What reasons were recorded, when, by whom, under what approval rule, and on what evidence?

Do not ask:

Did the Met need the tool?

Ask:

What operational assessment, pilot evaluation, alternatives analysis, public-safety model, and exit plan supports that claim?

The story is not only in the speeches.

The story is in the seams.

## 📌 First: Establish The Public Record

Ask:

- What is the exact proposed contract name?
- What was the proposed contract value?
- What period did the proposed contract cover?
- What optional extensions existed?
- What system or capability was being procured?
- What was the stated operational purpose?
- What earlier Palantir work had the Met already undertaken?
- What was the value of any earlier smaller contract?
- What did that earlier work involve?
- Which public bodies approved or reviewed the earlier work?
- What exactly did MOPAC refuse?
- On what date did MOPAC refuse approval?
- Who made the refusal decision?
- What formal document records the refusal?
- What reasons were given publicly?
- What reasons were given privately?
- What public statements did Palantir make after refusal?
- What public statements did the Met make after refusal?
- What public statements did MOPAC or City Hall make after refusal?
- Was a pre-action letter sent?
- Who sent it?
- To whom?
- On what date?
- What remedy was sought?
- Has a claim been filed?
- Has the dispute settled, paused, or escalated?

If the basic chronology is muddy, everything else becomes theatre.

Build the spine first.

## 🏷️ Entity Questions: Palantir Or Palantir UK?

Ask:

- Which Palantir entity held any earlier Met contract?
- Which Palantir entity was proposed for the larger contract?
- Which entity was listed on the procurement documents?
- Which entity was listed on any framework or supplier portal?
- Which entity sent or authorised legal correspondence?
- Which entity would bring any legal claim?
- Which entity claims commercial harm?
- Which entity would process data?
- Which entity would provide services?
- Which entity would hold liability?
- Which entity would sign the data-processing agreement?
- Which entity’s directors, accounts, persons with significant control, parent links, and affiliate relationships are relevant?
- Were parent-company guarantees, support obligations, or affiliate services involved?
- Were non-UK affiliates involved in support, implementation, updates, security, product development, or access?
- Where would data be hosted?
- Where would support staff be based?
- What cross-border access, if any, would be possible?
- Did public statements distinguish between the local entity and the global Palantir brand?
- Did Palantir rely on global credibility when selling and local-entity precision when challenged?

The legal name matters.

So does the aura.

Do not let either one eat the other.

## 🔮 Questions For Palantir

Ask Palantir:

- What exactly was unfair about the refusal?
- Was the complaint procedural, commercial, public-safety-based, reputational, or all of these?
- What procurement route did Palantir understand was being used?
- What approval route did Palantir understand applied?
- When did Palantir first become aware that MOPAC had concerns?
- What concerns were communicated to Palantir?
- How did Palantir respond?
- What evidence did Palantir provide about operational value?
- What evidence did Palantir provide about public-safety benefit?
- What evidence did Palantir provide about value for money?
- What alternatives did Palantir understand had been considered?
- What earlier pilot or smaller-contract outcomes did Palantir rely on?
- Were pilot success criteria agreed in advance?
- Were pilot results independently evaluated?
- What data safeguards did Palantir offer?
- What worker-facing safeguards did Palantir offer if staff data was involved?
- What audit logs, access controls, and human-review processes were proposed?
- What exit or transition provisions were offered?
- What subcontractors, affiliates, or parent-company functions would support delivery?
- What commercial harm does Palantir say it suffered?
- Does Palantir say the refusal harmed future market access?
- What documents support that claim?
- What records would Palantir be willing to disclose publicly, in redacted form, or to an independent reviewer?

Follow-up:

If Palantir says the refusal was political, ask what document proves political contamination.

If Palantir says public safety was harmed, ask what model proves harm.

If Palantir says the process was unfair, ask what process Palantir believed it was inside.

The crystal ball may answer.

The filing cabinet should answer too.

## 🐼 Questions For The Met

Ask the Met:

- What operational problem was the system meant to solve?
- Why was Palantir the preferred solution?
- What alternatives were considered?
- Was there a competitive process?
- Was there market engagement?
- What was the procurement route?
- What approval route was required?
- When was MOPAC first briefed?
- What did the Met submit to MOPAC?
- What concerns did MOPAC raise?
- When did MOPAC raise them?
- Did the Met believe approval was likely?
- Did operational dependency begin forming before approval was secured?
- What earlier Palantir work had been carried out?
- What did that earlier work show?
- Was there a formal pilot evaluation?
- What were the success criteria?
- Who evaluated them?
- Were results independently reviewed?
- Was the system linked to public-safety claims?
- Was it linked to officer-number or productivity modelling?
- What calculations supported those claims?
- What mitigation plan exists now that the contract has been blocked?
- What data categories would the system process?
- Would the system process staff data?
- Would it process victim, witness, suspect, intelligence, protest, internal-standards, misconduct, corruption, or vetting data?
- What DPIA was completed?
- When?
- Was it updated after refusal?
- What ethics review was carried out?
- What equality and human-rights assessment was carried out?
- What human-review safeguards existed?
- What error, bias, false-positive, or challenge routes existed?
- What staff notices were issued?
- Were unions or staff associations briefed?
- What exit plan existed?
- Could the Met walk away from the system without operational damage?

The Met may genuinely need better tools.

That does not cancel the need for receipts.

The panda car can keep the tactical map private.

It still needs to explain why it chose the route.

## 🏛️ Questions For MOPAC / City Hall

Ask MOPAC:

- What exactly was refused?
- Under what power was approval refused?
- Which approval threshold applied?
- Who made the decision?
- What document records the decision?
- What reasons were recorded at the time?
- Were those reasons procurement-based, value-for-money-based, public-trust-based, political, legal, or mixed?
- What value-for-money evidence was missing?
- What market engagement was considered insufficient?
- Was the issue the supplier, the route, the evidence, the price, the timing, or the governance package?
- When did MOPAC first become aware of the proposed contract?
- When did MOPAC first raise concerns?
- Did MOPAC raise concerns early enough for the Met to correct them?
- What did the Met submit?
- What did MOPAC ask for?
- What was not provided?
- Was public trust considered?
- How was public trust lawfully translated into procurement decision-making?
- Was Palantir’s wider brand or reputation considered?
- If yes, how was that separated from unlawful supplier prejudice?
- Were staff-data implications considered?
- Was the DPIA reviewed?
- Was the ethics review reviewed?
- Was equality or human-rights assessment reviewed?
- Was legal advice sought?
- Can non-privileged reasons be published?
- What records can be disclosed now?
- What records require redaction?
- What records require independent review?
- What would MOPAC need to see before approving any future similar contract?

Oversight cannot just say “we had concerns.”

Concerns are not minutes.

The temple needs receipts.

## ⚖️ Procurement Questions

Ask:

- What procurement route was used?
- Why was that route chosen?
- Was a framework used?
- Which framework?
- Who else was eligible under the framework?
- Was there a direct award?
- Was there competition?
- Was there market engagement?
- What evidence supported value for money?
- Was the contract below or above relevant approval thresholds?
- Who signed off each stage?
- Were procurement rules followed?
- Were deviations recorded?
- Was urgency used to justify the route?
- If urgency was used, who created the urgency?
- Was the procurement built around a pre-existing pilot relationship?
- Did earlier small-contract work create an incumbent advantage?
- Was supplier lock-in assessed?
- Was exit risk assessed?
- Was there a fallback supplier or fallback plan?
- Were alternative products compared?
- Were open standards or interoperability considered?
- Was public-sector in-house capacity assessed?
- Were contract termination provisions adequate?
- Were audit rights adequate?
- Were subcontractor arrangements clear?
- Were data rights and portability clear?
- Was future expansion anticipated?
- Was the optional extension part of the operational plan from the beginning?

Procurement is not admin garnish.

It is how public power prevents “inevitable” suppliers from becoming inevitable by accident.

## 🧾 DPIA, Data Protection, And Ethics Questions

Ask:

- Was a DPIA completed?
- When was it completed?
- Was it completed before dependency formed?
- Was it updated after scope changed?
- Who authored it?
- Who approved it?
- Was the ICO consulted or considered?
- What lawful basis was identified?
- What data categories were included?
- Were special-category data, criminal-offence data, intelligence data, or staff data involved?
- What data minimisation controls existed?
- What retention rules applied?
- What access controls existed?
- What audit logs existed?
- What human review existed?
- What challenge, correction, or appeal routes existed?
- What risks to rights and freedoms were identified?
- What mitigations were proposed?
- Were equality impacts assessed?
- Were human-rights impacts assessed?
- Was there an ethics review?
- Who sat on the ethics review?
- Were affected communities or staff representatives consulted?
- Was the DPIA treated as a living document or a procurement ornament?
- Can a redacted DPIA be published?
- If not, can a summary be published?
- If not, who has independently reviewed it?

A DPIA is not a decorative napkin under the dashboard.

It is supposed to catch risk before risk becomes infrastructure.

## 🛠️ Worker And Staff-Data Questions

Ask:

- Did the earlier Palantir work process Met staff data?
- Did the proposed system process Met staff data?
- What categories of staff data were involved?
- Were internal standards, misconduct, corruption, criminality, vetting, deployment, or concerning-behaviour records involved?
- Were inferred risk profiles created?
- Could outputs affect careers, discipline, vetting, deployment, internal reputation, or management attention?
- Were staff told?
- When?
- In what notice?
- Were staff associations or unions briefed?
- Were equality impacts on staff assessed?
- Were false positives assessed?
- Was human review mandatory?
- Could staff challenge or correct data?
- Were staff subject-access routes clear?
- Were disciplinary or employment-law implications assessed?
- Did worker-facing outcomes become part of the claimed operational value?
- Did worker-facing outcomes support the case for expansion?
- Did MOPAC review the staff-data implications?
- Did Palantir offer staff-facing safeguards?
- Did the Met document them?
- Could an independent reviewer inspect staff-data safeguards without exposing named staff records?

Workers are not an incidental data substrate.

Nobody should become the spreadsheet without being told.

## 🎭 Public-Safety Claim Questions

Ask anyone invoking public safety:

- What specific public-safety harm is alleged?
- Who modelled it?
- When?
- What assumptions were used?
- What data supports it?
- Is the harm immediate, medium-term, or speculative?
- Does it relate to investigations, intelligence, internal standards, productivity, officer numbers, budget, or response capacity?
- What alternatives were considered?
- What mitigation plan exists?
- Was public-safety harm recorded before refusal or mainly after refusal?
- Were internal caveats included?
- Were those caveats reflected in public statements?
- Was the claim independently reviewed?
- Can a redacted risk assessment be published?
- Can methodology be published without operational detail?
- Did public-safety claims become stronger after legal escalation?
- Who signed off the public-safety line?
- Was public safety being used as evidence, pressure, or both?

Public safety matters.

That is why it needs evidence.

A siren is not a spreadsheet.

- Why?
- Under which category?
- National security?
- Sensitive policing capability?
- Commercial confidentiality?
- Legal privilege?
- Personal data?
- Staff data?
- Procurement confidentiality?
- NDA?
- Operational secrecy?
- Who decided the category?
- Can the document title be disclosed?
- Can the date be disclosed?
- Can the author or decision-maker be disclosed?
- Can a document-category log be published?
- Can a redacted version be released?
- Can a summary be released?
- Can an independent reviewer see it?
- Can a regulator see it?
- Can a committee see it?
- Can a court see it under controlled handling?
- Can the public be told whether the hidden material supports or undermines the public claim?
- Is sensitivity being used as a scalpel or a duvet?

Sensitive does not mean untouchable.

It means route carefully.

## 👻 Litigation And Settlement Questions

Ask:

- What claims are being threatened or brought?
- What remedy is sought?
- Is Palantir seeking damages, reconsideration, disclosure, declaration, contract award, rerun, or settlement?
- Is the Met formally involved?
- Is MOPAC the target?
- Is City Hall involved?
- Are other public bodies watching?
- Could settlement bury the records?
- Would settlement include confidentiality?
- Would settlement preserve future bidding rights?
- Would settlement rerun the procurement?
- Would settlement include public reasons?
- What records should be preserved before settlement?
- What records could be requested through FOI before settlement?
- What worker-facing questions should unions ask before settlement?
- What procurement records should auditors request before settlement?
- What data-protection records should regulators request before settlement?
- Could the litigation create useful disclosure even if the claimant is self-interested?
- What accountability value disappears if the case settles quietly?

Commercial litigation is not sainthood.

Still check the cupboard.

- When did urgency enter the public line?
- When did public safety enter the public line?
- When did unfairness or politicisation enter the public line?
- Did public statements match legal correspondence?
- Did public statements match internal records?
- Did the Met’s public statements reinforce Palantir’s public-safety line?
- Did Palantir rely on the Met’s operational claim?
- Did MOPAC’s public statements match its refusal record?
- Were there media briefings?
- Were there stakeholder briefings?
- Were there investor-facing or client-facing reassurance lines?
- Were there internal caveats missing from public communication?
- Were local-entity and global-brand frames used differently in different forums?
- Which public line would become awkward if the internal record were published?

Institutions have body language.

Documents are the physiotherapy.

## ♚ First-Mover Risk Questions

Ask:

- What did Palantir gain by moving first?
- What did it expose by moving first?
- Which of Palantir’s own records become relevant because of its claims?
- Which Met records become relevant because Palantir relies on operational need?
- Which MOPAC records become relevant because Palantir challenges refusal?
- Which public-safety records become relevant because urgency is invoked?
- Which market-access records become relevant because commercial harm is claimed?
- Which staff-data records become relevant because operational value may involve internal standards?
- Did the move force MOPAC onto the defensive?
- Did it also place Palantir on an attacked square?
- What is the square each actor thinks it is occupying?
- What is already attacking that square?

A legal move may be available.

That does not mean the board is safe.

## 🕸️ Mandelson / Humble Address / Adjacent Palantir-Proximity Questions

Keep this file distinct from the Met procurement dispute.

Then ask the overlap questions carefully.

- What Palantir-related entities appear in the Met procurement record?
- What Palantir-related entities appear in Government contracting records?
- What Palantir-related entities appear in parliamentary conflict-of-interest or Humble Address material?
- What Palantir-related entities appear in public reporting around Lord Mandelson?
- Are these the same entity, related entities, parent/affiliate structures, or simply the same global brand?
- What material has been withheld at the request of the Met in the Mandelson investigation context?
- What categories of material were withheld?
- Do those categories reveal anything about the hidden-layer problem more broadly?
- Are the legal-risk networks distinct, overlapping, or merely reputationally adjacent?
- Does Palantir’s legal escalation over the Met contract make wider Palantir-proximity reporting more relevant?
- Does the public experience “Palantir” as one name across multiple sensitive state-capability contexts?
- If so, how should reporting separate proof from proximity?
- Who has the keys to each cabinet?
- Are any advisers, routes, redaction habits, entities, or decision-makers repeated?
- What would prove overlap?
- What would disprove overlap?

Do not mash the files together.

Do not pretend the repeated name is meaningless either.

Distinct cabinets.

Possible shared corridor.

Check the corridor.

That is exactly why Palantir matters.

## 📰 Questions For Editors

Ask:

- Are we distinguishing fact, allegation, public claim, and analysis?
- Are we naming the exact legal entity where possible?
- Are we avoiding mascot logic?
- Are we over-relying on one actor’s frame?
- Are we treating public safety as a claim requiring evidence?
- Are we treating sensitivity as a category requiring discipline?
- Are we separating the Met procurement dispute from adjacent Palantir-proximity files?
- Are we explaining why the adjacency matters without implying proof?
- Are we asking worker-facing questions?
- Are we asking data-protection questions?
- Are we asking procurement questions?
- Are we asking democratic-control questions?
- Are we preserving the structural story beyond Palantir?
- Are we publishing before checking the cupboard?
- Are we waiting so long that the cupboard gets quietly moved?

The headline may say Palantir.

The story should say paperwork.

## 🚩 What This Node Does Not Claim

This node does not claim:

- that Palantir acted unlawfully;
- that the Met acted unlawfully;
- that MOPAC acted unlawfully;
- that public-safety claims are false;
- that all hidden material should be published;
- that every adjacent Palantir-proximity issue is part of the same dispute;

Those questions should be asked now.
