# 🐼 Best Case Met  
**First created:** 2026-06-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-06-23  
*The strongest fair version of the Met’s case: operational need, broken systems, public-safety pressure, and the awkward little matter of showing the workings.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

This node gives the strongest fair version of the Metropolitan Police case in the Met / Palantir / MOPAC procurement dispute.

It does not endorse the Met.

It does not excuse weak governance.

It does not convert “public safety” into a blank cheque.

It asks what the Met’s case looks like if read seriously.

That matters because the Met may have real operational problems.

Fragmented systems can harm investigations.

Slow data analysis can harm victims.

Internal corruption and misconduct detection can matter deeply.

Budget pressure can force ugly trade-offs.

A policing body can have a genuine need for better tools and still fail to adopt them lawfully.

Both things can be true.

The panda car may be trying to get to a real emergency.

It still needs lights, brakes, and paperwork.

---

## 🧭 Steelman Rule

This is a steelman node.

It asks:

What is the strongest fair version of the Met’s position, assuming the force is not lying, not stupid, and not simply waving “public safety” around like a procurement lightsabre?

Then it asks:

What would the Met need to show for that version to survive scrutiny?

A best case is not a permission slip.

It is a higher evidential bar.

---

## 🐼 The Strongest Fair Case

The Met’s strongest fair case is roughly this:

London policing is under severe pressure.

The force faces complex criminal investigations, organised crime, hostile-state threats, corruption risks, internal misconduct, safeguarding duties, protest policing, public trust problems, and budget constraints.

Its data systems are fragmented.

Its analysts and officers cannot always access or connect information at the speed modern policing requires.

The proposed Unified Operational Analytics capability was intended to help the force work faster, identify risks earlier, improve productivity, support investigations, and detect internal corruption or concerning staff behaviour.

The Met believed Palantir’s system had already shown value through earlier work or pilot use.

The larger contract was therefore not frivolous.

It was an operational attempt to keep the house standing while the roof, budget, and public trust all made unpleasant noises at once.

That is a serious case.

It still needs evidence.

## 🗂️ Fragmented Systems

The Met may argue that fragmented systems are not a minor inconvenience.

They can cause operational harm.

If information sits across disconnected databases, legacy systems, spreadsheets, case files, intelligence logs, staff records, and local workarounds, then officers and analysts may miss patterns.

That can matter for:

- serious crime;
- organised criminal networks;
- safeguarding;
- exploitation;
- corruption;
- misconduct;
- repeat offenders;
- risk escalation;
- resource allocation;
- and internal standards work.

A force with poor data integration may reasonably seek better analytical tooling.

The public should not pretend this problem is fake just because the supplier is controversial.

But the Met must show:

- which systems were fragmented;
- what operational harm resulted;
- what use cases needed improvement;
- why existing tools were inadequate;
- why Palantir was the preferred answer;
- and what safeguards would stop integration becoming overreach.

Data fragmentation is a real problem.

So is data fusion without governance.

The cure should not become a larger creature than the disease.

---

## 🎭 Public Safety

The Met’s strongest fair case places public safety at the centre.

It may say the blocked contract affected:

- investigation speed;
- intelligence analysis;
- serious-crime disruption;
- organised-crime mapping;
- corruption detection;
- officer and staff standards work;
- productivity targets;
- and the force’s ability to manage budget pressure without deeper service cuts.

That case should be taken seriously.

Public safety is not a fake concern simply because it is rhetorically useful.

But public safety claims need a record.

The Met would need to show:

- what risks were forecast if the contract did not proceed;
- how those risks were calculated;
- what evidence linked UOA to specific safety improvements;
- what alternatives were available;
- what mitigations were considered after refusal;
- who signed off public-safety claims;
- and whether those claims were internally caveated before they became public.

Public safety can justify urgency.

It cannot justify skipping the question of whether the tool was lawfully, fairly, and safely adopted.

Emergency lighting is not a governance framework.

---

## 💸 Budget And Workforce Pressure

The Met may argue that budget pressure made the technology urgent.

If the force faced a funding gap, staffing pressure, or planned workforce reductions, then productivity tools may have seemed necessary to protect frontline delivery.

The strongest fair version is:

The Met was not trying to buy shiny AI because it fancied a dashboard.

It was trying to maintain service under financial constraint.

That is plausible.

It is also exactly why the numbers matter.

If the Met claimed the system could offset hundreds of posts, reduce cuts, or protect operational targets, the calculation should be available for scrutiny.

The questions are:

- who calculated the savings?
- what assumptions were used?
- were savings recurring or one-off?
- did savings depend on staff reductions?
- did the model account for implementation cost?
- did it account for training, governance, audit, maintenance, error correction, and legal compliance?
- did it account for risk if the system underperformed?
- and did MOPAC see the full calculation before refusal?

A budget crisis can be real.

A savings model can still be optimistic.

The spreadsheet is not holy just because it looks frightened.

## 🛠️ Internal Standards And Staff Data

The Met’s strongest case may include internal standards, corruption detection, or identifying concerning staff behaviour.

That is not a marginal issue.

The Met has faced severe public trust problems around misconduct, abuse, discrimination, corruption, and failures to protect women and vulnerable people.

A tool that helps identify serious internal risk could have public value.

But this is where the worker-facing problem becomes sharp.

If staff data, conduct records, complaints, vetting information, inferred risk indicators, or internal reputation signals were processed, then staff governance matters.

The Met would need to show:

- what staff were told;
- whether staff had meaningful notice;
- whether unions or staff associations were briefed;
- what categories of staff data were processed;
- whether sensitive or inferred risk data was used;
- whether equality and bias risks were assessed;
- how false positives were handled;
- whether human review was mandatory;
- whether staff could challenge or correct outputs;
- whether outputs could affect vetting, discipline, deployment, trust, or career progression;
- and how misuse would be detected.

A force trying to fix internal misconduct should not create a black-box worker-risk machine on the way there.

The goal may be legitimate.

The safeguards still matter.

---

## 🔮 Why Palantir?

The Met’s strongest fair case must explain why Palantir specifically.

It is not enough to say:

We needed better technology.

The question is:

Why this supplier, through this route, at this value, at this time?

The Met may argue:

- Palantir had already shown value;
- the tool was mature;
- integration would be faster;
- alternatives could not deliver quickly enough;
- the procurement route was available;
- existing familiarity reduced implementation risk;
- and operational urgency justified continuity.

Those are serious arguments.

But each one needs records.

If Palantir was uniquely suitable, what comparison showed that?

If alternatives were too slow, who assessed them?

If familiarity reduced risk, did it also create dependency?

If continuity was valuable, did it narrow competition?

If the route was lawful, was it also appropriate for sensitive policing infrastructure?

The Met does not need to prove Palantir is magic.

It needs to prove Palantir was selected properly.

---

## 🏛️ Relationship With MOPAC

The Met’s strongest fair case may say MOPAC was not surprised.

It may argue that MOPAC had been briefed, had opportunities to ask questions, and allowed the process to continue until late refusal created avoidable harm.

If true, that matters.

Oversight should not keep a procurement alive long enough to create dependency, then detonate it when the consequences are maximally painful.

But the Met also has questions to answer.

If MOPAC approval was required, why was the approval route not settled earlier?

If MOPAC had concerns, why were they not resolved before the contract reached crisis point?

If the Met knew the supplier was controversial, why was the evidential record not made especially robust?

If the operational need was urgent, why was the governance path allowed to become fragile?

A force cannot complain that the bridge collapsed if it drove heavy equipment over a bridge it knew still needed inspection.

The inspection may have been late.

The vehicle was still heavy.

---

## 🧾 Procurement Route

The Met may argue that it used a lawful procurement route, such as a recognised public-sector framework.

That is relevant.

But it is not complete.

The Met would need to show:

- which route was used;
- why it was suitable;
- what market engagement occurred;
- what alternatives were considered;
- why only one supplier was seriously engaged, if that is accurate;
- how value for money was assessed;
- who approved the strategy;
- when MOPAC approval was required;
- what documents were shared;
- and whether the route matched the sensitivity and scale of the proposed system.

A framework can make a contract available.

It does not make governance automatic.

The shelf may contain the tool.

Someone still has to justify taking it home.

## ⚖️ Strongest Defence Against The Veto

The Met’s strongest defence against the MOPAC veto may be:

The refusal came too late, after repeated engagement, when the force had already built operational and financial planning around the system.

The refusal caused avoidable disruption.

The procurement concerns could have been raised earlier, resolved earlier, or handled through conditions rather than a hard stop.

That is a fair argument to test.

But again, the Met must show the timeline.

It would need records of:

- briefings to MOPAC;
- written submissions;
- meeting notes;
- approval requests;
- responses received;
- unresolved concerns;
- risk escalation;
- internal warnings;
- and the point at which the Met believed approval was likely or agreed in principle.

The Met’s best case improves if the records show it was transparent, responsive, and procedurally careful.

It weakens if the records show urgency was allowed to outrun oversight.

The panda car cannot simply honk outside City Hall and call that consultation.

## 🗄️ What The Met Would Need To Show

For its strongest case to survive scrutiny, the Met would need records showing:

- the operational problem;
- the capability gap;
- why existing systems were inadequate;
- why UOA was the proposed answer;
- why Palantir was chosen;
- what alternatives were considered;
- what pilot or earlier work demonstrated;
- how public-safety claims were assessed;
- how budget and workforce claims were calculated;
- what procurement route was used;
- what approvals were required;
- when MOPAC was briefed;
- what MOPAC was told;
- what staff data would be processed;
- what staff-facing safeguards existed;
- what DPIA, equality, ethics, and governance reviews were completed;
- and what mitigations were available after refusal.

If the Met’s case is operational necessity, then the operational record matters.

If its case is process frustration, then the process record matters.

If its case is public safety, then the risk record matters.

This is not unfair.

This is what follows from taking the Met seriously.

## 🚩 Limits Of The Best Case

Even if the Met’s best case is strong, it would not prove:

- that Palantir was the only lawful supplier;
- that the procurement was properly approved;
- that MOPAC acted unlawfully;
- that public-safety claims were fully evidenced;
- that staff-data safeguards were adequate;
- that budget claims were realistic;
- that urgency justified late-stage pressure;
- or that a useful tool was adopted through a good process.

A real problem can be answered badly.

A useful tool can be procured weakly.

A public-safety claim can be sincere and still inflated.

A force can need modernisation and still need oversight.

That is the point.

The stronger the operational case, the stronger the governance obligation.

## 🧭 Questions This Node Routes Forward

This node routes the following questions:

- What operational problem was UOA intended to solve?
- What evidence showed Palantir’s tool worked?
- What alternative suppliers or tools were assessed?
- What public-safety risks were forecast if the contract failed?
- How were officer-number or workforce impacts calculated?
- What staff data was processed during earlier pilot or standards work?
- What staff notice, union briefing, or challenge route existed?
- When was MOPAC first briefed?
- What did the Met believe MOPAC had approved or accepted?
- What procurement route was used?
- Why was that route appropriate for sensitive policing infrastructure?
- What documents show urgency did not outrun lawful governance?

The Met gets its strongest case.

Then the records take the wheel.
