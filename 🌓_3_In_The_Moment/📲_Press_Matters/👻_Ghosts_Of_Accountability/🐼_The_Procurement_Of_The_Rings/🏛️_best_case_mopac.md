# 🏛️ Best Case MOPAC  
**First created:** 2026-06-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-06-23  
*The strongest fair version of MOPAC’s case: democratic oversight, value for money, procurement discipline, public trust, and the audacity of asking the police to show their working.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

This node gives the strongest fair version of the Mayor’s Office for Policing and Crime case in the Met / Palantir procurement dispute.

It does not endorse MOPAC.

It does not assume City Hall is clean because it says “oversight.”

It does not pretend political actors become holy when standing near a procurement rule.

It asks what MOPAC’s case looks like if read seriously.

That matters because MOPAC may have had a real oversight problem in front of it.

A large policing-technology contract involving sensitive data, a controversial supplier, operational policing, internal standards work, and public trust should not be waved through because the force says it is urgent.

Democratic oversight exists for exactly the moment when coercive public infrastructure wants to move quickly.

The lanyard is not a sacrament.

But sometimes the lanyard is attached to a real job.

---

## 🧭 Steelman Rule

This is a steelman node.

It asks:

What is the strongest fair version of MOPAC’s position, assuming it is not lying, not stupid, and not simply performing political values while hiding weak process?

Then it asks:

What would MOPAC need to show for that version to survive scrutiny?

A best case is not sainthood.

It is an evidential demand.

---

## 🏛️ The Strongest Fair Case

MOPAC’s strongest fair case is roughly this:

The Met was seeking approval for a major contract involving sensitive policing technology.

The proposed supplier was controversial.

The system would sit close to coercive public power.

The process raised serious concerns about procurement strategy, market engagement, value for money, approval route, public trust, and democratic accountability.

The Met had engaged too narrowly with one supplier.

The contract was too large, too sensitive, and too precedent-setting to approve without stronger evidence.

MOPAC therefore refused approval.

Not because it hates technology.

Not because it wants policing to fail.

Because public bodies should not buy powerful data infrastructure through a weak or insufficiently evidenced process.

That is a serious case.

It needs records.

---

## ⚖️ Procurement Discipline

MOPAC’s strongest case begins with procurement discipline.

Public procurement is not a formality.

It exists to protect:

- value for money;
- fair competition;
- transparency;
- public trust;
- equal treatment;
- lawful decision-making;
- and the integrity of public spending.

A police force cannot avoid those duties by saying the tool is useful.

Usefulness is relevant.

It is not the whole test.

MOPAC may argue that the Met had not shown enough market engagement, had not demonstrated value for money, had not followed approval routes properly, or had not provided the necessary evidence for a large contract.

If that is true, refusal may have been responsible.

A contract involving sensitive policing data should not be approved because everyone is tired and the demo looked good.

The public sector is not meant to sign procurement vows in a blackout.

---

## 💸 Value For Money

MOPAC’s strongest fair case includes value for money.

A £50 million shorthand contract, with a first-year value and optional extension, is not a trivial spend.

Public bodies have to justify why that money should go to this supplier, for this tool, through this route, at this time.

That justification should include:

- clear operational need;
- cost-benefit analysis;
- comparison with alternatives;
- implementation costs;
- training costs;
- governance costs;
- audit costs;
- maintenance costs;
- exit costs;
- dependency risks;
- and evidence that claimed savings are realistic.

MOPAC may argue that the Met did not demonstrate enough of this.

That is not anti-technology.

That is basic stewardship.

A tool that promises to save money should survive someone asking to see the maths.

## 🔮 Supplier Controversy And Public Trust

MOPAC’s strongest case may include public trust concerns around Palantir.

That is delicate.

Supplier controversy cannot simply replace procurement analysis.

A public body cannot lawfully refuse a supplier just because the supplier has become a vibes-based reputational inconvenience.

But public trust can matter where the contract involves sensitive policing infrastructure.

If a supplier’s wider reputation would affect public confidence in policing, data use, democratic oversight, or the legitimacy of coercive systems, then MOPAC may be entitled to consider that — provided it does so lawfully, consistently, and proportionately.

The strongest MOPAC case is not:

Palantir is unpopular, therefore no.

It is:

A controversial supplier entering sensitive policing infrastructure requires a stronger, clearer, more defensible record, not a weaker one.

That is a fair position.

It still needs evidence.

## 🐼 Oversight Of The Met

MOPAC’s role is not to act as the Met’s procurement rubber stamp.

The Met has operational expertise.

But the Met is also a coercive public authority with a long record of public trust problems, misconduct scandals, institutional failures, and reform pressures.

That does not mean every Met request is bad.

It means oversight matters.

MOPAC may argue that the Met was asking to expand a powerful analytical capability without enough approval discipline.

It may also argue that operational urgency was being used to compress democratic scrutiny.

That is a serious concern.

If the police say:

We need this urgently.

The oversight response cannot simply be:

Best not ask too many questions then.

Urgency may make scrutiny harder.

It also makes scrutiny more important.

---

## 🛠️ Staff And Worker Transparency

MOPAC’s strongest case may also include worker-facing governance.

If earlier Palantir work or the proposed system touched internal standards, corruption detection, misconduct, criminality, or concerning staff behaviour, then staff transparency matters.

MOPAC may argue that a system processing or analysing staff-related data requires stronger safeguards before expansion.

That may include:

- staff notice;
- union or staff-association briefing;
- categories of staff data processed;
- proportionality assessment;
- DPIA;
- equality assessment;
- false-positive controls;
- human review;
- audit;
- challenge routes;
- correction routes;
- restrictions on use in vetting, discipline, deployment, or career-impacting decisions;
- and clarity over whether pilot data became evidence for expansion.

This is not a defence of bad officers.

It is a defence of lawful staff governance.

A force cannot fix misconduct by building a second, less visible accountability problem inside the first one.

If MOPAC had concerns here, they should be documented.

If it did not, that absence is also interesting.

---

## 🧾 Approval Route

MOPAC’s strongest fair case depends heavily on approval route.

If the Met was required to present the procurement strategy, contract, or approval request to MOPAC at a particular stage, and did not do so properly, then MOPAC may have had a strong basis for refusal.

The questions are:

- what approval was required?
- under what scheme of delegation?
- at what threshold?
- when did the earlier pilot or lower-value work become relevant?
- when did the larger contract trigger approval duties?
- what did the Met submit?
- when did it submit it?
- what did MOPAC ask for?
- what did MOPAC receive?
- and what was missing?

MOPAC’s best case strengthens if the record shows clear rules, clear requests, clear gaps, and timely concern.

It weakens if the record shows delay, ambiguity, shifting objections, or late-stage political panic.

Oversight is strongest when it leaves footprints before the explosion.

---

## 🧩 Single-Supplier Concern

MOPAC’s strongest fair case may include concern that the Met engaged too seriously with only one supplier.

That matters because single-supplier drift can create dependency before a contract is awarded.

Supplier familiarity can become supplier inevitability.

A pilot can become a preferred route.

A preferred route can become a public-safety emergency.

Then the oversight actor is told refusal would be catastrophic.

MOPAC may argue that this is exactly why market testing matters.

The questions are:

- which suppliers were considered?
- what alternatives were available?
- what framework options existed?
- why were alternatives rejected?
- was Palantir uniquely suitable?
- or was Palantir simply already inside the building?

A single-supplier path may be justified in some cases.

But the justification should be explicit.

The crystal ball cannot become inevitable because everyone else was kept in the cupboard.

---

## 🎭 Public Safety As Pressure Frame

MOPAC’s strongest case may treat the public-safety line with caution.

The Met and Palantir may argue that blocking the contract risked public safety.

That may be true.

But MOPAC may argue:

Public safety cannot be used to override procurement legality after the force has allowed itself to become dependent on an unapproved path.

If the contract was so critical that refusal damaged public safety, then the governance failure may have begun earlier.

Who allowed such a critical capability to reach dependency before approval?

Who attached officer-number claims to an unapproved system?

Who assessed alternatives?

Who decided the risk of refusal was acceptable?

MOPAC does not need to deny public-safety concerns to ask those questions.

It can say:

If public safety is at stake, show the record.

If the record is weak, the urgency may be part of the problem.

The smoke alarm may be real.

Someone still needs to explain why the wiring was left like that.

---

## 🗄️ Democratic Control Of Policing Technology

MOPAC’s strongest case is ultimately about democratic control.

Policing technology is not ordinary software procurement.

It may affect:

- suspects;
- victims;
- witnesses;
- communities;
- protesters;
- staff;
- officers;
- intelligence subjects;
- vulnerable people;
- and people whose lives can be changed by invisible inferences.

That means public oversight cannot be an afterthought.

MOPAC may argue that large-scale analytical tools inside policing require a higher public-governance standard.

Not because technology is bad.

Because coercive technology is powerful.

The stronger the tool, the stronger the oversight should be.

The more useful the system, the more dangerous it is to adopt badly.

That is the paradox.

A weak tool is a waste of money.

A strong tool is a governance emergency if bought casually.

## 🔎 What MOPAC Would Need To Show

For its strongest case to survive scrutiny, MOPAC would need records showing:

- the approval rule or scheme of delegation;
- the procurement threshold;
- when the Met was required to seek approval;
- what the Met submitted;
- what MOPAC reviewed;
- when concerns were first raised;
- what concerns were raised;
- whether those concerns were consistent over time;
- what value-for-money evidence was missing;
- what market-testing evidence was missing;
- how public-trust concerns were framed lawfully;
- whether values concerns were separated from procurement reasoning;
- what staff-data or worker-transparency concerns were considered;
- what legal advice or procurement advice supported refusal;
- and why refusal was proportionate at the point it happened.

If MOPAC’s case is oversight, then the oversight record matters.

If its case is value for money, then the value record matters.

If its case is process, then the process record matters.

The temple needs receipts.

## 🚩 Limits Of The Best Case

Even if MOPAC’s best case is strong, it would not prove:

- that Palantir’s tool lacked operational value;
- that the Met had no genuine need;
- that public-safety concerns were invented;
- that political discomfort played no role;
- that refusal was timely;
- that MOPAC handled the process well;
- that staff-data questions were adequately considered;
- or that democratic oversight automatically means good governance.

A public body can be right to refuse and still have handled the route badly.

An oversight actor can identify a real problem too late.

A values concern can be morally understandable but legally messy.

A procurement objection can be valid and still politically convenient.

That is why MOPAC’s strongest case also creates disclosure pressure.

If the office did the grown-up thing, it should be able to show the grown-up paperwork.

## 🧭 Questions This Node Routes Forward

This node routes the following questions:

- What approval rule did the Met need to follow?
- When did the proposed contract trigger MOPAC approval?
- What was MOPAC told about the earlier pilot or lower-value work?
- When did MOPAC first raise concern?
- What market engagement did MOPAC think was missing?
- What value-for-money evidence did MOPAC require?
- How did MOPAC separate procurement concerns from values concerns?
- What public-trust assessment was made?
- Were staff-data or worker-transparency issues considered?
- Did MOPAC act early enough?
- Did MOPAC leave open a fairer procurement route?
- What documents show refusal was reasoned, proportionate, and lawful?
- What documents might show delay, political contamination, or shifting rationale?

MOPAC gets its strongest case.

Then the records get to judge whether the lanyard was doing governance or theatre.
