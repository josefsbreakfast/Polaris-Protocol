# ⚖️ What Each Side Needs To Disclose  
**First created:** 2026-06-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-06-23  
*The disclosure triangle: if each actor wants its best case believed, each actor needs to show its working.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

This node turns the three best-case nodes into a disclosure map.

The question is no longer:

Who sounds most persuasive?

The question is:

What would each actor need to disclose for its own strongest case to survive scrutiny?

Palantir has a best case.

The Met has a best case.

MOPAC has a best case.

Each case creates a paperwork burden.

That is the disclosure triangle.

The triangle matters because every actor’s defence puts pressure on the others.

If Palantir says the refusal was unfair, MOPAC’s reasoning becomes relevant.

If the Met says the tool was operationally necessary, the Met’s assessment becomes relevant.

If MOPAC says the procurement was weak, the procurement record becomes relevant.

Nobody can defend themselves cleanly without making the record more interesting.

Awkward.

Excellent.

---

## 🧭 Disclosure Rule

This node does not assume wrongdoing.

It does not demand reckless publication of sensitive policing material.

It does not pretend every document can or should be made public in full.

Some material may be constrained by:

- legal privilege;
- commercial confidentiality;
- national security;
- sensitive policing capability;
- personal data;
- staff data;
- operational risk;
- procurement confidentiality;
- or court directions.

That does not make disclosure irrelevant.

It means the disclosure questions need discipline.

The public may not be entitled to every operational detail.

The public is still entitled to know whether large policing-technology decisions were lawful, reasoned, governed, proportionate, and properly approved.

The locked cabinet may stay locked.

But someone still has to explain why the cabinet exists, who put it there, and which register says what is inside.

## ⚖️ The Disclosure Triangle

The dispute creates three linked evidential burdens.

### 🔮 Palantir

Palantir needs to show that the refusal was unfair, irrational, politicised, procedurally flawed, commercially damaging, or harmful to public safety.

### 🐼 The Met

The Met needs to show that the proposed system was operationally necessary, properly assessed, fairly procured, lawfully governed, and genuinely connected to public-safety, productivity, or internal-standards needs.

### 🏛️ MOPAC

MOPAC needs to show that the refusal was lawful, timely, reasoned, proportionate, grounded in procurement or governance concerns, and not merely political discomfort wearing a procurement hat.

Each actor’s claim creates a request for records.

The triangle is not decorative.

It is a machine for turning public posture into document demand.

---

## 🔮 What Palantir Needs To Show

If Palantir wants its strongest case believed, it needs to show more than grievance.

It needs to show the evidential basis for saying the refusal was unfair or improper.

That may include:

- what the Met asked Palantir to provide;
- what Palantir offered;
- what earlier pilot or contract work showed;
- what success criteria were used;
- what operational benefits were recorded;
- what user feedback or evaluation existed;
- what representations the Met made about approval, timing, suitability, or likely expansion;
- what procurement route Palantir understood was being used;
- what Palantir knew about MOPAC approval requirements;
- when Palantir first became aware of MOPAC concerns;
- how Palantir responded to those concerns;
- what alternatives Palantir believed had been considered;
- why Palantir believed it was being treated inconsistently or unfairly;
- what commercial harm followed from the refusal;
- and what legal basis supports its challenge.

If Palantir says politics contaminated the decision, it needs evidence of contamination.

If it says the decision harmed public safety, it needs evidence of operational consequence.

If it says the process was unfair, it needs the process record.

The crystal ball does not get to rely on atmosphere.

It needs receipts.

---

## 🔮 Palantir’s Awkward Disclosure Risk

Palantir’s own case may make its own records relevant.

That may include:

- pre-contract correspondence;
- public-relations strategy;
- legal escalation timing;
- internal assessment of reputational risk;
- records of lobbying or stakeholder engagement;
- records of what it was told by the Met;
- records of what it understood MOPAC had approved;
- internal assessment of the likelihood of challenge;
- claims made to investors, clients, or public-sector prospects;
- and records showing how it framed the refusal internally and externally.

This does not mean Palantir did anything wrong.

It means that once a supplier says the process was unfair, the supplier’s own understanding of the process becomes relevant.

If the paperwork battlefield is chosen, the paperwork does not politely stop at the other side’s doorstep.

---

## 🐼 What The Met Needs To Show

If the Met wants its strongest case believed, it needs to show more than urgency.

It needs to show the operational basis for the proposed contract.

That may include:

- the operational problem UOA was intended to solve;
- the systems or workflows said to be fragmented;
- the capability gap assessment;
- why existing tools were inadequate;
- what earlier Palantir work demonstrated;
- why Palantir was preferred;
- what alternatives were considered;
- how value for money was assessed;
- how public-safety claims were calculated;
- how budget and workforce claims were calculated;
- what productivity savings were expected;
- what risk was forecast if the contract did not proceed;
- what mitigations were available after refusal;
- what procurement route was used;
- what approvals were required;
- what MOPAC was told and when;
- what staff-data safeguards existed;
- what DPIA, equality, ethics, and governance reviews were completed;
- and how the system would be audited, challenged, corrected, and governed.

If the Met says the tool was necessary, it needs the necessity record.

If it says refusal created public-safety risk, it needs the risk record.

If it says the procurement was proper, it needs the procurement record.

The panda car can have a real emergency.

It still needs a roadworthy file.

---

## 🐼 The Met’s Awkward Disclosure Risk

The Met’s own case may expose weaknesses it would rather leave vague.

That may include:

- whether the force became operationally dependent before approval;
- whether public-safety claims were internally caveated;
- whether officer-number claims were based on robust modelling;
- whether budget savings were optimistic;
- whether staff-data use was properly explained;
- whether unions or staff associations were briefed;
- whether the earlier pilot created continuity pressure;
- whether alternative suppliers were seriously assessed;
- whether MOPAC concerns were known earlier than publicly suggested;
- and whether urgency was created by the force’s own sequencing.

This does not mean the Met acted improperly.

It means that operational necessity is not a magic curtain.

If the force wants the public to understand the harm of refusal, the public can ask how that harm was allowed to become so large before final approval.

Emergency is sometimes real.

Sometimes emergency is also a governance invoice arriving late.

---

## 🏛️ What MOPAC Needs To Show

If MOPAC wants its strongest case believed, it needs to show more than oversight language.

It needs to show the decision was lawful, reasoned, timely, and properly grounded.

That may include:

- the approval rule or scheme of delegation;
- the relevant financial threshold;
- what approval the Met was required to seek;
- when that approval was required;
- when MOPAC first learned of the proposed contract;
- when MOPAC first learned of the earlier pilot or smaller contract;
- what the Met submitted;
- what MOPAC reviewed;
- what concerns MOPAC raised;
- when those concerns were raised;
- whether those concerns were consistent over time;
- what value-for-money evidence was missing;
- what market-testing evidence was missing;
- what procurement advice was received;
- what legal advice supported refusal;
- how public-trust concerns were framed;
- whether values concerns were separated from procurement reasoning;
- whether staff-data and worker-transparency issues were considered;
- and why refusal, rather than conditional approval or rerun procurement, was proportionate.

If MOPAC says the procurement was inadequate, it needs the inadequacy record.

If it says value for money was not shown, it needs the value-for-money gap.

If it says oversight worked, it needs the oversight timeline.

The temple needs receipts too.

---

## 🏛️ MOPAC’s Awkward Disclosure Risk

MOPAC’s own case may expose uncomfortable questions.

That may include:

- whether concerns were raised early enough;
- whether refusal came late in the process;
- whether public-trust concerns were mixed with political values concerns;
- whether values language was doing procurement work;
- whether similar standards were applied to other suppliers;
- whether MOPAC knew about the earlier pilot and did not intervene;
- whether it left the Met enough time to correct the process;
- whether it relied on procurement concerns that were not clearly documented before refusal;
- and whether its public reasoning matched its internal reasoning.

This does not mean MOPAC was wrong to refuse.

It means democratic oversight is not immune from audit.

If the office says it acted as the grown-up in the room, the public can ask where the grown-up notes are.

## 🧾 Shared Records Needed

Several categories of records may matter to all three sides.

These include:

- procurement strategy documents;
- approval-route documents;
- scheme-of-delegation records;
- market engagement records;
- supplier comparison records;
- framework procurement records;
- value-for-money assessments;
- operational-need assessments;
- pilot evaluation documents;
- user feedback;
- board papers;
- meeting minutes;
- emails and correspondence between the Met, Palantir, and MOPAC;
- risk registers;
- legal correspondence;
- DPIAs;
- equality impact assessments;
- ethics review material;
- staff-data assessments;
- information-governance reviews;
- public-safety assessments;
- budget and workforce modelling;
- records of mitigations after refusal;
- and any later court filings, settlement documents, consent orders, or judgments.

The public-interest question is not whether every line becomes public.

The question is whether the existence, category, governance route, and conclusions of these records can be accounted for.

A redacted file can still reveal whether the work was done.

An absent file reveals something else.

## 🛠️ Worker-Facing Disclosure

The worker-facing disclosure question should not be lost inside the commercial fight.

If Met staff were part of an earlier pilot, internal standards tool, corruption-detection system, misconduct analysis, or staff-risk process, then staff-facing records matter.

That may include:

- staff notices;
- privacy notices;
- internal communications;
- union or staff-association briefings;
- categories of staff data processed;
- data-minimisation records;
- proportionality assessments;
- equality and bias assessments;
- false-positive handling;
- human-review rules;
- audit logs;
- access-control rules;
- challenge and correction routes;
- retention periods;
- and records showing whether pilot outputs could affect vetting, discipline, deployment, promotion, trust, or reputation.

A commercial procurement claim may not care about workers as workers.

But the documents may still matter to workers.

That is the opportunity.

Disclosure driven by supplier self-interest may still create accountability routes for staff who were never the legal protagonist.

The case may not be about them.

The records may still belong near them.

---

## 🎭 Public-Safety Disclosure

The public-safety claim requires particular care.

If any actor says refusal affected public safety, officer numbers, operational targets, corruption detection, or service delivery, then the evidence behind that claim matters.

Relevant records may include:

- operational-risk assessments;
- public-safety impact assessments;
- officer-number modelling;
- staff-reduction modelling;
- productivity assumptions;
- expected savings;
- alternative-options analysis;
- mitigation plans;
- post-refusal contingency plans;
- internal caveats;
- and records showing who authorised the public-safety claim.

Public safety may be true.

It may be strategic.

It may be exaggerated.

It may be all three.

The paperwork is how the public tells the difference.

A siren is not an argument.

It is a reason to ask who turned it on.

---

## 🗄️ Sensitive Material And The Locked Cabinet

Some records may not be safely publishable in full.

That is expected.

Sensitive policing technology may involve:

- operational methods;
- intelligence workflows;
- staff investigations;
- corruption detection;
- criminal investigations;
- counterterrorism implications;
- national-security-adjacent material;
- commercial secrets;
- personal data;
- and legal privilege.

But sensitivity should not become a blanket fog machine.

Where full publication is impossible, accountability can still ask for:

- document categories;
- dates;
- decision-makers;
- governance routes;
- summary conclusions;
- redacted versions;
- independent review;
- audit confirmation;
- committee scrutiny;
- regulator access;
- judicial handling;
- or protected disclosure to appropriate oversight bodies.

The public does not need operational blueprints.

The public does need assurance that the blueprints were not drawn on the back of a napkin during a fire drill.

## 🧩 The Contradiction Map

The disclosure triangle is useful because each actor may contradict itself.

### Palantir

Palantir may want to say the contract was ordinary enough to proceed, but important enough that refusal caused major harm.

### The Met

The Met may want to say the procurement was lawful and reversible, but the tool was operationally critical.

### MOPAC

MOPAC may want to say refusal was a clean procurement decision, while also acknowledging public-trust or values concerns around the supplier.

None of those contradictions is automatically fatal.

But each needs explanation.

The point of disclosure is not to punish complexity.

It is to stop complexity being used as a hiding place.

## 🪞 What A Good Record Would Show

A good record would show:

- when the need was identified;
- how the supplier was selected;
- how alternatives were tested;
- what the pilot showed;
- what risks were assessed;
- what approvals were required;
- what concerns were raised;
- when concerns were raised;
- how concerns were answered;
- what staff-facing safeguards existed;
- how public-safety claims were calculated;
- why refusal happened when it did;
- and what happened after refusal.

A good record may still support disagreement.

That is fine.

Public accountability does not require all actors to agree.

It requires the disagreement to have a traceable shape.

The problem is not conflict.

The problem is conflict with no audit trail.

---

## 🚩 What This Node Does Not Demand

This node does not demand:

- publication of live operational methods;
- exposure of sensitive investigations;
- reckless disclosure of staff personal data;
- disclosure of privileged legal advice;
- publication of commercially sensitive material without proper handling;
- or simplification of a complex procurement dispute into one guilty actor.

This node demands something narrower.

Each actor should be expected to evidence the claims it is already making.

If you say unfairness, show process.

If you say public safety, show risk.

If you say value for money, show analysis.

If you say oversight, show timeline.

If you say staff safeguards, show governance.

If you say sensitive, show the lawful route by which someone independent can check.

That is not radical.

That is what public power should have expected before the ring started glowing.
