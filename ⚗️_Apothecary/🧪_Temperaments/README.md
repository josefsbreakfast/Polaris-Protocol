# 🧪 Temperaments  
**First created:** 2026-05-12 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-15  

*An examination of recurring online behavioural expressions, social signalling patterns, and collective interaction styles produced within algorithmically mediated environments.*  

---

> “Platforms do not merely host behaviour.  
> They cultivate styles of behaving.”  

---

## 🛰️ Orientation  

The **Temperaments** cluster examines recurring behavioural expressions commonly observed within digital ecosystems.  

Where **Constitutions** describe enduring predispositions and **Vapours** describe collective atmospheres, temperaments describe:

> the visible styles of interaction that emerge between people, platforms, and informational conditions.  

These behaviours are not treated as purely individual personality traits.  

Instead, they are understood as adaptive responses shaped by:

- platform architecture,
- social reward systems,
- visibility incentives,
- emotional contagion,
- memetic circulation,
- and algorithmic amplification.  

Many temperaments become widespread not because they are psychologically healthy or socially constructive, but because they are:

- highly transmissible,
- emotionally activating,
- engagement-efficient,
- or structurally rewarded by platforms themselves.  

Temperaments may emerge as:

- collective escalation dynamics,
- adaptive coping behaviours,
- symbolic communication systems,
- reputational management strategies,
- or visibility-conditioned interaction styles.  

The cluster therefore studies:

- how people behave online,
- why those behaviours spread,
- and what kinds of systems incentivise them.  

---

## 🧠 Core Principles  

### 🌡️ Behaviour Is Environmentally Conditioned  

Digital behaviour does not emerge in a vacuum.  

Interface structures shape:

- pacing,
- escalation,
- attention,
- visibility,
- social reward,
- and emotional tone.  

People often adapt behaviour unconsciously to whatever produces:

- recognition,
- safety,
- engagement,
- or belonging within a given platform ecology.  

---

### 🪞 Temperaments Are Contagious  

Many online behaviours spread memetically through:

- imitation,
- algorithmic visibility,
- social modelling,
- and emotional synchronisation.  

Users often absorb behavioural norms long before consciously evaluating them.  

---

### ⚙️ Platforms Reward Particular Behaviours  

Modern systems frequently amplify behaviours which generate:

- interaction,
- emotional activation,
- circulation,
- and prolonged engagement.  

This means highly visible behaviours are not always the healthiest, wisest, or most representative ones.  

---

### 📡 Behaviour Can Become Infrastructure  

Repeated interaction styles eventually shape:

- platform culture,
- emotional atmospheres,
- social expectations,
- and informational trust systems.  

Behaviour therefore contributes to the construction of informational reality itself.  

---

## ⚔️ Behavioural Dynamics  

Temperaments often emerge through interaction between:

- constitutions,
- vapours,
- and platform incentives.  

For example:

- outrage atmospheres may intensify dogpiling,
- performative reward systems may encourage irony posting,
- paranoia climates may increase swarm behaviour,
- and passive drift environments may normalise lurking cultures.  

The same behaviour may therefore function differently depending upon the surrounding atmosphere.  

---

## 🕸️ Escalation And Visibility  

Many online temperaments are visibility-efficient because they produce:

- emotional clarity,
- rapid recognisability,
- factional signalling,
- and memetic compression.  

As a result, behaviours such as:

- brigading,
- dogpiling,
- irony posting,
- and swarm mobilisation  

often spread more effectively than slower forms of dialogue.  

This creates environments where:

> behavioural intensity becomes socially legible faster than nuance.

## ✨ Key Themes  

### 🧠 Online Behaviour Is Often Adaptive  

Many behaviours that appear irrational or hostile may function adaptively within specific informational environments.  

Examples include:

- irony as reputational protection,
- lurking as risk minimisation,
- dogpiling as coalition signalling,
- doomscrolling as uncertainty management,
- or meme usage as compressed identity communication.  

Some behaviours function primarily as:

- self-protection,
- uncertainty management,
- emotional regulation,
- or adaptation to high-visibility environments.  

Understanding the function of a behaviour is often more useful than simply condemning it.  

---

### 🌡️ Visibility Distorts Norms  

Highly visible behaviours often appear more socially dominant than they truly are.  

This creates feedback loops where users increasingly imitate:

- emotionally intense,
- performative,
- or adversarial behaviours  

because those behaviours appear culturally normative.  

---

### 🪞 Memes Function Socially As Well As Informationally  

Memes are not merely jokes or entertainment.  

They often function as:

- identity markers,
- coalition signals,
- emotional regulation systems,
- ideological shorthand,
- and social sorting mechanisms.  

---

### 🧴 Behavioural Recovery Requires Environmental Change  

Individuals often struggle to sustain healthier interaction styles inside systems structurally optimised for:

- speed,
- outrage,
- virality,
- and continuous visibility competition.  

Meaningful behavioural change therefore frequently requires:

- pacing shifts,
- altered incentives,
- friction,
- and environmental redesign.
