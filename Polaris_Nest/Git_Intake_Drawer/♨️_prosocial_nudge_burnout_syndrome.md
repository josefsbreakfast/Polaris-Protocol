## How over-nudging happens
Nudges are designed as gentle behavioural prompts. However, they rely on the assumption that the target will adjust easily. The people who adjust first become the â€œproof of concept,â€ and institutions slowly begin to depend on them. Over time, these individuals are subjected to more prompts, more responsibility, and higher expectations. Their compliance becomes a resource.

## Concentration of behavioural pressure
Because antisocial or resistant individuals ignore nudges, systems place increasing behavioural weight on those who respond. This leads to disproportionate demands: an empathic worker calming down every meeting, a considerate housemate absorbing all domestic friction, or a socially conscious citizen modifying behaviour to compensate for others.

## The burnout trajectory
The early stages look like accommodation, then depletion, then emotional shutdown. Prosocial people often feel ashamed of burnout because they believe they â€œshould be able to handle it.â€ When they finally reach a limit, their withdrawal or frustration is interpreted as instability or unreliability rather than exhaustion.

## Misinterpretation by systems
Once burnout begins, institutions may mistake it for non-compliance or increased risk. In stressed systems, emotional fatigue can be escalated instead of supported. This traps the individual in a cycle where the very traits that made them responsive become grounds for further intervention or scrutiny.

## Why over-nudging is dangerous
Over-nudging erodes wellbeing, removes autonomy, and creates a quiet moral injury. It teaches people that cooperation leads to exploitation. At scale, this produces disengagement: those who once upheld the system lose trust in it. For public services, this represents a loss of social capital and relational glue.

## A predictable outcome, not an accident
Burnout in this pattern is not personal weakness. It is a predictable outcome of an imbalanced behavioural governance design. Systems strain the most compliant individuals until they cannot continue, then misinterpret the collapse as a behavioural issue rather than exhaustion.
