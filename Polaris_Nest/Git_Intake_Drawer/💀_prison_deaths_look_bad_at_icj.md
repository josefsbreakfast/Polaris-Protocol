### Genocide Law Is About Prevention, Not Intent  
Under settled ICJ jurisprudence (notably *Bosnia and Herzegovina v Serbia and Montenegro*), responsibility for genocide-related breaches does **not** require intent to destroy a group.

It requires:
- Knowledge of a serious risk  
- Capacity to influence or restrain  
- Failure to act with due diligence  

Credibility in prevention therefore matters.

---

### Domestic Conduct as Credibility Evidence  
When a state claims internationally that it:
- Takes civilian death seriously  
- Acts early to prevent foreseeable harm  
- Prioritises life under security pressure  

its **domestic conduct under stress becomes probative**.

If, contemporaneously, the same state:
- Knows a prisoner faces a serious risk of death  
- Receives medical advice to intervene  
- Has the capacity to prevent harm  
- And chooses restraint or delay  

then its assurances of preventive vigilance weaken.

---

### Risk Acceptance as a Pattern  
The issue is not a single death. It is **decision logic**.

Where a state demonstrates that:
- Lethal risk is tolerated when politically costly  
- Fallout is managed rather than harm prevented  
- Oversight is resisted once narratives harden  

opposing parties can argue that the state normalises preventable death under pressure.

That argument directly undermines claims of good faith in genocide-prevention contexts.

---

### Why the ICJ Notices This  
The ICJ does not rule in a vacuum. It evaluates:
- Whether a state *acts like* a prevention-oriented actor  
- Whether assurances are borne out by behaviour  
- Whether â€œwe would intervene if risk were realâ€ is credible  

A state that accepts foreseeable death in custody provides **contemporary counter-evidence** against its own claims.

---

### What This Does *Not* Do  
- It does **not** establish genocide liability  
- It does **not** prove complicity  
- It does **not** substitute for factual findings in the principal case  

It **does**:
- Weaken evidentiary posture  
- Undermine moral authority  
- Strengthen arguments of selective restraint  
- Support claims of prevention failure as a structural issue
