What ‚Äúprosociality‚Äù means in this context
Prosocial individuals tend to show higher empathy, stronger emotional attunement, higher trust in relationships, motivation to maintain group cohesion, and an internal sense of responsibility for the wellbeing of others. In practice, these traits map onto neural systems responsible for bonding, cooperation, moral emotions, and norm alignment.

Why this makes influence easier
Prosociality activates brain systems that are receptive to subtle social cues: mirror neurons, oxytocin pathways, prefrontal norm-tracking, and emotional-safety networks. These systems evolved to support cooperation but also make externally provided guidance feel natural, expected, or morally desirable. A nudge works by triggering a small version of ‚Äúalign with the group‚Äù behaviour. Prosocial people respond faster and more fully.

The asymmetry of influence
Nudging theory assumes small behavioural cues affect everyone evenly. In reality, it works disproportionately on people who already care. Prosocial individuals internalise expectations, seek harmony, avoid conflict, and respond to relational signals. People with low empathy or high antagonism simply do not change behaviour in the same way. This creates an extraction bias: the cooperative carry the weight of adaptation.

Consequences at scale
A governance system relying on nudges unintentionally pressures prosocial citizens while leaving antisocial behaviour untouched. This leads to over-compliance, emotional burnout, guilt loops, and people feeling responsible for systemic failures. It also makes institutional systems appear more effective than they are, because the most responsive group produces the behavioural change outcomes.

Why it belongs in a counter-nudge toolkit
Prosociality is usually framed as a strength, but in behavioural-governance contexts it becomes a predictable vulnerability. This node supports recognition, boundarying, and informed refusal. It also helps explain wider harms when nudging regimes are overused in stressed or collapsing public services.
