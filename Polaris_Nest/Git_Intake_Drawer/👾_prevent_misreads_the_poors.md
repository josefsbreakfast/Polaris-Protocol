## Key Features
- Economic crises increase visible distress, anger, and instability.
- Overstretched services escalate more because they fear liability.
- Staff under strain rely on templates instead of context.
- PREVENT misfires more when poverty increases.

---

## Economic stress as a behavioural amplifier
During a cost-of-living crisis, people display more:
- irritability
- emotional volatility
- fear
- shutdown
- sleep disruption
- hypervigilance
- conflict spillover
These are normal reactions to instability. PREVENT frameworks, however, treat behavioural shifts as potential early indicators of radicalisation, making economic distress a functional risk multiplier.

## Institutional panic under austerity
Austerity reduces staffing, training, and time. When institutions fear complaints, oversight failures, or missed warning signs, they default to over-escalation. PREVENT becomes a catch-all for behaviour that staff canâ€™t contextualise due to lack of capacity. â€œBetter safe than sorryâ€ becomes a coping mechanism rather than a safeguarding strategy.

## Why misreads increase
- Staff lack time to build rapport.
- Distress looks like risk under pressure.
- ND traits become harder to recognise.
- Emotional expression becomes less tolerated.
- Institutional fear becomes the dominant logic.
PREVENT thrives in environments where fear replaces clarity.

## Escalation as harm recycling
Economic strain also increases contact with health, housing, welfare, or social-care systems. Each of these systems is fragile. When distress enters multiple services simultaneously, it accumulates interpretation errors. A minor concern in one setting becomes a justification for escalation in another. PREVENT absorbs the accumulated noise.

## Why certain groups are hit hardest
During crises, the following groups are more likely to be misinterpreted:
- low-income individuals
- racialised communities
- autistic and ADHD adults
- women absorbing emotional labour
- the chronically ill
- people in unsafe housing
- victims of coercive control
These groups already show distress and frustration. Economic stress magnifies these signals and makes them more visible to staff who are themselves under strain.

## Feedback loop between poverty and scrutiny
Poverty increases emotional expression.
Emotional expression increases staff discomfort.
Staff discomfort triggers PREVENT escalation.
PREVENT escalation worsens instability.
Instability deepens poverty.
This creates a reinforcing loop where living conditions and scrutiny become mutually amplifying.

## Systemic consequences
PREVENT loses legitimacy when it becomes a mechanism for managing the fallout of economic collapse. Genuine safeguarding signals drown in a sea of poverty-driven â€œconcerns.â€ Communities lose trust in institutions. Systems become more defensive, not more effective.

## Why this matters
If PREVENT is to serve any constructive role, it cannot absorb the ambient distress created by a national economic crisis. Understanding escalation patterns in collapsing services is essential for reform and for protecting vulnerable individuals from harm disguised as safeguarding.
