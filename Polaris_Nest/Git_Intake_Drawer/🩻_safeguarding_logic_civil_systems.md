## Overview

UK safeguarding does *not* treat harm as a matter of intent.  
It treats harm as:

- **pattern**  
- **impact**  
- **foreseeability**  
- **preventability**  

In everyday language, the terms **abuse** and **neglect** are used narrowly.  
But in safeguarding law and practice, these terms are broad categories capturing:

- acts  
- omissions  
- system failures  
- risk architecture  
- structural pressures

## 1. Abuse: Pattern, Not Motive

In UK safeguarding logic:

> **Abuse = behaviour or pattern that causes harm,  
> regardless of intent or personal motive.**

Key features include:

- unpredictability  
- coercive dynamics  
- contradiction  
- fear-driven behavioural adaptation  
- pressure escalation  
- harmful power imbalance  
- punitive or arbitrary outcomes  

Whether the cause is a person or a disjointed bureaucracy,  
if the pattern matches, safeguarding recognises it as *abuse-like*.

## 2. Neglect: When Needs Are Not Met

Neglect is:

> **the omission of necessary care, support, or attention,  
> resulting in foreseeable harm.**

Again, intent is irrelevant.

Neglect may arise from:

- poor communication between institutions  
- administrative silence  
- procedural delays  
- withheld access to essential services  
- contradictory instructions  
- chronic failure to respond  
- system latency loops  

Structural neglect creates real-world risk:

- deterioration  
- loss of autonomy  
- medical harm  
- financial harm  
- destabilisation of living conditions  
- psychological injury
