Harm reduction is often treated as a moral shortcut: if an intervention lowers aggregate harm, it is presumed justified. This node challenges that assumption by asking where harm reduction **fails as an ethical framework**, not because it is ineffective, but because it collides with consent, personhood, and irreversible power asymmetries.

The focus here is not hypothetical disgust or technological novelty, but **boundary conditions**. Some harms are not meaningfully reducible without being displaced onto those with the least capacity to refuse, resist, or recover. In such cases, optimisation risks becoming laundering: harm is rendered quieter, less visible, or statistically improved, while its moral cost is redistributed rather than resolved.

This node treats extreme cases as analytically necessary, not sensational. Edge conditions expose where doctrines break, where metrics become unreliable, and where governance relies on silence rather than consent. If harm reduction cannot survive scrutiny at the limit, it will fail invisibly in the middle.
