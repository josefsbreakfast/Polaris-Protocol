Why gender matters in behavioural influence
Women are often socialised into attunement, de-escalation, emotional smoothing, and responsibility for group harmony. These behaviours are learned, rewarded, and reinforced. They map onto neural systems for empathy, relational safety, and threat calibration. A nudge is a miniature social request; women are disproportionately trained to respond to such requests.

Why nudges hit harder
A nudge is designed to avoid confrontation and evoke cooperation. Women, especially those carrying chronic emotional labour, respond to this because ignoring a relational cue feels socially unsafe. This is not weakness; it is the accumulated effect of cultural expectations. Antisocial or aggressive individuals ignore nudges entirely; women and caregivers adapt.

Structural consequences
Because women respond more consistently, public systems lean on them disproportionately. They adjust earlier, comply faster, resolve tensions, and modify behaviour even when they are not the cause of systemic harm. Why this is a Stepford mechanism
The Stepford reference captures the pattern: compliant behaviour produced not by innate docility, but by coercive expectation, invisible labour, and subtle fear of social penalty. A nudge cannot force a change in those who resist, so it exerts more force on those trained to care. The system then misreads compliance as success, entrenching the inequity.
