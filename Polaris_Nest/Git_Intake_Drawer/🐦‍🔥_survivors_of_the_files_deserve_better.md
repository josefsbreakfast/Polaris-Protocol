### 1. Files Are Not Neutral  
Files do not exist outside human impact.

When material concerning sexual violence and exploitation is released:
- survivors are re-exposed,
- memories are reactivated,
- safety is destabilised,
- and private pain becomes public property.

Treating files as neutral artefacts erases the people whose lives they describe.

---

### 2. Exposure Without Support Is Harm  
In trauma-informed practice, exposure requires:
- preparation,
- consent,
- pacing,
- and aftercare.

Public disclosure regimes provide none of these.

Survivors are expected to:
- manage their reactions alone,
- absorb renewed scrutiny,
- and tolerate speculation about their lives.

This is not transparency.  
It is abandonment.

---

### 3. Survivors Are Not Evidence Repositories  
Survivors are repeatedly asked to:
- clarify gaps created by redaction,
- correct public misunderstanding,
- defend obvious harm,
- and explain why minimisation is retraumatising.

This turns survivors into unpaid interpreters of institutional failure.

They are not archives.  
They are people.

---

### 4. Justice Delayed Is Trauma Extended  
When disclosures are staggered, partial, or endlessly contextualised:
- closure is withheld,
- hope is prolonged,
- and stress becomes chronic.

For survivors, this is not abstract delay.  
It is lived time â€” years of unresolved harm.

Justice that never arrives still does damage along the way.

---

### 5. Survivors Deserve Sovereignty  
Survivor sovereignty means:
- having a say in how harm is disclosed,
- being protected from unnecessary re-exposure,
- being consulted rather than bypassed,
- and having agency respected over institutional convenience.

Current practices repeatedly violate this principle.

Survivors are spoken about, not with.

---

### 6. Dignity Is the Minimum Standard  
Survivors do not need:
- voyeuristic attention,
- moral theatre,
- or symbolic transparency.

They need:
- clear acknowledgment of harm,
- accountability proportionate to that harm,
- safeguards against further injury,
- and systems that do not demand endless resilience.

Dignity is not a reward.  
It is the baseline.

Survivors of the files deserve:
- trauma-informed disclosure standards,

- legal systems that do not confuse ambiguity with fairness,

Anything less is failure dressed as procedure.
