### 1. The myth of precision  

Conventional threat models assume attackers must:
- identify a specific individual,
- access protected systems,
- obtain confidential data,
- act with high confidence.

In reality, **most harm does not require correctness**â€”only *plausibility*.

### 2. How probabilistic targeting works  

Probabilistic targeting typically follows this pattern:

1. **Inference**  
   Use metadata, timing, behaviour, or contextual clues to identify a *likely* subject pool.

2. **Narrowing**  
   Apply geographic, institutional, or social filters to reduce uncertainty.

3. **Light Contact or Pressure**  
   Introduce:
   - gossip
   - intimidation
   - reputational hints
   - unsolicited contact
   - social friction
   - indirect signalling

4. **System Amplification**  
   Allow trauma, fear, and institutional delay to magnify the effect.

At no stage is confirmation required.

### 5. Plausible deniability by design  

Because targeting is probabilistic:

- mistakes are invisible  
- false positives appear incidental  
- no single action is decisive  
- intent is difficult to prove  
- responsibility diffuses  

This makes the strategy:
- legally low-risk,
- socially deniable,
- operationally resilient.

The absence of certainty protects the actor.

### 6. Why individual hacking is no longer optimal  

Compared to hacking, probabilistic targeting is:

- cheaper  
- faster  
- scalable  
- harder to attribute  
- less technically demanding  
- less likely to trigger incident response  

As systems become more complex and leaky, **precision becomes unnecessary**.

- increased attrition  
- uneven justice outcomes  
- community-level fear  
- normalisation of silence  

The system appears functional while quietly failing its most vulnerable users.
