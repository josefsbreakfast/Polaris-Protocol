### 1) What is a data shadow
A data shadow is the accumulated byproduct of:
- indexing,
- scraping,
- mirroring,
- aggregation,
- and automated inference.

It is not authored.
It is assembled.

Data shadows are often:
- incomplete,
- out of date,
- context-free,
- and structurally sticky.

They persist because systems optimise for recall, not accuracy.

---

### 2) Identity residue
Identity residue refers to fragments of meaning that remain attached to
a personâ€™s name or identifiers even after the originating material is gone.

This can include:
- outdated descriptors,
- hostile language,
- threat-adjacent terms,
- false associations,
- or historical misclassifications.

Residue does not require belief.
It only requires replication.

---

### 3) Detachment from the person
Once identity fragments circulate independently, they no longer require
the person they reference to participate.

At this point:
- correction becomes local,
- persistence becomes global,
- and the burden of interpretation shifts to the individual encountering it.

The person becomes a *reader* of their own shadow.

---

### 4) Why data shadows resist erasure
Data shadows persist because:
- systems are federated, not unified,
- deletion does not cascade,
- caches outlive sources,
- mirrors replicate mirrors,
- and automated content generators reassemble fragments.

This produces a condition where:
removal â‰  disappearance.

### 5) Lived effects
For the affected person, identity residue produces:
- repeated encounters with outdated or hostile representations,
- difficulty establishing narrative control,
- exhaustion from constant correction,
- and uncertainty about what others are seeing.

The harm is not always dramatic.
It is cumulative.

---

### 6) Relationship to algorithmic violence
Data shadows are a core substrate of algorithmic violence.

They provide the material that:
- ranking systems elevate,
- classifiers misinterpret,
- and replication engines reproduce.

Without data shadows, many forms of ambient harm could not persist.
