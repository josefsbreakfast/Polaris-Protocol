## ğŸ§¿ Analysis  

### 1. The Two-Layer Problem  

Most document portals operate with at least two text layers:

1. **Display layer**  
   - OCR text embedded in PDFs  
   - Highlightable, copyable, human-readable  

2. **Index layer**  
   - Tokenised, filtered text used by search  
   - Subject to stop-lists, masking, and partial rebuilds  

If a token never enters layer (2), it is **effectively invisible**, despite being present.

This produces the illusion of absence without altering the record.

---

### 2. Why This Happens (Usually Not Malice)  

In bulk releases, especially retroactive unsealing:

- OCR is re-run without reindexing  
- Sensitivity filters over-fire  
- Tokenisation fails on names, spacing, or scan artefacts  
- Index rebuilds are skipped for time or cost  

The result is common, boring, and dangerous to interpretation.

---

### 3. Why Indexing Is the Soft Failure Point  

Indexing failures are:

- Invisible to users  
- Plausibly deniable  
- Non-evidentiary  
- Rarely audited  

They do not remove information â€” they **remove discoverability**.

This disproportionately benefits anyone relying on:  
> â€œIf it doesnâ€™t appear in search, it isnâ€™t there.â€

That assumption is false.

---

### 4. Reindexing as a Countermeasure  

The fix is straightforward:

1. Download the full corpus  
2. Run independent OCR  
3. Extract raw text  
4. Build a local index (classical or AI-assisted)  

This restores:
- Token completeness  
- Transparent search logic  
- Reproducibility  

And it collapses the gap between disclosure and access.

---

### 5. Methodological Integrity  

To remain defensible:

- Preserve original PDFs  
- Cite page numbers, not search hits  
- Treat OCR output as derived, not authoritative  
- Separate text recovery from interpretation  

Reindexing improves access â€” it does not change evidence.
