## üõ∞Ô∏è Orientation  

Carl Schmitt famously argued that sovereignty reveals itself not in the rule, but in the **exception** ‚Äî the moment when law is suspended in the name of necessity.

In authoritarian contexts, this logic is not introduced as theory.  
It is imposed **through practice**.

This node documents how policing institutions are pressured into enacting Schmittian logic operationally: enforcing law in ways that quietly teach officers that **power, not legality, is what ultimately decides**.

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è Law Collapsing into Power Under Pressure  

In healthy systems, law:
- constrains power,
- defines limits,
- and structures accountability.

Under sustained political, social, or coercive pressure, that relationship inverts.

Police are pushed to decide:
- what is enforceable,
- what is ‚Äúmanageable‚Äù,
- what can be safely ignored.

Law becomes conditional ‚Äî applied only where power permits.

This is not ideological conversion.
It is institutional survival logic.

---

## üß® The Exception Becoming Routine  

Authoritarian pressure rarely announces itself as permanent.

Instead:
- emergencies are extended,
- special measures persist,
- temporary discretion hardens into habit.

Officers learn, through repetition, that:
- exceptions are rewarded,
- restraint is risky,
- and escalation resolves uncertainty faster than legality.

What was once extraordinary becomes **baseline practice**.

---

## üî• Intimidation, Escalation, and Obstruction  

Schmittian collapse is accelerated through behavioural coercion, including:

- **Procedural intimidation**  
  Serial complaints, legal threats, and reputational pressure designed to create hesitation.

- **Strategic escalation**  
  Becoming maximally difficult when challenged: contesting every step, demanding resources, provoking confrontation.

- **Operational obstruction**  
  Making enforcement so costly that avoidance appears rational.

Over time, policing institutions internalise the lesson:
> Enforcing the law against certain actors is more dangerous than not enforcing it.

## üìú Jim Crow as Coercive Precedent  

Jim Crow‚Äìera policing demonstrates this logic clearly.

Even where individual officers were not ideologically aligned:
- armed white supremacist violence,
- credible threats of reprisal,
- and community-wide intimidation

made lawful enforcement **personally and institutionally dangerous**.

The result was not simply racist policing by belief, but **policing overpowered by coercive social force**.

Law existed on paper.
Power decided in practice.

---

## ü™ñ ‚ÄúOut-Policing‚Äù the Police  

Policing depends on a core premise:
- the state can ultimately **subdue** those who break the law.

Authoritarian-aligned actors undermine this by:
- over-arming,
- over-escalating,
- embedding themselves in networks that make enforcement prohibitively costly.

They do not need to win every encounter.
They only need to make subdual **untenable**.

In effect, they **out-police the police**.

---

## üß† Learning Curve ‚Üí Doctrine  

Importantly, this process often begins unintentionally.

Behaviour that starts as:
- entitlement,
- aggression,
- contempt for process,

is reinforced by institutional avoidance.

Actors learn:
- intimidation works,
- escalation pays,
- law bends under pressure.

What begins as impulse becomes **coercive strategy**.

## üö® Diagnostic Signals  

Indicators that Schmittian logic is taking hold include:
- enforcement justified by ‚Äúwhat we can manage‚Äù rather than law,
- selective hesitation around specific actors,
- symbolic enforcement against low-risk targets,
- officers expressing fear of enforcing the law equally.

This is the threshold where legality has yielded to power.
