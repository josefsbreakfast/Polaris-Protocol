### 1. The obsolete threat model  

Traditional justice-sector security assumes attackers are:
- technically sophisticated  
- externally hostile  
- attempting unauthorised access  
- operating at identifiable breach points  

This model fails to capture contemporary risk.

Most meaningful harm now arises from:
- authorised access used loosely  
- routine workflows misaligned with sensitivity  
- metadata interpretation rather than file access  
- human curiosity and gossip  
- commercial data availability  

The barrier to entry is low.

- has access to commercial surveillance data  
- knows where institutions intersect  

No hacking is required.  
No system needs to be breached.

Competence is social and contextual, not technical.

### 4. Scaling without coordination

### 5. Why sexual offence cases are especially vulnerable  

Rape cases involve:
- intense stigma  
- personal risk perception  
- reliance on survivor endurance  
- long investigative timelines  
- low baseline conviction rates  

This means:
> **The system amplifies interference instead of absorbing it.**

Low-skill actions can therefore have **outsize consequences**.
