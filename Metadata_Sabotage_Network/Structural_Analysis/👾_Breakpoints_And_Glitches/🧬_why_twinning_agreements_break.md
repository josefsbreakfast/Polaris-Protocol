### 1. Consent Exists on a Spectrum (Not a Switch)

Twinning systems often treat consent as a **single historical event** (â€œyou agreedâ€) rather than a **continuing condition**.

In practice, at least five consent states commonly coexist:

- **Explicit consent:** Clear, informed, context-appropriate agreement.  
- **Implied or assumed consent:** Participation inferred from proximity, silence, or prior behaviour.  
- **Partial consent:** Agreement to one use, timeframe, or pairingâ€”but not others.  
- **Expired consent:** Consent given under conditions that no longer apply.  
- **Context-invalid consent:** Consent obtained without disclosure of the *actual operational environment*.

Breakage most often occurs in the last two categories.

Consent that is not **contextually applied** is not valid consent, even if it once existed.

Participants may agree to twinning without:

### 3. Temporal Drift and Scope Creep

Twinning arrangements tend to expand along three axes:

- **Time:** Short-term linkage becomes persistent or archival  
- **Scope:** One dataset or narrative becomes a bundle  
- **Audience:** Internal use becomes external visibility  

Even when each expansion appears minor in isolation, their combined effect produces a **material change in conditions**â€”often without a fresh consent checkpoint.

This is where ethical recoil typically begins.

### 4. Algorithmic Lock-In and Asymmetric Risk

Many twinning systems embed **continuation bias**:

- Rewards for staying linked  
- Friction or penalty for withdrawal  
- Obscured exit pathways  
- Narrative framing of withdrawal as hostility or instability  

Risk, however, is rarely symmetric.

One party may absorb:
- Disproportionate reputational damage  
- Legal or regulatory exposure  
- Long-tail inference risks  
- Identity or safety impacts  

As asymmetry becomes legible, attempts to disengage increase.
