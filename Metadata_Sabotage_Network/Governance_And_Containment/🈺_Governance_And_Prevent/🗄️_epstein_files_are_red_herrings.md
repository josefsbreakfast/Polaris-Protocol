### 1. Red Herring Transparency  
A red herring does not deny reality; it **rearranges attention**.

The Epstein files confirm what was already broadly known:
- that Epstein abused children,
- that **Ghislaine Maxwell** facilitated,
- that **Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor (aka "Prince" Andrew)** remains implicated.

What they do *not* meaningfully illuminate:
- the full client base,
- the scale and evolution of victimisation,
- why safeguarding systems failed,
- or how demand was sustained over time.

The result is informational activity without epistemic movement.

### 2. Familiar Villains, Narrow Blast Radius  
The files repeatedly centre a small set of already-scrutinised individuals, creating the impression of exhaustiveness.

This has a stabilising effect:
- outrage is absorbed by known targets,
- institutional and peer networks remain diffuse,
- scrutiny does not propagate laterally.

A network crime is narratively collapsed into a character crime.
