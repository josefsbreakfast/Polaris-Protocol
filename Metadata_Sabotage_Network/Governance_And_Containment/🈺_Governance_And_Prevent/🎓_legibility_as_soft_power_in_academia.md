### Governance under reputational constraint
Academic institutions are reputationally sensitive and networked. They operate within environments where:
- colleagues may talk informally
- future collaborations are possible
- regulatory decisions may be reviewed
- mishandling visible cases carries downstream risk

When a person becomes legible as a serious actor, governance decisions are more likely to prioritise accuracy and containment over dismissal or stonewalling.

---

### Containment through accuracy
In multi-controller environments, clarity becomes a containment strategy.

Institutions often prefer to:
- acknowledge narrow, local errors
- correct limited compliance failures
- document scope precisely

rather than risk being seen as responsible for broader, systemic harm. Legible counterparts make this strategy viable by asking precise questions and demonstrating understanding of system boundaries.

---

### Why this is often misread
Because these shifts are informal and unspoken, they are frequently misinterpreted as:
- favouritism
- influence
- networking advantage
- implicit advocacy

In reality, they reflect standard institutional behaviour under uncertainty and reputational exposure. Legibility alters perceived risk, not entitlement.
