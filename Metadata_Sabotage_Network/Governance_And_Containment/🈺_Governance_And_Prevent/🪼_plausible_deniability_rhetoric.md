## Orientation
This note explains why anyone working inside or close to PREVENT, safeguarding, or multi-agency risk environments cannot reasonably claim that they did not understand the consequences of escalating someone. PREVENT is not obscure: its effects are well-known within the professions that use it, and its harm patterns are widely recognised. This node documents the structural reasons that ignorance is not credible.

---

## Key Features
- PREVENT is widely understood as escalatory and punitive.
- Practitioners receive mandatory training on consequences.
- Exposure to referral pathways eliminates plausible ignorance.
- Misuse of PREVENT requires deliberate framing or omission of context.

## Why misuse cannot be accidental
A PREVENT referral requires:
- selecting concerning behaviours
- summarising them in a specific tone
- excluding contextual information
- using keywords known to trigger escalation
- invoking risk where none exists
This process involves choices. It is inconsistent with the idea of accidental harm. The practitioner must actively frame the situation.

- ignore professional norms
- ignore previous cases
- ignore institutional gossip
- ignore legal duties
- ignore behavioural templates
This is incompatible with functioning in the role.
