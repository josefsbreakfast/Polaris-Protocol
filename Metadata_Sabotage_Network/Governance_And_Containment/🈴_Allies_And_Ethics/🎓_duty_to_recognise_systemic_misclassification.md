### 1. The Principle of Interpretive Responsibility  
In governance systems, many harms arise not from action, but from **unchallenged interpretation**.

Professionals whose roles involve:
- assessing risk,  
- interpreting metadata,  
- weighing behavioural inference,  
- participating in multi-agency decision-making,

They are not expected to be technically omniscient.

- proportionality,  
- necessity,  
- fairness,  
- least-harm principle,  
- precaution balanced with evidence,  
- protection of vulnerable individuals.

They also rely heavily on:
- inferred risk,  
- incomplete information,  
- cross-system data flows,  
- third-party analytics,  
- automated flagging.

This combination creates **known risk of false positives**.

### 3. Recognition Threshold: What Professionals Should Notice  
A professional exercising reasonable care should recognise red flags such as:

- persistent contradictions across systems,  
- risk markers without plausible behavioural origin,  
- identity instability that defies lived reality,  
- escalation driven by metadata rather than conduct,  
- safeguarding or Prevent concern without grounding evidence,  
- healthcare context flags lacking clinical basis,  
- repeated administrative friction without explanation.

These are not subtle signals.  
They are classic indicators of **systemic misclassification**.
