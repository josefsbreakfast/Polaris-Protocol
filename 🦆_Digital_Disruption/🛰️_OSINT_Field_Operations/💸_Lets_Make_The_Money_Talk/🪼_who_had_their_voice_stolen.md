## What ‚ÄúVoice Stolen‚Äù Means

A voice is ‚Äústolen‚Äù when someone is pushed into:
- silence,

- incoherence,

- institutions remain uncorrected,
- and future whistleblowers learn not to speak.

## Who Commonly Has Their Voice Stolen

This project maps silencing by **role**, because roles leave institutional traces.

Commonly impacted groups include:
- survivors and survivor-advocates,
- journalists and editors,
- institutional whistleblowers,
- compliance and risk professionals,
- academics and researchers,
- junior staff in elite environments,
- and small or precarious organisations caught in reputational blast radius.

These groups are targeted or pressured because they are:
- proximate to records,
- capable of triggering process,
- or able to shape public narrative.

## Mechanisms of Voice Theft

Voice theft commonly occurs through:

- **NDAs and confidentiality regimes**  
  Broad agreements that raise perceived risk of speaking, often beyond what is legally enforceable.

- **SLAPP-style legal threat**  
  Lawsuits or threats designed to impose cost and fear, regardless of ultimate success.

- **Workplace retaliation**  
  Demotions, isolation, ‚Äúperformance‚Äù write-ups, disciplinary proceedings, or forced exits.

- **Funding withdrawal and donor pressure**  
  Financial dependency used to induce self-censorship or organisational collapse.

- **Reputational sabotage**  
  Smears, credibility erosion, targeted harassment, or contamination of a person‚Äôs public image.

- **Procedural attrition**  
  Endless delays, opaque processes, and ‚Äúno outcome‚Äù outcomes that exhaust a person into silence.

Silencing is usually layered.
One mechanism alone rarely needs to succeed.

## What Evidence Can Look Like Without Naming Individuals

Silencing often erases direct evidence of itself.
However, its presence can be inferred through governance signals such as:

- sudden departures clustered around key events,
- unusually heavy or inconsistent redactions,
- shifting institutional justifications over time,
- repeated reliance on legal counsel for basic disclosure,
- patterns of ‚Äúno records found‚Äù in contexts where records should exist,
- institutional pivot away from topics after initial engagement,
- overuse of confidentiality language beyond statutory requirement.

These are signals for **audit priority**, not proof of motive.
