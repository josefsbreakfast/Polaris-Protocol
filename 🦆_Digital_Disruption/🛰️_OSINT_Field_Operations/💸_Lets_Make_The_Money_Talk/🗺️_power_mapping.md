## What Power Mapping Is

Power mapping is the analysis of:
- institutional relationships,
- financial dependencies,
- service ecosystems,
- and incentive structures.

It asks:
- who provides essential services,
- who absorbs risk,
- who gains from delay,
- and who remains insulated when harm is disclosed.

It does not seek hidden truths.  
It organises **visible information** into legible structure.

---

## What Power Mapping Is Not

Power mapping is not:
- identifying perpetrators,
- proving guilt,
- reconstructing crimes,
- or inferring intent.

It does not rely on:
- private communications,
- leaked materials,
- or unverifiable claims.

Absence of conclusion is not failure.  
Resisting speculation is part of the method.

---

## The Central Question: Who Benefits

When direct accountability is obstructed, responsibility becomes diffuse.  
Benefit does not.

Power mapping prioritises:
- material benefit,
- reputational insulation,
- risk offloading,
- and continuity of influence.

The question is not ‚Äúwho knew?‚Äù

## Institutions Over Individuals

This work maps:
- companies,
- regulators,
- service providers,
- financial vehicles,
- and governance bodies.

Private individuals are not the unit of analysis.

This reduces:
- survivor retraumatisation,
- defamation risk,
- and narrative collapse into personality.

Institutions leave records.  
Records enable accountability.

## Common Power Domains

Power commonly concentrates across:
- finance and banking,
- legal and compliance services,
- public relations and crisis management,
- hospitality and transport,
- technology platforms,
- media and publishing,
- academia and philanthropy,
- and regulatory interfaces.

Mapping focuses on **dependency**, not proximity.

---

## Mapping Without Conspiracy

Power mapping does not assume coordination.

Patterns may arise from:
- shared incentives,
- industry norms,
- risk tolerance,
- or structural capture.

Correlation is not proof.  
But repeated correlation signals **audit priority**.

---

## Temporal Mapping

Time matters.

Mapping should track:
- when relationships began,
- when disclosures occurred,
- when contracts changed,
- when narratives shifted,
- and when accountability stalled.

Sequence often reveals more than volume.

---

## Mapping Outputs

Acceptable outputs include:
- timelines,
- relationship diagrams,
- service dependency lists,
- procurement patterns,
- regulatory interaction logs,
- and risk matrices.

Outputs should be:
- reproducible,
- sourced,
- and readable without interpretation.

## Power Mapping as Harm Reduction

Power mapping supports harm reduction by:
- shifting focus away from survivors,
- identifying leverage points,
- and enabling lawful intervention.

It prepares the ground for:
- FOI and SAR requests,
- regulatory scrutiny,
- and financial compliance review.

Mapping precedes action.  
Action without mapping escalates risk.
