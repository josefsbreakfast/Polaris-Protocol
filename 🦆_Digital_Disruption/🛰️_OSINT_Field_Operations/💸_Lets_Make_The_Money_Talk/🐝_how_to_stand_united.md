## The Reality of Division

Contemporary publics are saturated with:
- adversarial framing,
- moral panic,
- and zero-sum narratives.

People are routinely encouraged to:
- dehumanise political opponents,
- treat disagreement as threat,
- and imagine violence as resolution.

This context cannot be ignored.
It must be accounted for.

---

## What a United Front Is

A united front is:
- procedural,
- time-bound,
- and purpose-specific.

It is formed around:
- shared minimum goals,
- agreed constraints,
- and mutual non-aggression.

It does not require ideological alignment.

---

## What a United Front Is Not

A united front is not:
- consensus on worldview,
- loyalty to leaders,
- or suspension of critique.

It does not erase difference.
It brackets difference to prevent mutual destruction.

---

## The Shared Interest

Across political, cultural, and social divides, there is broad shared interest in:
- functional rule of law,
- protection of children,
- accountability for power,
- and reliable public institutions.

These are not partisan goals.
They are civic prerequisites.

---

## Resisting Enemy Construction

Enemy narratives simplify complexity and accelerate harm.

They operate by:
- collapsing institutions into identities,
- personalising systemic failure,
- and rewarding outrage over understanding.

Standing united requires refusing to turn neighbours into enemies.

---

## Anger Without Violence

Anger in response to harm is legitimate.

Violence is not.

This work acknowledges rage while insisting that:
- violence fractures coalitions,
- legitimises repression,
- and destroys the very protections people seek.

Anger can fuel persistence.
It must not dictate action.

---

## Procedural Unity

Unity is maintained through:
- shared principles,
- agreed methods,
- and clear boundaries.

Disagreement is managed by:
- slowing down,
- documenting differences,
- and returning to process.

Procedure prevents fracture.

---

## Refusing Purity Tests

Purity tests divide potential allies.

They demand:
- total alignment,
- public performance,
- or ideological conformity.

A united front survives by:
- tolerating difference,
- rejecting shaming,
- and focusing on outcomes.

---

## Avoiding Scapegoating

When pressure rises, groups often:
- externalise blame,
- target minorities,
- or revive historic prejudices.

Scapegoating is a sign of collapse, not strength.

Unity requires active resistance to:
- racism, islamophobia, and antisemitism,  
- misogyny, homophobia, and transphobia,
- and other displacement tactics.

We don't have to like each other, but we do have to resist being divided.  

Child abuse is child abuse; we might have to tolerate people we don't normally tolerate.  

---

## The Role of Ordinary People

Standing united does not require heroism.

It requires:
- refusing calls to dehumanise,
- maintaining lawful engagement,
- and supporting boring process.

Ordinary restraint is powerful.

---

## Closing

A united front is not forged through agreement.
It is maintained through restraint.

In a moment where division is profitable and violence is encouraged,
standing united is a deliberate act of refusal.
