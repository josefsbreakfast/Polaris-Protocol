## What Is Currently Visible

- entities that have already been taken to court,

## Secrecy as a Structural Factor

Many roles operate under:
- statutory secrecy obligations,
- contractual NDAs,
- political confidentiality rules,
- or security-related restrictions.

These include:
- public servants,
- political party staff,
- contractors,
- consultants,
- and private-sector professionals.

Secrecy laws shape what can surface, not what occurred.

---

## Whistleblowing Constraints

Whistleblowing in high-power environments is:
- legally risky,
- professionally costly,
- and personally dangerous.

Those who:
- refused participation,
- attempted to raise concerns,
- or exited quietly

may have done so under extreme pressure.

Silence may reflect constraint, not complicity.

---

## Soft Power and Informal Pressure

Not all involvement is formal or contractual.

Influence may occur through:
- informal requests,
- favours,
- expectations of loyalty,
- or implicit career leverage.

These mechanisms leave fewer records and are harder to surface through traditional inquiry.

They should not be excluded a priori.

---

## The Problem of Convenient Narratives

Public speculation often clusters around:
- familiar adversaries,
- culturally primed suspicions,
- or historically charged institutions.

Such focus can be:
- politically convenient,
- emotionally satisfying,
- and analytically misleading.

Bias narrows inquiry and protects the unexamined.

---

## Avoiding Antisemitic and Racialised Frames

Attributing systemic abuse primarily to:
- foreign intelligence services,
- ethnic groups,
- or external conspirators

has a long historical association with antisemitic and racialised narratives.

This framing:
- distracts from domestic accountability,
- flatters Western institutions,
- and fragments coalitions.

Structural analysis resists scapegoating.

---

## Inclusion as a Methodological Safeguard

Open-mindedness functions as a safeguard.

It requires:
- testing hypotheses rather than assuming them,
- applying the same questions across institutions,
- and allowing evidence to exclude actors over time.

Exclusion should be earned through analysis, not comfort.

---

## Protecting the Innocent Through Process

Maintaining openness protects:
- individuals coerced into roles,
- institutions wrongly assumed to be immune,
- and those operating in good faith under constraint.

Clear process distinguishes:
- involvement,
- complicity,
- resistance,
- and absence.

Process is protective.
