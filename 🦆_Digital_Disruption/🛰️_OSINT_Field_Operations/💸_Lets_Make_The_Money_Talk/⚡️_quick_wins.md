- stabilising actions,

## What a Quick Win Is

A quick win:
- is lawful,
- is reversible,
- reduces confusion,
- and produces immediate governance signal.

Quick wins improve conditions.
They do not resolve cases.

---

## What a Quick Win Is Not

A quick win is not:
- a public accusation,
- a viral moment,
- a naming exercise,
- or a demand for immediate punishment.

Actions that increase heat are not quick wins.
They are liabilities.

---

## Clarifying Public Records

Simple actions include:
- organising existing documents,
- creating timelines from disclosed material,
- indexing redactions,
- and flagging inconsistencies in public statements.

These improve legibility without interpretation.

---

## Process Documentation

Documenting:
- FOI response times,
- exemption usage,
- review outcomes,
- and regulator engagement

creates immediate accountability artefacts.

Even one well-documented failure is a win.

---

## Correcting Euphemism

Replacing softened language with accurate terms in:
- summaries,
- descriptions,
- and metadata

is a low-risk intervention.

Clarity changes downstream interpretation.

---

## Reducing Survivor Load

Quick wins that protect survivors include:
- redirecting questions toward institutions,
- publishing clear boundaries on engagement,
- and discouraging repetitive survivor commentary requests.

Less pressure is an immediate gain.

---

## Internal Alignment

Agreeing early on:
- scope limits,
- tone standards,
- and escalation thresholds

prevents later fracture.

Alignment saves time.

---

## Visibility Without Virality

Publishing:
- procedural notes,
- methodology explanations,
- or transparency guides

signals seriousness without courting spectacle.

Legibility beats reach.

---

## Knowing When to Stop

A quick win ends when:
- risk increases,
- heat escalates,
- or survivor safety changes.

Stopping early preserves the win.

---

## Closing

Quick wins are not about speed.
They are about removing friction.

Each small improvement makes sustained accountability easier.
