## What good governance actually looks like

Good governance is rarely flashy. It tends to be:
- specific rather than grand  
- cautious rather than confident  
- boring rather than visionary  

Common markers include:
- DPIAs that describe the system accurately, including limits  
- clear statements of what the system is *not* allowed to do  
- named accountability (roles, dates, sign-off)  
- acknowledgement of residual risk rather than denial  
- documentation that matches operational reality  

Nothing here promises perfection.  
That restraint is the point.

Reassurance does real civic work.

- disagreement becomes specific rather than diffuse  
- public trust becomes conditional rather than blind  
- future changes have a baseline to be measured against

- what assumptions were accepted  
- which risks were consciously tolerated  
- how changes would need to be approved

- constrains future drift

## The difference between reassurance and complacency

Reassurance is evidence-based.  
Complacency is unexamined.

Signs of reassurance:
- documentation is current  
- scope is narrow  
- language is precise  
- responses invite follow-up  

Signs of complacency:
- â€œindustry standardâ€ without detail  
- no update history  
- generic language reused across contexts  
- defensiveness rather than clarity  

Compliance that welcomes being read is different from compliance that hopes not to be.
