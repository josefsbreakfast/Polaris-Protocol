This node sets out a **specific, repeatable method** for examining data services delivered under government contracts, using parallel and lawful requests.

- personal data is processed

## What â€œDual DPIAâ€ means in government contexts

In public-sector contracting, responsibility is often split across layers:
- the **public authority** (usually the data controller)
- one or more **private suppliers** (processors or joint controllers)

A *Dual DPIA* approach means:
- asking the **controller** for governance and risk documents  
- asking the **processor** what they supplied or hold  
- and comparing both against lived reality (via SARs, where applicable)

You are not duplicating effort.  
You are cross-validating roles.

## Why government contractors often narrow responses

It is common for contractors â€” and sometimes authorities â€” to:
- interpret requests as narrowly as possible  
- answer only the literal wording, not the intent  
- exclude inferred or derived data unless explicitly named  
- redirect responsibility sideways  

This behaviour is usually:
- risk-averse  
- legally coached  
- process-driven  

It does not automatically indicate wrongdoing.  
It *does* mean clarity matters.

## What should normally be disclosable

Without getting technical, the following are **reasonable to expect**, subject to lawful exemptions:

### From the public authority (FOI)
- confirmation whether a DPIA exists  
- copies of DPIAs or equivalent risk assessments  
- dates, authors, and scope descriptions  
- procurement documents referencing data protection  
- high-level risk registers or assurance summaries  

Even where content is partially withheld, **existence and metadata** are usually disclosable.

---

### From either party (SAR, if you are in scope)
- personal data held about you  
- inferred, derived, or predicted attributes  
- profiles, flags, or risk indicators  
- metadata relating to decision-making or sharing  

You are allowed to name these categories explicitly.

## When responses feel belittling or dismissive

Occasionally, responses may:
- minimise your question  
- suggest misunderstanding on your part  
- imply the request is unnecessary  
- adopt a lecturing or patronising tone  

This is not a signal to escalate immediately.

Often, it reflects:
- internal discomfort  
- unfamiliarity with being asked  
- poorly scripted templates  

The most effective response is usually:
> calm clarification, restating the request, narrowing scope if needed.

Tone mismatches are data, but not emergencies.

---

## When responses cross a line

Very rarely, responses may feel:
- hostile  
- bullying  
- threatening  
- or personally directed  

If this happens:
- pause  
- save the correspondence  
- do not reply while emotionally activated  

You are entitled to:
- professional communication  
- proportionate handling  
- responses free from intimidation  

You do not need to justify your interest.

This folder contains later guidance on options **if** behaviour becomes genuinely inappropriate. You do not need to jump ahead.

---

## How to compare what you receive

Once you have responses:
- place them side by side  
- note differences in scope, timing, and description  
- look for what is missing rather than what is said  

You are looking for:
- consistency  
- clarity  
- alignment between promise and practice  

Most findings are subtle, not dramatic.
