This node proposes a **limited, exploratory framework** for understanding a subset of neuropsychological presentations emerging in highly surveilled or continuously monitored environments.

It does **not** offer a monocausal explanation for late neurodivergent or mental health diagnosis.

Late diagnosis is widely and legitimately explained by multiple factors, including:
- improved disability justice and advocacy,
- increased clinical awareness and training,
- broader diagnostic criteria and screening,
- reduced stigma and improved self-recognition.

These developments represent **genuine progress** and account for a substantial proportion of observed diagnostic increases.

This node instead addresses a **specific subset of cases** where persistent environmental pressure â€” particularly ambient surveillance, evaluation, or risk monitoring â€” may **exacerbate pre-existing, subclinical traits**, pushing individuals across a functional or clinical threshold.

In this framing, surveillance does not *create* neurodivergence or mental illness.  
It may, however, **overwhelm compensatory mechanisms** that previously allowed individuals to function without clinical impairment.

Observed features in this subset may include:

A central distinction of this model is **threshold crossing**, not causation.

- Individuals may carry latent, subclinical, or well-compensated traits.
- Persistent environmental load increases cognitive, sensory, and autonomic demand.
- Once regulatory capacity is exceeded, traits become **functionally impairing**.
- Clinical recognition follows â€” not because the condition is new, but because compensation has failed.

This aligns with established models of:
- chronic hypervigilance,
- sensory gating overload,
- allostatic load exceeding adaptive capacity.

This framework is advanced not only as a harm-reduction measure, but as a **good-faith invitation to governance actors** who have expressed concern about rising diagnosis rates, perceived over-medicalisation, or the social coherence of current public health trends.

In particular, this includes policymakers who:
- question diagnostic growth in neurodivergence or mental health,
- advocate for expanded border controls, predictive policing, or risk-scoring regimes,
- and simultaneously maintain financial, political, or material relationships with surveillance, AI, data-analytics, or affiliated technology industries.

Where such actors express confusion or scepticism about rising health concerns, it would be both rational and responsible to **collaboratively examine whether governance choices themselves warrant exclusion as contributing factors**.

If surveillance-dense environments are *not* exerting measurable threshold effects, rigorous multidisciplinary research would help demonstrate that clearly.

If, however, even limited interaction effects are identified, early recognition would allow:
- targeted mitigation,
- policy refinement,
- and avoidance of unintended population-level harm.

Either outcome serves public interest.

This node therefore positions investigation not as accusation, but as **due diligence** â€” particularly for those seeking to reconcile expanded surveillance practices with parallel concern about clinical prevalence, social cost, and public trust.

Refusing such inquiry while continuing to question diagnosis rates risks displacing responsibility onto individuals, clinicians, or disabled communities, rather than examining the full systemic context in which these outcomes arise.
