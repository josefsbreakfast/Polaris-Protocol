## Purpose

This directory provides a structured, evidence-led examination of the **holistic health consequences** associated with sustained exposure to **high-surveillance environments**.

The term ‚Äúholistic health‚Äù is used deliberately to encompass:
- mental and psychological health,
- physical and physiological health,
- social functioning, trust, and agency.

This folder treats surveillance not solely as a legal, technical, or security issue, but as a **health-relevant environmental condition** with cumulative effects.

---

## Framing Assumption

Persistent surveillance functions as a **chronic stressor**.

Where individuals experience ongoing monitoring, uncertainty about observation, or asymmetric accountability, the health impacts are best understood through **chronic stress and systems-level health models**, rather than single-incident or single-diagnosis frameworks.

The emphasis throughout this directory is on **chronicity, accumulation, and embodiment**, not episodic harm.

---

## Evidentiary Orientation

Materials in this directory draw on established research from adjacent domains, including:

- chronic stress physiology and multisystem health models,
- occupational health and workplace surveillance research,
- public health analyses of policing and carceral exposure,
- psychological research on perceived surveillance and anticipatory behaviour,
- institutional surveillance literature relating to schools and healthcare settings.

Where direct ‚Äúsurveillance and health‚Äù research is limited, **mechanistic and analogical evidence** is used, with assumptions made explicit.

## Core Health Pathways

The following pathways are treated as central across the directory:

- prolonged stress activation and dysregulation,
- sleep disruption and circadian instability,
- sustained hypervigilance and attentional load,
- behavioural suppression and self-regulation (‚Äúchilling effects‚Äù),
- erosion of social trust and perceived safety.

Health impacts are considered **multisystem**, affecting neuroendocrine, cardiovascular, immune, cognitive, and relational domains.

---

## Moderating Factors

Evidence reviewed here consistently indicates that health impact is shaped by context, including:

- transparency and intelligibility of surveillance,
- consent and meaningful choice,
- proportionality and purpose limitation,
- institutional legitimacy and trust,
- whether surveillance is experienced as protective or punitive.

These moderators are treated as analytically significant, not secondary.

This directory is intended to support:

Content is written to withstand **policy, legal, and clinical scrutiny**, with emphasis on precision and reproducibility of claims.

## Scope Notes

This work does not assume:
- universal misuse of surveillance technologies,
- uniform intent across institutions,
- or pathology in individuals reporting distress.

It does assume that:
- environments shape health outcomes,
- power asymmetries matter,
- and cumulative stress effects are predictable and measurable.
