### 1. What Blood Libel Actually Is (Historically)

Blood libel refers to a specific conspiratorial structure first articulated in 12th-century England:

- Jews are accused of **collective, ritualised murder of non-Jews (often children)**  
- The violence is framed as **religiously mandated and secret**  
- Guilt is collective and unfalsifiable  
- Denial is treated as proof of deception  

This template justified pogroms, expulsions, and mass violence across Europe.
It is not a metaphor. It is a historically lethal narrative form.

### 2. Why Britain Is a Special Case

The first fully articulated blood-libel narrative emerged in 1144 in Norwich.
That origin matters.

For British Jewish communities, the term carries:
- local historical memory,
- awareness of how easily accusations metastasise,
- and recognition of a pattern that once had state and church backing.

This history shaped:
- settlement patterns,
- relationships with authority,
- and heightened sensitivity to accusations involving children, sexuality, secrecy, or collective guilt.

The trauma response is **historically conditioned**, not hypersensitive.

### 3. When the Term Is Legitimately Applicable Today

The term is analytically valid **only when modern claims reproduce the same structure**:

- Collective Jewish guilt  
- Ritualised or inherent violence  
- Unfalsifiability  
- Conspiratorial closure  

Absent that structure, use of the term becomes **signalling**, not description.
