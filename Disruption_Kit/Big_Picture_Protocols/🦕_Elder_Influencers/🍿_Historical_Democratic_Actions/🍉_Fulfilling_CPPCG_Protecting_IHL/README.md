## âš–ï¸ The CPPCG: What It Is and What It Requires  

The **Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (CPPCG)** is a binding international treaty adopted in 1948 in the immediate aftermath of the Holocaust.

A core â€” and frequently misunderstood â€” feature of the Convention is that it establishes a **duty to prevent genocide**, not merely to punish it after the fact.

Key points relevant to this folder:

- The obligation to prevent genocide **arises at the point of serious risk**, not after judicial determination.  
- States are required to act **within their capacity and influence**, proportionate to their knowledge and means.  
- The duty applies to **policy, material support, economic activity, diplomatic action, and failure to act**, not only to direct perpetrators.  
- Genocide prevention is not discretionary or symbolic â€” it is a **legal obligation**.  

The CPPCG does not require certainty.  
It requires **reasonable concern and preventative action**.

## ğŸ” Interpretation, Scope, and Role-Dependent Responsibility  

By necessity, the CPPCG is **interpreted differently depending on role, sector, and function**.

The Convention places overlapping â€” but not identical â€” expectations on:

- individuals  
- journalists and civil society  
- academic and cultural institutions  
- businesses and financial actors  
- governments and enforcement bodies  
- the international community as a whole  

What constitutes an appropriate preventative response will differ depending on whether one is:

- organising speech or protest  
- conducting research or teaching  
- providing finance, insurance, or trade  
- setting government policy  
- enforcing domestic law  

People will also reasonably differ in **how urgent or forceful** they believe prevention must be:

- some emphasise documentation, warning, and advocacy  
- others disruption of material support  
- others institutional refusal, withdrawal, or sanction  

These differences are not evidence of extremism.  
They are the **inevitable result of a prevention duty that must operate before certainty exists**.

This is precisely why **transparency and open discussion are essential**.  
Without them, societies cannot agree on proportionality, assess risk coherently, or distinguish disagreement from danger.

## ğŸ•Šï¸ International Humanitarian Law (IHL) and the Geneva Conventions  

**International Humanitarian Law (IHL)** â€” most notably the **Geneva Conventions** â€” governs the conduct of armed conflict and the protection of civilians, prisoners, and the wounded.

Across historical genocides, including the Holocaust, a consistent pattern is observable:

> **IHL is eroded, suspended, or reinterpreted early â€” often before genocide is formally recognised.**

Common early violations include:

- collective punishment  
- dehumanisation or mass criminalisation of civilians  
- denial of medical care or humanitarian access  
- destruction of civilian infrastructure  
- framing civilian populations as inherently complicit  

These violations are not incidental.  
They are **structural preconditions** for mass atrocity.

For this reason, defence of IHL norms is widely understood as **inseparable from genocide prevention itself**.

## ğŸ“˜ Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **CPPCG** | **Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (1948).** A binding international treaty establishing genocide as a crime and imposing a **duty to prevent** genocide when there is a serious risk, not only to punish it after the fact. |
| **Genocide (Legal)** | Acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group, including killing, causing serious harm, inflicting destructive conditions of life, preventing births, or forcibly transferring children. Genocide is understood as a **process**, not a single event. |
| **Duty to Prevent** | The legal obligation under the CPPCG requiring states â€” and actors within their influence â€” to take **reasonable, proportionate measures** to prevent genocide where there is a serious risk, based on capacity and knowledge. |
| **IHL** | **International Humanitarian Law.** The body of international law governing armed conflict, including protection of civilians, the wounded, prisoners of war, and medical personnel. Applies regardless of the legality of the conflict itself. |
| **Geneva Conventions** | A set of treaties forming the core of IHL. They prohibit collective punishment, targeting of civilians, denial of medical care, and other acts historically associated with the early stages of mass atrocity. |
| **Collective Punishment** | The imposition of penalties or harm on a civilian population for acts they did not individually commit. Prohibited under IHL and commonly observed as an early indicator of atrocity escalation. |
| **Proportionality (IHL)** | A principle requiring that civilian harm not be excessive in relation to the concrete and direct military advantage anticipated. Proportionality does **not** legalise otherwise prohibited acts. |
| **IAC / NIAC** | **International Armed Conflict (IAC):** armed conflict between states. **Non-International Armed Conflict (NIAC):** armed conflict within a state involving non-state actors. IHL applies in both contexts, with some rule variation. |
| **Preventative Action** | Action taken **before** genocide is legally determined, including warning, documentation, advocacy, refusal to participate, withdrawal of support, or disruption of material facilitation. Central to genocide prevention. |
| **Securitisation** | The process by which political, moral, or humanitarian issues are reframed as security threats, enabling exceptional measures and narrowing tolerance for dissent. |
| **Counter-Terror Drift** | The gradual expansion of counter-terrorism frameworks into areas of lawful speech, protest, or civil activity due to ambiguity, risk aversion, or precedent anxiety rather than direct threat. |
| **Public Order Offence** | Domestic criminal offences relating to disorder, disturbance, or disruption. Distinct from terrorism offences and historically subject to higher protections for political speech. |
| **IHRA Definition** | A non-binding working definition of antisemitism adopted by some institutions. Criticised for ambiguity and for conflating certain political critiques of Israel with antisemitism, particularly under genocide-prevention stress. |
| **JDA** | **Jerusalem Declaration on Antisemitism.** An alternative definition developed by leading scholars of Holocaust studies, Jewish history, and antisemitism, explicitly distinguishing antisemitism from legitimate political criticism of Israel or Zionism. |
| **Good-Faith Dissent** | Speech or action undertaken with genuine intent to prevent harm, fulfil legal or ethical obligations, or engage in democratic debate, even when disruptive, uncomfortable, or politically inconvenient. |
| **Governance Drift** | A condition in which institutions substitute risk management, narrative protection, or damage control for principled legal or moral judgment, often without explicit intent. |
| **Narrative Stability** | The prioritisation of maintaining a coherent moral or historical self-image (e.g. national myths) over confronting evidence that complicates or destabilises that narrative. |
| **Prevention vs. Punishment** | **Prevention** is forward-looking, precautionary, and necessarily contested. **Punishment** is retrospective, legalistic, and requires certainty. Confusing the two delays action and increases risk of failure. |
