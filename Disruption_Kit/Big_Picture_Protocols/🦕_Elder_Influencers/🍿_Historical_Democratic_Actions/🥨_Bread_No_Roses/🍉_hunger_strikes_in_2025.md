## 2. Historical Function: Hunger Striking as Theological Shame

## 3. Why Hunger Strikes Lost Effectiveness  
Modern carceral and political systems have shifted from **moral legitimacy** to **risk legitimacy**. They are not ashamed; they are **risk-sensitive**. The primary sensitivities of 2025:

### 3.1. International Reputation Risk  
Human rights optics, ICC exposure, NGO documentation, and diplomatic fallout matter more than moral guilt.

### 3.2. Domestic Political Volatility  
Middle-class outrage, leaks, activist-lawyer mobilisation, and precarious ministries create *political instability*, not shame.

### 3.3. Legal Liability  
Article 2/3 ECHR exposure, negligence claims, coronersâ€™ inquests, judicial review precedent, medical regulation risk.

### 3.4. Operational Disruption  
Hunger strikes are administratively expensive:  
- medical monitoring  
- emergency procedures  
- multi-agency coordination  
- safeguarding escalation  
- ministerial oversight  
States dislike **unpredictable workload spikes**, not moral contradiction.

### 3.5. Narrative/OSINT Volatility  
States fear:  
- rapid narrative contagion  
- sentiment shifts  
- reputational heatmaps  
- social listening alerts  
- diaspora volatility  
- unexpected media attention

This is the modern equivalent of â€œpublic scandal,â€ but measured algorithmically, not morally.

### 3.6. Internal Coherence Threats  
The greatest fear of modern bureaucracies:  
- policy contradictions  
- cross-departmental friction  
- safeguarding vs. security conflict  
- medical ethics vs. political orders  
- legal vs. operational paralysis  
- loss of narrative discipline  

This is where contemporary pressure now sits.

## 4. Why Modern Hunger Strikes *Usually* Fail  
1. **States medically manage them.**  
   Force-feeding is bureaucratised and normalised, not scandalous.

2. **Media attention is distributed.**  
   A single death rarely becomes a focal narrative.

3. **Reputational shame requires amplification infrastructure.**  
   Without community organisation, legal mobilisation, and visibility channels, the action remains invisible.

4. **States frame hunger striking as pathology.**  
   Medicalisation neutralises political meaning.

5. **Risk registers overtake moral registers.**  
   If death = â€œcontained operational event,â€ it produces no policy movement.

## 6. The Neo-Hunger Strike: 2025 Variants That Actually Apply Pressure  
### 6.1. **Administrative Starvation**  
Withholding paperwork: SAR refusals, data withdrawal, FOI visibility plays.  
This forces the state to chase the citizen.

### 6.2. **Digital Fasts**  
Withdrawing from platforms where the state calculates sentiment, thereby reducing system signal integrity.

### 6.3. **Institutional Opt-Outs**  
Refusal to participate in programmes that require risk scoring, visibility algorithms, or behavioural prediction.

### 6.4. **Narrative Embargoes**  
Strategic refusal to speak to certain media outlets; giving exclusives to others.

### 6.5. **Legal Fasting**  
Coordinated withholding of consent for non-mandatory assessments, creating administrative gaps the state must fill.

These are not bodily harms; they are **signal harms**, **data denials**, and **operational burdens**.
