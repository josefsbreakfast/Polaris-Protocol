## ğŸ§¿ What custodianship actually means  

Custodianship separates **use** from **speculation**.

In practice, it usually means:
- land is held by a trust or protected vehicle,
- use-rights are long-term, secure, and inheritable,
- sale for speculative gain is restricted,
- and stewardship obligations are enforceable.

The goal is not control for its own sake.  
It is **continuity of function**.

This distinction matters.

## ğŸŒ¾ Why farming fits custodianship particularly well  

Food production requires:
- long planning horizons,
- soil and ecosystem repair over decades,
- protection from forced liquidation,
- and insulation from short-term shocks.

Custodianship models align naturally with this reality because they:
- reduce exposure to asset-price volatility,
- prevent land fragmentation under tax pressure,
- and allow farmers to invest without fearing dispossession.

This is stewardship **with** sovereignty, not without it.

## âš–ï¸ Common custodianship forms (non-exhaustive)

### 1) **Community Land Trusts (CLTs)**  
- Land held collectively  
- Use-rights leased long-term  
- Resale value capped to prevent extraction  

Often used for housing, but increasingly relevant for peri-urban and small-scale farming.

---

### 2) **Agricultural Land Trusts**  
- Land protected for farming use  
- Ownership removed from speculative markets  
- Farmers retain operational control  

These models explicitly treat food production as a public good.

---

### 3) **Covenanted Freehold**  
- Private ownership retained  
- Binding use-value covenants applied  
- Speculative resale constrained  

This model appeals where absolute title is politically sensitive.

---

### 4) **Publicâ€“Trust Hybrids**  
- Land held by public-interest bodies  
- Managed by local custodians  
- Long-term continuity guaranteed  

These arrangements often emerge after land failure or consolidation crises.

## âš ï¸ What custodianship is *not*  

It is not:
- forced collectivisation,
- removal of farmers,
- central planning of production,
- or denial of inheritance.

In fact, it often:
- protects family continuity,
- stabilises rural communities,
- and lowers conflict over land use.

Resistance to custodianship is usually rhetorical, not practical.
