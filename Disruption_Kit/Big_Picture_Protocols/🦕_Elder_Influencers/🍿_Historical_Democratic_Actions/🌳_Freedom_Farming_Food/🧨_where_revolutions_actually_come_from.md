## ðŸ›‘ A note on tolerance, connection, and fantasy risk  

It is important to distinguish between **social connection** and **revolutionary convergence**.

Healthy democracies require:
- cross-community dialogue,
- tolerance of uncomfortable symbolism and speech,
- and the ability for different groups to appear in public *as themselves*.

None of these, in themselves, meaningfully weaken safeguards against violent revolution.

In the UK context in particular, the cultural and organisational conditions that historically precede regime-threatening revolt are **not present**.

Even where limited bridge-building or mutual recognition occurs:
- movements remain fragmented,
- goals remain divergent,
- risk tolerance remains low,
- and institutional entanglement remains high.

This is not a value judgment.  
It is a sociological observation.

Fears that allowing:
- farmers to protest with tractors, or  
- activists to use charged or internationalist slogans  

somehow dismantles constitutional or cultural protections against violent revolution rely on a **fantasy model of UK political culture** â€” one that vastly overstates both coordination capacity and revolutionary appetite.

Suppressing benign connection does not increase safety.  
Historically, it does the opposite.

When communication and visibility are tolerated:
- conflict stays social,
- grievances remain negotiable,
- and escalation pressure dissipates.

When connection is pathologised:
- movements harden,
- mistrust accelerates,
- and alignment risks increase artificially.

Tolerance of overlap is therefore not indulgence.  
It is a **stabilising practice**.

Understanding how revolutions actually form allows societies to avoid panicked overreaction â€” which is one of the few ways to accidentally manufacture the very convergence being feared.
