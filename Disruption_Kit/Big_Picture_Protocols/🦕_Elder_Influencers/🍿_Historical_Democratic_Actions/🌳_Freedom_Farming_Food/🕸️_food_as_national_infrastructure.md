Infrastructure is defined by four properties:

1. **Non-optional use**  
2. **System-wide dependency**  
3. **Catastrophic failure modes**  
4. **Public responsibility for continuity**

Food meets all four.

Unlike other sectors:
- demand cannot be deferred,
- substitution is limited,
- failure propagates rapidly,

Treating food as anything else is a category error.

Current governance implicitly assumes:
- markets will allocate food efficiently,
- producers can absorb shocks,
- imports can substitute indefinitely,
- and consumers will adapt.

When:
- climate volatility increases,

Infrastructure thinking exists precisely for this reason.

## âš–ï¸ What infrastructure governance does differently  

When a system is recognised as infrastructure, states typically:
- underwrite catastrophic risk,
- guarantee continuity,
- regulate for resilience rather than price alone,
- and prevent extraction that undermines function.

They do **not**:
- force liquidation to meet paper valuations,
- rely on post-hoc compensation,
- or allow ownership concentration to hollow out capacity.

Food policy routinely does all three.

- maintain land and water cycles,
- absorb climate and disease shocks,
- and stabilise downstream supply.

- contracted with continuity guarantees,

In food systems, they are often treated as **price takers with moral obligations**.

This mismatch explains much of the current stress.
