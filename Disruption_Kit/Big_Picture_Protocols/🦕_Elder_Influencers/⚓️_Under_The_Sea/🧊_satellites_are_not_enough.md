# 🧊 Satellites Are Not Enough
**First created:** 2026-05-31 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-31  
*A resilience note on why satellite systems matter but do not currently replace the undersea cable layer.*

## 🛰️ Orientation

Satellite connectivity is important. It can provide emergency communications, rural and remote access, maritime links, military redundancy, disaster recovery options, and routing alternatives when terrestrial infrastructure is damaged.

But satellites are not a full substitute for the undersea cable system.

This matters because lazy resilience talk often jumps from “cables can be damaged” to “use satellites then,” as if the sky is simply the spare ocean. It is not. Satellites are a resilience layer. They are not currently a like-for-like replacement for the volume, latency, cost profile, and integration depth of undersea fibre.

## 📡 What Satellites Can Do

Satellite networks can help with:

- emergency restoration of limited connectivity;
- remote-area service;
- ship, aircraft, and expeditionary communication;
- military redundancy;
- disaster response;
- backup links for selected institutions;
- coverage where terrestrial or subsea infrastructure is absent.

Low Earth orbit constellations have changed the practical conversation. Satellite connectivity is no longer merely slow, rare, and specialist. It is increasingly visible in war zones, disaster zones, maritime settings, and emergency planning.

That is useful. It is not enough.

## 🕸️ What Cables Still Do Better

Undersea fibre carries enormous volumes of data with low latency and high reliability. It is deeply integrated into cloud infrastructure, finance, public services, telecoms, content distribution, and cross-border business systems.

Replacing that with satellites is not simply a matter of launching more hardware. It means confronting bandwidth constraints, spectrum constraints, terminal availability, power, weather effects, orbital congestion, cyber risk, commercial control, cost, and legal-jurisdiction questions.

Even where satellites can preserve access, they may not preserve normality.

A backup that keeps some systems alive is not the same as a backup that lets the whole economy carry on as if nothing happened.

## ⚖️ Resilience Is Layered, Not Magic

The realistic goal is layered resilience:

- diverse cable routes;
- protected landing stations;
- rapid repair capacity;
- maritime monitoring;
- domestic fibre redundancy;
- emergency satellite failover;
- resilient cloud architecture;
- tested public-sector continuity plans;
- allied cable-protection coordination.

The point is not “satellites bad.” The point is “satellites are one layer.”

A serious state does not bet the continuity of its financial, governmental, medical, and communications systems on one infrastructure layer while pretending another layer can absorb everything without constraint.

## 🧿 The Public Literacy Problem

The public often imagines the internet as wireless because their own device connects wirelessly.

That is an understandable mistake. Phones use mobile networks. Laptops use Wi-Fi. Platforms feel placeless. Clouds sound like weather. Satellites sound futuristic.

But most of that wireless experience quickly resolves into terrestrial fibre, data centres, exchange points, and undersea cables.

A phone signal can be wireless while the system behind it remains extremely physical.

That distinction matters for democratic consent. People cannot make sense of AUKUS, Russian submarine activity, cable repair, or undersea deterrence if they think international connectivity is basically orbital.

## 🧭 Policy Implication

Satellite investment should be part of resilience planning, but cable protection remains central.

The practical policy stance is:

> Build satellite redundancy. Do not use it as an excuse to neglect the seabed.

The seabed remains where civilian infrastructure, maritime defence, energy, cables, and deterrence now overlap. The sky can help. It cannot be asked to do everything because the ocean is politically inconvenient.
