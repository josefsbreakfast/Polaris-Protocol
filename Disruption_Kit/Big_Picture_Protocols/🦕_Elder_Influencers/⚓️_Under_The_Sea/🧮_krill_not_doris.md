# 🧮 Krill Not Doris
**First created:** 2026-05-31 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-31  
*A governance node on strategic compute, maritime resilience, and why routine citizen scoring is a bad use of national data capability.*

## 🛰️ Orientation

A serious maritime state needs serious data capability.

That is not the argument. The argument is not “no data centres,” “no modelling,” “no surveillance,” or “no national-security sensing.” A state with a coastline, ports, submarine deterrence, cable landings, offshore energy, and hostile-state exposure needs compute, cyber defence, environmental modelling, maritime domain awareness, and high-end analytics.

The issue is mission allocation.

Use the data-centre capacity for krill, not Doris.

## 🧮 Compute Is A Strategic Resource

Compute is not infinite. Neither is engineering talent, procurement attention, public legitimacy, legal oversight, energy, money, or institutional patience.

Every domestic suspicion machine competes with other uses:

- undersea domain awareness;
- cable anomaly detection;
- port resilience;
- cyber defence;
- hostile-state interference mapping;
- offshore energy security;
- UUV autonomy testing;
- anti-submarine support;
- emergency communication modelling;
- health-system resilience;
- grid protection;
- disaster response.

A country can spend its data appetite watching ordinary people, or it can spend it defending the systems ordinary people depend on.

That is a strategic choice, not only a civil-liberties choice.

## 🌊 The Real Threat Surface

Doris doing her shopping is not the threat surface.

The threat surface is:

- cable landing stations;
- seabed routes;
- ports;
- offshore energy;
- cloud infrastructure;
- critical suppliers;
- hostile cyber activity;
- maritime chokepoints;
- submarine operating assumptions;
- information operations;
- public trust under stress.

A state that cannot distinguish these from ordinary civilian life will misallocate attention. It will watch the manageable because the meaningful is harder.

The seabed is difficult to explain. Doris is administratively available.

That does not make Doris a national-security strategy.

## ⚠️ Surveillance Dependency Is Brittle Governance

If basic law and order becomes dependent on population-scale behavioural monitoring, the system becomes brittle.

It creates:

- false confidence in risk scores;
- bias laundering through technical language;
- institutional deskilling;
- overfitting to past patterns;
- political temptation;
- hostile-actor data targets;
- chilled public behaviour;
- legitimacy loss;
- procurement capture;
- endless expansion pressure.

A machine cannot assign most people to perfect innocence. Once everyone is scored, ordinary life becomes administratively suspicious by default.

That is not sustainable law and order. It is a dependency loop.

## 🧿 Legitimacy Is Defence Infrastructure

Public trust is not soft. It is infrastructure.

A state needs public cooperation during emergencies: cable outages, cyber incidents, disinformation campaigns, infrastructure disruption, defence mobilisation, disaster response, and political stress.

If the public has learned that “national security data capability” means being nudged, scored, profiled, or treated as a pre-crime object, the state damages the consent base it needs for actual resilience.

People are more likely to accept serious national data infrastructure when it is clearly pointed at real infrastructure protection: cables, ports, grids, hospitals, hostile interference, maritime safety, and emergency response.

They are less likely to accept it when it looks like the state built a telescope and pointed it through the kitchen window.

## 🧭 Policy Line

The line is not anti-defence. It is pro-seriousness.

A coastal democracy should prioritise:

- strategic infrastructure modelling;
- cable and seabed resilience;
- hostile-state threat detection;
- cyber hardening;
- lawful, targeted investigation with human accountability;
- public-interest oversight;
- clear separation between military capability and domestic population management.

It should resist:

- routine behavioural scoring;
- dragnet suspicion architecture;
- procurement systems that turn ordinary citizens into risk objects;
- using national-security language to justify administrative convenience;
- treating domestic compliance as a substitute for strategic resilience.

## 🧨 The Plain Version

We need data centres for the coastline.

We need compute for cables, ports, submarines, energy, hospitals, cyber defence, hostile interference, and the undersea systems that may keep deterrence credible as ocean opacity erodes.

We do not need to spend the future building better ways to watch Doris.

Doris is not the submarine.
