### 1. What is meant by â€œeugenics residueâ€  
Eugenics residue does **not** mean:
- explicit belief in biological superiority  
- advocacy for sterilisation or extermination  
- conscious ideological commitment  

It refers instead to **inherited assumptions** embedded in systems, including:
- whose lives are worth supporting  
- whose autonomy is trusted  
- whose dependency is tolerated  
- whose suffering is framed as inevitable  

Residue persists when systems are reformed superficially but not rebuilt.

### 2. From ideology to administration  
Eugenics did not only exist as ideology.  
It also existed as **bureaucratic practice**.

Historically, it manifested through:
- capacity assessments  
- segregation and institutionalisation  
- reproductive control  
- labour worth metrics  

When ideology became taboo, many administrative tools remained â€” reframed, renamed, and normalised.

---

### 3. Capacity regimes and conditional autonomy  
Modern mental capacity frameworks are designed to protect people. They also carry risk.

In practice, capacity assessments can:
- default to restriction rather than support  
- remove decision-making rights instead of scaffolding them  
- conflate difference with incapacity  
- prioritise institutional convenience  

When autonomy is conditional and revocable, disabled people live under **probationary citizenship**.

---

### 4. Sorting mechanisms in care and welfare  
Contemporary systems continue to sort disabled people through:
- eligibility thresholds  
- deserving vs undeserving narratives  
- compliance-based access  
- surveillance-heavy assessments  

These mechanisms do not require malicious intent to produce harm.

They function by narrowing who qualifies for support and expanding who is excluded.

---

### 5. Restriction as the path of least resistance  
Across care systems, a pattern recurs:
- independent living is complex and underfunded  
- institutional or carceral solutions are easier to audit  
- restriction is cheaper to manage than autonomy  

This creates structural pressure toward containment â€” even when policy language emphasises independence.

---

### 6. Early warning signs (structural, not rhetorical)  
Comparative historical analysis identifies warning signs that appear **before** overt atrocity.

These include:
- framing disabled lives primarily as cost  
- normalising preventable deaths as unfortunate but inevitable  
- eroding procedural safeguards  
- increasing suspicion toward claimants  
- tolerating excess mortality among marginalised groups  

These signals do not predict outcomes.  
They indicate **risk**.

### 7. The UK context: unresolved findings  
The UK has faced:
- repeated UN scrutiny regarding disability rights  
- documented cases where welfare processes contributed to deaths  
- persistent gaps between rights on paper and outcomes in practice  

These are not abstract concerns.  
They are live governance issues.

Failure to address them does not make them disappear.
