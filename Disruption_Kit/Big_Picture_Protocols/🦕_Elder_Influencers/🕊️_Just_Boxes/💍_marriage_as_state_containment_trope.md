### 1. Marriage as a Governance Container

States consistently treat marriage as a **multi-purpose box** into which complex social needs are placed:

- care of dependents  
- economic precarity  
- sexual regulation  
- inheritance and lineage  
- immigration legitimacy  
- respectability and moral signalling  

By placing these inside a private contract, the state reduces its direct responsibility while retaining **regulatory oversight**.

The box looks intimate.  
Its function is administrative.

---

- neutral

What does not fit the box becomes:
- deviant
- risky
- undeserving
- administratively inconvenient

### 3. Conditional Citizenship and Eligibility

- housing
- tax relief
- healthcare decisions
- migration status
- survivor benefits
- next-of-kin recognition

through marriage.

This turns a personal relationship into a **citizenship modifier**.

Marriage does not just express commitment.  
It **produces legibility** for the state.

### 5. Stabilisation Theatre During Crisis

During periods of:
- economic stress
- demographic anxiety
- moral panic
- political instability

marriage is rhetorically elevated as:
- foundational
- traditional
- civilisational
- restorative

This is not coincidence.

Marriage becomes a **symbolic anchor** used to signal order when institutional capacity is strained elsewhere.

### 6. Exit Penalties and Stickiness

Containment only works if exit is costly.

Marriage is reinforced through:
- legal complexity of divorce
- economic penalties
- custody leverage
- immigration vulnerability
- social stigma

These frictions do not protect relationships.  
They protect **containment**.

This node does **not** argue that marriage is inherently harmful.

- relational intimacy is instrumentalised for administrative convenience
- personal contracts are overloaded with public policy tasks
