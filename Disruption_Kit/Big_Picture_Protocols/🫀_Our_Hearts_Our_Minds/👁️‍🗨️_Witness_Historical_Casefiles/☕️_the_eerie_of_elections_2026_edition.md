# ☕️ The Eerie of Elections — 2026 Edition  
**First created:** 2026-05-07 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-07  
*Notes on platform-mediated political affect, optimisation systems, behavioural abstraction, and the strange atmosphere surrounding contemporary elections.*

---

There is a particular feeling surrounding modern elections which does not sit neatly within older ideas of propaganda, persuasion, or even media spectacle. It is not simply distrust, nor merely cynicism. It is closer to what Mark Fisher described as *the weird* and *the eerie* — the sensation that political mood is being continuously shaped by systems whose agency feels both diffuse and difficult to locate.

This does not require conspiracy thinking. Nor does it require the denial of voter agency. The point is not that outcomes are “fake,” but that the *conditions through which collective sentiment forms* have changed profoundly within the platform era.

The public increasingly experiences politics through:
- algorithmic feeds,
- segmented messaging,
- behavioural targeting,
- engagement optimisation,
- predictive modelling,
- polling loops,
- and recursive media amplification.

These systems are not isolated to one party or ideology. They have become normalised campaign infrastructure across democracies. What began as scandal — Cambridge Analytica, psychographic targeting, Facebook political manipulation discourse — gradually became ambient practice.

And yet the atmosphere remains strange.

---

## ☁️ The Eerie as Distributed Agency  

The eerie emerges when agency becomes difficult to localise.

Political narratives now appear:
- suddenly,
- simultaneously,
- and with unusual emotional synchronisation.

A sentiment can seem to stabilise before it has been consciously deliberated. A party can feel “inevitable” before its support feels socially grounded in ordinary life. Polling, feeds, commentary, clips, local grievances, influencer discourse, and predictive modelling all begin recursively reinforcing one another.

No single actor controls this process completely.

That is precisely what makes it eerie.

The machinery is distributed across:
- platforms,
- campaign consultants,
- media systems,
- analytics firms,
- recommendation algorithms,
- polling industries,
- and engagement economics.

Collective mood begins to feel semi-autonomous — continuously modulated rather than periodically expressed.

---

## 🧠 Behavioural Optimisation & Imperfect Psychology  

A core contradiction sits beneath contemporary political technology.

These systems operate as though populations can be rendered legible through behavioural data:
- voter archetypes,
- regional sentiment clusters,
- psychographic assumptions,
- engagement probabilities,
- and emotional responsiveness metrics.

Yet human beings remain inconsistent, contradictory, and socially embedded.

The psychology underpinning these systems is powerful but imperfect:
- people do not behave consistently,
- local identities remain unstable,
- emotional context changes rapidly,
- and populations resist neat abstraction.

The result is not total control, but continuous approximation.

Politics becomes a process of probabilistic nudging layered atop noisy human behaviour.

This creates a peculiar sensation:

> society being interpreted through predictive abstractions that never fully fit lived reality.

---

## 📊 Proxy Data & The Simulation Problem  

A major limitation of contemporary political modelling is that much of the data collected about populations is not direct knowledge, but proxy inference.

Campaign systems rarely possess meaningful interpretive understanding of individual voters. Instead they infer probable traits through:
- clicks,
- viewing duration,
- purchasing patterns,
- postcode clusters,
- friendship networks,
- device behaviour,
- demographic assumptions,
- engagement habits,
- and behavioural similarity models.

These traces may correlate with political behaviour without fully explaining it.

A proxy is not the same thing as understanding.

The system may infer:
- dissatisfaction,
- ideological alignment,
- emotional susceptibility,
- or issue salience,

while remaining unable to understand:
- irony,
- contradiction,
- local context,
- social performance,
- transient emotional states,
- or the instability of human belief itself.

This creates an important tension:
- the systems are influential,
- but their models are often incomplete, approximate, and partially inaccurate.

Political technology increasingly operates through compressed abstractions of social life:

> demographic clusters,  
> behavioural archetypes,  
> sentiment maps,  
> persuasion likelihoods.

And once institutions optimise around those abstractions, populations begin experiencing themselves as *modelled* rather than understood.

Citizens become predictive metadata.

Regions become emotional heatmaps.

Collective behaviour becomes a simulation continuously attempting to anticipate itself.

This contributes heavily to the eerie atmosphere surrounding modern elections:
- systems exerting influence without fully comprehending the people they classify,
- abstractions recursively shaping reality,
- and behavioural infrastructures acting upon populations through probabilistic assumptions that are always partially wrong.

The irony is that the more technically sophisticated these systems become, the easier it is culturally to overestimate what they truly “know.”

## 🌃 Why It Feels Different From 20 Years Ago  

Older political systems still involved propaganda, lobbying, tabloid influence, and narrative management. But persuasion moved more slowly:
- fewer channels,
- delayed feedback,
- less behavioural data,
- and greater friction between message and response.

Now political systems react in near real time:
- polling alters coverage,
- coverage alters feeds,
- feeds alter perception,
- perception alters polling,
- and campaigns recalibrate continuously.

Politics becomes recursive optimisation.

The “offness” many people feel may stem from sensing this acceleration intuitively before fully articulating it analytically.

Not:

> “people are mind-controlled,”

but:

> “public affect is increasingly shaped inside infrastructures designed to maximise behavioural response.”

## 🪞 Reform UK, Labour, and the Atmosphere of Inevitability  

This sensation is not unique to any one party.

After 2024, aspects of Labour’s broad coalition carried a similar atmospheric flattening: inevitability preceding emotional settlement. In 2026, Reform UK sometimes produces an intensified version of that sensation because insurgent movements interact especially effectively with:
- high-engagement platforms,
- grievance-based signalling,
- emotionally compressed narratives,
- and fragmented media environments.

The eerie is not necessarily located in the voters themselves.

It is located in:
- the velocity of narrative convergence,
- the optimisation of political mood,
- and the sensation that collective sentiment is being rendered faster than democratic culture can metabolise it.
