### 1. What diagnosis is for (clinically)  
In medicine, diagnosis is not an award, a label, or a lifestyle.

It is a **tool**.

Its functions include:
- reducing uncertainty  
- guiding treatment and management  
- identifying contraindications and risks  
- enabling informed consent  
- opening access to options and adaptations  

Even where no cure exists, diagnosis still:
- reduces trial-and-error harm  
- allows planning instead of crisis  
- gives patients language to negotiate care  
- increases autonomy within constraint  

Clinically, diagnosis is about **expanding choice**, not narrowing it.

---

### 2. Diagnosis, consent, and harm reduction  
Healthcare ethics are grounded in informed consent.

Without diagnosis:
- consent is compromised  
- risk cannot be accurately explained  
- patients cannot meaningfully choose between options  

Diagnosis enables harm reduction by:
- avoiding inappropriate treatments  
- tailoring interventions  
- preventing foreseeable deterioration  

In this sense, diagnosis is not a declaration of deficiency.  
It is a prerequisite for ethical care.

Treating diagnosis itself as the problem confuses medicine with politics.

### 8. The problem with able-bodied health standards  
When health is defined only in able-bodied terms:
- disabled people are treated as permanently unhealthy  
- quality of life is conflated with productivity  
- social exclusion is medicalised rather than addressed  

Yet reality contradicts this model.

People with:
- amputations  
- spinal injuries  
- chronic illness  
- neurodivergence  

may not meet able-bodied norms â€” and still live full, meaningful, high-quality lives.

Health is not the absence of difference.  
It is the presence of support, agency, and dignity.

### 9. Bodies are not binaries  
This problem extends beyond disability.

Ideas about:
- fatness and fitness  
- strength and size  
- capacity and appearance  

are routinely oversimplified.

There are large bodies that are strong, fast, and resilient.  
There are thin bodies that are fragile and unwell.

Binary thinking about bodies is not science. It is cultural habit.
