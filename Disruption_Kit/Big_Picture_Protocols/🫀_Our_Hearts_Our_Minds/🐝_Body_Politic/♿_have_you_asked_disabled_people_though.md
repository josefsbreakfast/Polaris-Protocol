### 1. Disability justice is not disability policy  
Disability justice is not:
- a checklist  
- a diversity initiative  
- a consultation exercise  
- a compensatory afterthought  

It is a political framework developed by disabled people â€” particularly those marginalised by race, class, gender, and poverty â€” to address **structural harm**, not surface inclusion.

Treating it as an add-on guarantees failure.

---

### 2. â€œNothing about us without usâ€ is not a slogan  
The principle â€œnothing about us without usâ€ is often quoted and rarely honoured.

Honouring it would mean:
- disabled people shaping policy goals  
- disabled people defining problems  
- disabled people holding decision-making power  
- disabled people being believed about harm  

Instead, disabled people are frequently:
- consulted after decisions are made  
- invited to validate pre-existing agendas  
- ignored when inconvenient  
- overridden when costs are named  

That is not participation.  
It is extraction.

---

### 3. Proxy voices and selective listening  
A common manoeuvre in disability discourse is **proxy advocacy**.

This includes:
- elevating a narrow subset of disabled voices  
- privileging those least disruptive to power  
- citing misdiagnosis narratives to undermine access  
- presenting partial experiences as universal truth  

This is not representation.  
It is instrumentalisation.

Disability justice rejects the idea that some disabled people can be used to discipline others.

---

### 4. Lived experience is not anecdotal noise  
Disabled testimony is often dismissed as:
- emotional  
- subjective  
- biased  

Yet disabled people are the only group with:
- continuous exposure to the system  
- cumulative knowledge of its failure modes  
- direct understanding of how policy translates into harm  

Ignoring that knowledge is not neutrality.  
It is epistemic exclusion.

---

### 5. The ten principles of disability justice (briefly)  
Disability justice frameworks emphasise:
- leadership of those most impacted  
- intersectionality  
- anti-capitalist politics  
- collective access  
- interdependence  
- sustainability  
- commitment to cross-movement solidarity  

These principles are not abstract ideals.  
They are survival strategies forged under constraint.

---

### 6. Independence is not the opposite of interdependence  
Mainstream policy fetishises independence.

Disability justice recognises that:
- all humans are interdependent  
- independence is scaffolded by infrastructure  
- pretending otherwise hides who pays the cost  

Systems that punish dependence do not create freedom.  
They create precarity.

A justice framework asks a different question:
> *What conditions make diagnosis necessary for survival?*

### 8. Who decides what counts as harm?  
In current governance, harm is often defined by:
- budget lines  
- productivity metrics  
- political optics  

Disability justice insists harm be defined by:
- lived consequence  
- preventable suffering  
- risk of death or institutionalisation  
- loss of dignity and agency  

These definitions are incompatible.

That tension is political â€” not accidental.

### 9. Consultation without power is containment  
Inviting disabled people to speak without giving them power is not inclusion.

It is:
- reputational shielding  
- risk management  
- legitimacy laundering  

Disability justice requires **transfer of authority**, not testimony on demand.
