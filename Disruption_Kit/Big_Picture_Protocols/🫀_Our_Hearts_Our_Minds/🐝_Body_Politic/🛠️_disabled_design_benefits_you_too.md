### 1. Universal design is not special treatment  
Universal design does not mean:
- bespoke solutions for a few  
- indulgent extras  
- lowered standards  

It means designing environments, systems, and tools so that **the widest range of people can use them without penalty**.

This benefits:
- disabled people  
- ageing populations  
- parents and carers  
- injured or temporarily impaired people  
- anyone under stress, fatigue, or time pressure  

Which is to say: *most humans, most of the time*.

### 2. Disability is an innovation driver, not a drag  
Many commonplace technologies originated as disability adaptations, including:
- keyboards and typewriters  
- speech-to-text and captions  
- predictive text  
- automatic doors  
- ramps and step-free access  
- visual icons and signage  

These are not marginal conveniences.  
They are foundational tools of modern life.

Disability forced design to become **better**, not weaker.

### 4. Why universal design feels threatening to power

Universal design shifts effort **upstream**, away from individual pleading and toward structural competence.
