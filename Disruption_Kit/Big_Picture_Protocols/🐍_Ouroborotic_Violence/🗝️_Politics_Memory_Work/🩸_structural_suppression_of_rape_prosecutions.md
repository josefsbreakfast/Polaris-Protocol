### 1. Suppression without prohibition  

Rape is criminalised.  
Reporting is encouraged.  
Safeguards are publicly affirmed.

And yet:
- reporting rates stagnate or fall  
- cases collapse pre-charge  
- victims withdraw at high rates  
- conviction rates remain extremely low  

This contradiction is resolved when suppression is understood as **structural**, not intentional.

---

### 2. Attrition as the primary mechanism  

The dominant mechanism of suppression is **attrition**.

Attrition is produced by:
- long investigative timelines  
- repeated disclosure demands  
- digital evidence overreach  
- exposure anxiety  
- metadata leakage  
- fear of gossip or retaliation  
- lack of meaningful interim support  

Each factor alone is survivable.  
Together, they become overwhelming.

---

### 3. Data exposure as psychological pressure  

Modern rape prosecutions occur in an environment where:

- confidentiality feels uncertain  
- digital systems are opaque  
- vendor involvement is invisible  
- metadata circulates commercially  
- R&D pipelines fragment control  

Victims do not need proof of exposure.  
**The perception of risk is sufficient** to alter behaviour.

This produces:
- self-censorship  
- delayed responses  
- reduced cooperation  
- withdrawal framed as â€œchoiceâ€  

Suppression is internalised.

---

### 4. Recursive harm cycles (ouroborotic dynamics)  

Structural suppression feeds itself:

1. Low conviction rates reduce trust  
2. Reduced trust lowers reporting  
3. Lower reporting emboldens offenders  
4. Increased offending strains systems  
5. Strain increases shortcuts and leakage  
6. Leakage heightens fear and attrition  

The system consumes its own credibility.

This is an **ouroborotic violence loop**.

---

### 5. Why reform efforts often fail  

Many reforms focus on:
- training  
- messaging  
- victim-blaming reduction  
- procedural tweaks  

They rarely address:
- data architecture  
- vendor pipelines  
- metadata ecosystems  
- R&D exposure  
- psychological containment  
- system-bound protections  

As a result, reforms improve optics while leaving suppression intact.

---

### 6. Disproportionate impact  

Structural suppression does not affect all victims equally.

It disproportionately harms:
- women and girls  
- disabled victims  
- racialised communities  
- migrants  
- LGBTQ+ survivors  
- those with precarious housing or work  
- individuals already under surveillance  

For these groups, exposure risk compounds existing vulnerability.

---

### 7. Silence as an emergent outcome  

When suppression is structural:
- silence looks voluntary  
- withdrawal looks personal  
- failure looks incidental  
- responsibility dissolves  

The system appears neutral while producing predictable harm.
