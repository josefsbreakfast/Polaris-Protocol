# 🌐 The CIA, Scorpios, and Total Information Awareness  
First created: 2026-06-04 | Last updated: 2026-06-04  
A note on astrology, information fantasy, redomination, technocracy, and the human weakness for impressive classification systems.

---

## 🛰️ Orientation  

This node is not an argument that Scorpios caused the intelligence state.

That would be stupid.

This node is about something more useful and more annoying: how human beings are repeatedly captured by classification systems that feel personal, mathematical, predictive, secret, complete, or cool-looking.

Astrology is a useful teaching object here because it is visibly not modern science, while also having many features that modern technical cultures still find seductive: complex tables, impressive calculations, personality categories, medical correspondences, timing systems, relational analysis, prediction rituals, elite patronage, and confident interpretation.

The joke is there to get people in the room.

The serious point is that defence, intelligence, policy, and technology cultures are not immune to this. They may laugh at star signs while being captured by dashboards, risk scores, threat matrices, “total information” fantasies, and models that look objective because the interface is expensive.

Some of you need to calm down.

## 🧿 Why This Belongs Under Redomination  

Redomination often works by returning a person, group, or institution to a narrowed interpretive frame.

The person may feel seen.  
The group may feel chosen.  
The institution may feel more informed than its critics.  
The leader may feel uniquely able to interpret hidden patterns.  
The model may appear to turn confusion into certainty.

That is the danger.

The redomination process does not always begin with force. Often it begins with recognition.

Someone names something that matters to you.  
Someone sees a pattern you thought others ignored.  
Someone offers a private interpretive key.  
Someone flatters your intelligence, pain, vigilance, or special role.  
Someone says: you and I understand what the others cannot.

That can be connection.  
It can also be capture.

The question is not only whether the interpretation feels meaningful. The question is what the interpretation is being used to make you do.

## 🌐 Total Information Awareness Is Not Neutral  

“Total Information Awareness” names a recurring fantasy: if we could gather enough information, we could make better decisions, prevent danger, and finally overcome uncertainty.

On the surface, this is not silly.

Incomplete information kills people.  
Bad records damage lives.  
Missed patterns can allow abuse, terrorism, fraud, negligence, and institutional drift to continue.  
Better information can improve diagnosis, safeguarding, defence, journalism, governance, and repair.

The problem begins when “more information” becomes a theology.

Information is never interpreted from nowhere. It is flavoured by:

- what is collected;
- what is excluded;
- who defines the categories;
- who is allowed to interpret;
- what the institution fears;
- what the institution wants;
- which harms count;
- which people are already treated as suspicious;
- which explanations are emotionally or politically convenient.

A crystal ball is not neutral just because it is transparent.

Lead glass distorts.  
Clear quartz refracts.  
Obsidian darkens and reflects differently.  
A dashboard does the same thing.

The object may look like it is showing reality. It is also showing the properties of the medium.

## 🖤 Why Obsidian, Not Lead Crystal Or Clear Quartz?  

There is a reason this project keeps returning to obsidian.

A clear crystal ball is the obvious image for “seeing.” It suggests transparency, purity, revelation, and access. Lead crystal glass adds another layer: polish, refinement, expensive optics, elite drawing-room divination, the object that looks neutral because it looks clear.

But clear does not mean neutral.

Glass bends light.  
Quartz refracts.  
Lead crystal dazzles.  
Every medium changes what passes through it.

When people talk about “total information,” they often imagine a perfectly transparent object: all the data, all the signals, all the patterns, all the threat indicators, all the hidden associations, finally made visible. But no information system is just a window. It is always also a lens.

What you see depends on what the system collects, excludes, weights, distorts, brightens, darkens, or makes emotionally compelling.

Obsidian gives us a better metaphor.

Obsidian is dark. It does not pretend that seeing is innocent. It reflects, but not softly. It is volcanic glass: made from heat, rupture, pressure, cooling, and sharpness. In many symbolic systems, it is associated less with easy revelation than with protection, boundary, shadow-work, and dangerous seeing.

That matters.

Deep information is not automatically safe information. Information about trauma, coercion, networks, threat, abuse, state power, intimate life, political violence, health, vulnerability, or hidden patterning can harm people if accessed without protection.

The old symbolic lesson is surprisingly sensible:

> If you are going to look into deep things, you need protection.

Not because the object is magic.

Because the human being is not neutral either.

We bring fear, hunger, ambition, grief, suspicion, vanity, shame, longing, ideology, and institutional incentives to whatever we inspect. A clear tool in an unprotected hand can still become dangerous.

So Polaris chooses obsidian as a reminder:

- do not confuse visibility with wisdom;
- do not confuse information with safety;
- do not confuse clarity with innocence;
- do not enter deep material without boundaries;
- do not treat the observer as outside the system;
- do not build tools that reveal without protecting the people revealed.

Lead crystal says: look how clear this is.

Quartz says: look how pure this is.

Obsidian says: be careful what you are asking to see.

That is the better ethic for work involving intelligence, trauma, surveillance, abuse, coercive systems, and redomination.

The aim is not total information awareness.

The aim is protected interpretation: enough information to act, enough humility to revise, enough accountability to stop, and enough boundary to avoid turning sight into harm.

## 🛡️ Why Guardrails Are Not Optional  

If you pour an institution into a powerful information system without protection, you do not automatically get wisdom.

You often get the institution’s worst habits at scale.

A system trained on British bureaucracy will not suddenly become fair, humane, and efficient because it has been digitised. It may instead reproduce the old unresolved machinery faster: imperial filing habits, suspicion of the governed, procedural cruelty, class sorting, racialised risk, administrative delay, opaque discretion, and the old habit of making the person prove their own humanity to the system.

The computer does not decolonise the form.

It can just make the form move faster.

This is why “total information” is dangerous without ethics, limits, appeal, audit, and human accountability. Information systems do not only reveal reality. They also amplify the assumptions of whoever built them, funded them, classified the data, chose the categories, and decided what counted as a problem.

Without guardrails, a system can bias toward its own worst extremes.

A suspicious institution becomes more suspicious.  
A punitive institution becomes more punitive.  
A colonial institution becomes more colonial.  
A status-obsessed institution becomes more obsessed with ranking.  
A risk-averse institution becomes more willing to sacrifice people to avoid embarrassment.

That is why regulation is not the enemy of intelligence.

Regulation is one of the ways a society says:

> We know power will flatter itself.  
> We know systems will overreach.  
> We know clever tools can magnify old harms.  
> We know visibility is not the same as justice.  
> We know someone must be able to stop the machine.

Obsidian is the better metaphor because it reminds us that deep seeing requires protection.

Not secrecy for its own sake.  
Not darkness as mystique.  
Protection.

The point is not to hide information from accountability. The point is to prevent information from being extracted, distorted, weaponised, or fed back into an institution that has never repaired its own violence.

A clear crystal says: look, everything is visible.

Obsidian says: visibility without protection can cut.

## ⚙️ Technocracy Is An Accelerator, Not A Moral Compass  

Technocracy is not automatically fascist.

It does not inherently lean toward fascism simply because it is technical, administrative, or efficient. A technically capable state can use its tools to deliver healthcare, housing, education, climate adaptation, public transport, disability support, food security, and material redistribution.

Technocracy can help a society reach humane goals faster.

It can also help a society reach cruel goals faster.

That is the point.

Efficiency does not have a political opinion. A system will accelerate whatever it is built to preserve, reward, ignore, punish, or optimise.

If a bureaucracy already contains imperial habits, class sorting, racialised suspicion, procedural cruelty, opaque discretion, or a tendency to protect itself over the people it serves, digitising it will not magically make it just. It may simply make those habits faster, harder to challenge, and easier to hide behind a dashboard.

The danger is not “technology.”

The danger is technical capacity joined to uncorrected moral failure.

A technocratic leader who imagines administrative efficiency as utopia has to plan for the ways the system will fail morally. Otherwise the system will protect itself at all costs. It will preserve the procedure, the category, the department, the target, the risk model, the budget line, the reputation, the chain of command.

And it may steamroll people to keep itself coherent.

This is why guardrails matter.

Regulation is not anti-intelligence.  
Appeal routes are not inefficiency.  
Audit is not sabotage.  
Human review is not sentimentality.  
Political accountability is not an obstacle to delivery.

They are how a society stops a powerful system from becoming loyal only to its own continuation.

A clinical analogy helps.

We do not put someone in intensive care, choose ventilator settings once, and then leave those settings unchanged until extubation. That would be dangerous. We adjust around response: oxygenation, carbon dioxide, pressure, fatigue, infection, sedation, haemodynamics, complications, recovery.

A governance system needs the same discipline.

Set the intervention.  
Observe the response.  
Look for harm.  
Adjust.  
Escalate if needed.  
Wean if possible.  
Stop if the treatment is becoming the injury.

Technocracy becomes dangerous when it forgets that people are not there to keep the machine stable.

The machine is there to help people live.

Obsidian says: do not mistake a powerful lens for a moral one. If the system is not protected against its own worst tendencies, it will magnify them.

## ♏ Why Scorpio Is Funny Here  

Scorpio is useful because it is dramatic enough to attract attention and ridiculous enough to stop the piece becoming too solemn.

Modern shorthand says:

> Scorpio means secrecy, intensity, sex, power, revenge, obsession, death, hidden knowledge, and locked filing cabinets.

But the fuller correspondence systems also give us flowers, trees, herbs, minerals, salts, foods, insects, cities, body parts, diseases, colours, stones, and timing rules.

Scorpio is not only “CIA man with a locked briefcase.”

Scorpio is also garlic, peppercorns, horseradish, leeks, asparagus, butterflies, calcium sulfate, deep red flowers, blackthorn, blood, sex, secrecy, recovery, poisoning, and mum and dad doing school-year algebra.

That is the point.

The system is not subtle. It is totalising.

It shows how human beings build filing cabinets for reality, then start mistaking the filing cabinet for the world.

## 🗓️ Why So Many Autumn Birthdays Might Not Be Mystical  

There is also a very boring possible explanation for why some powerful men in this orbit appear to cluster around September, October, and November.

It may not be Mars.  
It may not be Pluto.  
It may not be the intelligence state summoning children under the sign of the locked filing cabinet.

It may be the school year.

In many Western school systems, children born near the start of the academic year are among the oldest in their cohort. That can create early advantages: more physical maturity, more verbal confidence, better performance in classroom comparison, more leadership opportunities, and more likelihood of being treated as “bright,” “ready,” or “promising.”

Those early signals can compound.

A child who is slightly older may be read as more capable.  
A child read as more capable may be given more opportunity.  
A child given more opportunity may become more confident.  
A confident child may be selected for leadership, extension work, competitive schools, scholarships, teams, cadets, debate, public speaking, or elite pathways.

By adulthood, the original difference may no longer be obvious. It has been absorbed into biography as “talent,” “drive,” “leadership,” or “natural confidence.”

This is where contraception, class, and family planning enter.

The contraceptive pill became available in the 1960s, but many families were still moving between older forms of natural family planning, newer contraceptive possibilities, religious norms, class expectations, and changing ideas about how much control parents could or should have over family size and timing.

For aspirational middle-class families, timing a child was not necessarily mystical or sinister. It could be part of a wider household strategy: when to marry, when to conceive, when to move house, which school catchment to target, when maternity would fit around work, how to avoid financial strain, and how to give a child the best possible start.

Sometimes this is love expressed through logistics.

Sometimes it is anxiety displaced into scheduling.

Sometimes it is class trauma.

Sometimes it is controlling behaviour.

Sometimes it is neurospicy spreadsheet parenting.

Sometimes it is gold-trophy parenting: the child is treated as a visible proof of family success.

Sometimes it is NASA parenting: the family is trying, with too many checklists and not always enough warmth, to get the child safely into orbit.

Usually, humans are a mix.

So the better hypothesis is not:

> These men are Scorpios, therefore they entered the orbit of power.

It is:

> Some men associated with post-9/11 security, technology, defence, or muscular Americana may have been shaped by family planning, school-year advantage, class aspiration, institutional selection, and early confidence loops — and their autumn birthdays are one visible trace of that wider system.

In other words: not astrology as causation.

Birth timing as a possible social artefact.

All this really proves is that NASA parents and gold-trophy parents exist.

Which, frankly, most of us already knew.

Scorpio may be the funny doorway.  
The actual chart is parental optimisation, school-year structure, class pressure, reproductive technology, gendered confidence, elite selection, and the state/private security pipeline.

Some men are not born under Scorpio so much as under the sign of parental optimisation, school-year advantage, middle-class dread, and a national security state that mistakes controlled anxiety for leadership.

## 🔢 The Personality Quiz Had More Than Twelve Boxes  

Newspaper astrology flattens the system into twelve signs.

Older and more serious astrological practice is not that simple. Even using only the simplified modern “big three” — sun, moon, and ascendant — there are:

> 12 × 12 × 12 = 1,728 possible combinations.

That means there are 144 possible combinations for any one sun sign before adding houses, aspects, planetary placements, dignities, transits, decans, medical correspondences, synastry, electional timing, or any of the advanced celestial admin department.

So when someone says “Scorpios are all like this,” even astrology itself would like a word.

Which Scorpio?

Scorpio sun with Aries moon and Capricorn rising?  
Scorpio sun with Pisces moon and Libra rising?  
Scorpio sun with Gemini moon and Leo rising?

The label is not the analysis. The label is where lazy analysis often begins.

This matters beyond astrology.

Modern systems do the same thing with “risk object,” “extremist,” “founder,” “genius,” “high agency,” “unstable,” “threat,” “operator,” “leader,” “radical,” and “vulnerable person.”

A complex map gets flattened into a category.  
The category starts acting like evidence.  
The person disappears.

## 🧪 The Old System Was Not Stupid. It Was Incomplete.  

Astrology, medical astrology, alchemy, herbalism, salts, humoral theory, and older systems of correspondence were part of the messy workshop from which modern science and medicine eventually emerged.

That does not make them correct.

It does mean we should be careful about sneering too easily.

People were observing bodies, plants, minerals, seasons, disease patterns, pregnancy, pain, anxiety, appetite, sleep, temperament, injury, climate, labour, fertility, and recovery. Sometimes the observations were real. Sometimes the remedies helped. Sometimes the explanation was completely wrong.

A bad theory can preserve good observations.

That is the lesson.

Technical skill does not guarantee ontological truth. A medieval astrologer could do complex mathematics and still misunderstand causation. A modern platform can do complex statistics and still misunderstand society. A security model can identify correlations and still build a dangerous mythology around them.

Getting some predictions right does not mean you understand what is happening.

## 🧲 Cold Reading, Hot Reading, and Power  

Astrology is also useful because it shows how easily humans respond to being named.

Cold reading offers something broad enough, likely enough, or emotionally precise enough that the listener helps complete the meaning.

Hot reading uses information already known about the person — class, role, accent, background, profession, anxiety, posting history, grievance, ambition — and reflects it back in a way that feels intimate.

This is not only a stage-mystic trick.

People do this in business, politics, recruitment, sales, leadership, coercive relationships, cults, extremist pipelines, and high-control groups.

It often begins with:

> You are not like the others.  
> You have noticed something real.  
> You are unusually perceptive.  
> You have been underestimated.  
> You and I understand what other people cannot.

Those statements are not always bad. Sometimes they are true. Sometimes they are kind.

The danger is the next move.

Does the person use recognition to give you more room to think?  
Or do they use recognition to make themselves the only safe interpreter?

That is the redomination test.

## 🪞 Being Fooled Is Human  

This node should not be read as: look how stupid people are for being fooled.

That helps no one.

Human brains are built to respond to pattern, belonging, threat, status, beauty, recognition, and story. That is not a personal defect. It is part of being a social animal.

People can be conned.  
Clever people can be conned.  
Serious defence men with lanyards can be conned.  
Doctors can be conned.  
Journalists can be conned.  
Activists can be conned.  
Policy people can be conned.  
Technologists can be conned by anything with a clean interface and a confidence interval.

The adult question is what happens next.

Shame says:

> I was fooled, therefore I am ruined.

Responsibility says:

> I was fooled, and now I need to understand how, repair what I can, and stop feeding the system that fooled me.

That is the route back.

You are allowed to admit you were misled.  
You are not allowed to keep harming people because admitting it would embarrass you.

## 🧰 Astrology As Interpretive Multitool  

Astrology is not a reliable causal account of human personality.

But as a symbolic language for reflection, it may be less dangerous than many modern identity systems that teach people to turn pain into hierarchy.

In a world where the alternative pipeline for confused, lonely, angry, self-searching people is often:

> your pain is women’s fault;  
> your worth is dominance;  
> empathy is weakness;  
> status is everything;  
> humiliation must be avenged;  
> buy my course;

then someone saying, “maybe I need to work on trust, jealousy, secrecy, emotional intensity, and control” is not the worst outcome on the table.

Astrology can be an interpretive EDC multitool.

Not a lab instrument.  
Not a court judgment.  
Not a medical diagnosis.  
Not a targeting system.  
Not a governance model.

But a pocket tool for human reflection? Sometimes, yes.

It can be:

- a mirror;
- a conversation starter;
- a pattern prompt;
- a language for temperament;
- a way to think about timing;
- a symbolic filing system;
- a joke delivery mechanism;
- a soft entry into harder conversations about power, personality, family, risk, control, shame, desire, and fear.

Like any multitool, the question is what you are using it for.

Opening a bottle? Fine.  
Tightening a screw? Fine.  
Cutting a loose thread? Fine.  
Performing vascular surgery? Absolutely not, put it down.

Astrology for reflection: fine.  
Astrology for storytelling: fine.  
Astrology for “maybe I repeat this pattern under stress”: useful enough.  
Astrology for coercing someone, excusing harm, replacing evidence, or running public policy: no.

A tool does not become wise because it has many attachments. It becomes safer when people remember what it is not for.

## 🧭 Humility After the Crystal Ball  

Astrology is not useful here because it is true.

It is useful because it shows how human beings build totalising interpretive systems.

These systems can contain real observations, practical remedies, good mathematics, local ecological knowledge, medical pattern recognition, political intelligence, and cultural memory — while still making causal claims that are completely wrong.

That should make modern institutions more humble.

Every age thinks it has the best tools available. Sometimes it does.  
Every age thinks it is on the cusp of better knowledge. Sometimes it is.  
Every age has people with charts, tables, patrons, instruments, satisfied clients, and successful predictions.

And sometimes those people are still putting asparagus in the Scorpio drawer.

The modern version has better maths.

It does not automatically have better humility.

## 🛠️ Practical Checks  

When a system claims to make hidden patterns visible, ask:

- What information is being collected?
- What information is missing?
- Who chose the categories?
- Who benefits if this interpretation is accepted?
- Who can appeal the classification?
- What happens if the model is wrong?
- Does the system increase human judgement or replace it?
- Does it help people think, or make them dependent on the interpreter?
- Does it create room for repair?
- Does it punish people for questioning the frame?
- What existing institutional harms might it accelerate?
- What guardrails would let people stop, correct, or challenge it?

The point is not to reject information.

The point is to stop worshipping it.

A better aim than total information awareness is accountable uncertainty management:

> enough information to act,  
> enough humility to revise,  
> enough accountability to stop.
