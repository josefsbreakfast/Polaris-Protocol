# 🧰 Poor Political Varnish

**First created:** 2026-05-28 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-28
*Why “small boats as invasion” is a weak political coating over older British rescue memory, class grievance, and wartime contradiction.*

## 🛰️ Orientation

The hostile “small boats” narrative is poor political varnish.

It can be applied.

It can darken the surface.

It can make people angry, suspicious, punitive, racist, defensive, and attached to a sharpened form of patriotism.

But it does not bond cleanly with the older grain underneath.

That older grain contains:

* Dunkirk;
* the Channel;
* little ships;
* lifeboat culture;
* people fleeing war;
* Commonwealth sacrifice;
* Somme poetry;
* distrust of elite command;
* wartime family trauma;
* the belief that force should protect rescue;
* the belief that Britain’s noblest self goes out to get people.

This does not mean British people cannot be racist.

They can.

This does not mean hostile border narratives cannot work.

They can.

It means the narrative is structurally unstable because it has to fight the country’s own rescue mythology.

The varnish changes the colour for a while.

But the grain keeps showing through.

## 🪵 The Older Grain

Before “small boats” became a hostile political slogan, small boats already carried a different British meaning.

They belonged to:

* fishermen;
* lifeboats;
* Channel crossings;
* rescue at sea;
* Dunkirk;
* ordinary courage;
* civilians answering disaster;
* people brought home under pressure;
* “we go and get them.”

That memory is selective and mythologised.

It edits out much.

It sentimentalises much.

It is entangled with empire, class, whiteness, masculinity, regional loss, and national self-flattery.

But it remains emotionally active.

This is why “small boats” is not an empty phrase in Britain.

It is a contested symbolic object.

One political story says:

> small boats are threat.

Another older story says:

> small boats are rescue.

That is the problem.

## 🧰 The Varnish

The hostile political varnish tries to make “small boats” mean:

* invasion;
* disorder;
* illegality;
* queue-jumping;
* criminal networks;
* loss of control;
* national humiliation;
* outsiders arriving to take what is ours.

This varnish can work at the surface level.

It can produce anger.

It can make people feel that sovereignty has been breached.

It can mobilise resentment.

It can attach itself to genuine pressures around housing, wages, services, councils, NHS access, dentistry, schools, local infrastructure, and regional decline.

But it has to work hard.

It has to keep insisting that the people in the boats are not vulnerable, not rescue-coded, not fleeing danger, not morally legible, not connected to Britain’s wider historical story, and not people whom decent societies might have obligations toward.

That is why the rhetoric becomes aggressive.

It has to shout over the rescue association.

## 🛥️ The Boats Do Not Stay in the Box

The political machine wants “small boats” to stay inside a narrow border-control box.

But the boats keep leaking.

They leak into:

* Dunkirk;
* refugees;
* war zones;
* asylum;
* lifeboats;
* Channel danger;
* humanitarian rescue;
* the moral test of whether people at sea should be helped.

This is why the phrase is volatile.

A government may try to say:

> small boats mean border failure.

But a viewer may feel:

> small boats mean people in danger.

A newspaper may try to say:

> small boats mean invasion.

But the cultural memory may answer:

> small boats are what people use when official systems fail.

A minister may try to say:

> stop the boats.

But the older memory may ask:

> why would you stop rescue?

That instability does not automatically make the public humane.

But it does make the hostile frame harder to keep coherent.

## 🧨 Why It Has To Get Nasty

To override the rescue frame, the hostile narrative has to become nasty.

It has to make the people in the boats appear:

* fraudulent;
* threatening;
* criminal;
* undeserving;
* culturally alien;
* sexually dangerous;
* economically parasitic;
* militarily invasive;
* morally suspect.

The narrative must strip vulnerability away.

Otherwise the rescue memory starts leaking through.

This is why racism, Islamophobia, suspicion, and punitive language become so useful to the political frame. They are not incidental excesses. They help suppress recognition.

The public has to be trained not to see people in danger.

It has to be trained to see a threat object.

That training is costly.

It may harden some people.

But it also degrades public trust, increases paranoia, and makes the country harder to govern in other ways.

## 🧠 The Narrative Does Not Stay Coherent

The hostile frame can make people angry.

It can make them loud.

It can make them repeat slogans.

But when questioned, the narrative often starts to wobble.

Because too many older stories interfere.

If small boats are always threat, what was Dunkirk?

If people fleeing war are automatically suspect, what did Britain claim to stand for?

If migrants are pure outsiders, why were so many countries already inside Britain’s imperial and wartime story?

If too much racism is bad because it starts to resemble the Nazi role, how far can the rhetoric go before it touches the wrong memory?

If sovereignty matters, why is anger directed at desperate people rather than the political and economic classes that hollowed out ordinary life?

These questions do not magically solve racism.

But they reveal the weakness of the varnish.

The hostile story can be repeated.

It cannot always be metabolised.

## 🧱 What Is Coherent: Sacrifice Without Return

The incoherence is not in the underlying grievance.

The underlying grievance is often very coherent.

Many people can make perfect sense of this:

> My family sacrificed.
> My town sacrificed.
> My region built ships, weapons, planes, coal, steel, ports, railways, factories, and military infrastructure.
> Young men died.
> Women held households and factories together.
> Streets were bombed because they mattered.
> People endured rationing, grief, trauma, silence, and rebuilding.
> And now everything is worse.

That is not stupid.

That is a real political wound.

People can see:

* housing insecurity;
* wage stagnation;
* insecure work;
* council collapse;
* unaffordable rent;
* overloaded hospitals;
* inaccessible dentistry;
* neglected towns;
* hollowed-out high streets;
* transport failure;
* regional abandonment;
* futures smaller than the sacrifice they were taught to honour.

That anger is not imaginary.

The incoherence begins when that anger is redirected toward desperate people in boats.

The coherent claim is:

> We gave too much for this country to become this.

The incoherent claim is:

> And therefore the people fleeing war are the enemy.

The grievance is coherent.

The scapegoat is not.

## 🎯 The Supplied Target

The hostile small-boats narrative supplies a target for pain that was produced elsewhere.

The pain may come from:

* austerity;
* deindustrialisation;
* housing policy;
* wage suppression;
* regional neglect;
* privatisation;
* NHS strain;
* local government collapse;
* landlordism;
* insecure work;
* asylum-system dysfunction;
* political abandonment;
* media resentment cycles;
* the loss of meaningful democratic control.

But instead of naming those systems clearly, the narrative points to the visible person in the boat.

This is convenient.

The person in the boat is visible.

The machinery is harder to see.

The person in the boat can be photographed, named, racialised, demonised, and made symbolic.

The people who designed, profited from, neglected, or mismanaged the systems often remain more protected.

That is the trick.

The wound is real.

The target has been supplied.

## 🏭 Defence Regions and the Complicity Problem

This matters especially in regions with military, industrial, shipbuilding, RAF, Navy, Army, or arms-production memory.

It is not contradictory for those places to carry both pride in defence work and resistance to weapons being used against civilians.

In fact, the resistance can come from the same memory.

The inherited moral story may not be:

> weapons are always good because our region made them.

It may be:

> force is justified when it protects, rescues, defends, liberates, or stops something worse.

So if people believe local labour, engineering, exports, parts, bases, or defence partnerships are linked to civilian harm, they may experience that not as abstract left politics but as a betrayal of the moral story defence was supposed to carry.

The reaction may be:

> We were told this work was for defence.
> We were told our people served to protect civilians.
> We were told Britain stood against occupation, siege, starvation, and overwhelming force.
> So why is our labour implicated in this?

That is not anti-military by default.

It may be military memory turning against force in the wrong role.

## 🪖 The Haig-Shaped Anger Route

British wartime memory also contains a strong class-coded suspicion of elite decision-making.

In popular memory, figures such as General Haig stand for a broader pattern:

* remote command;
* upper-class certainty;
* bad decisions made far above the mud;
* ordinary men paying the price;
* poor families burying the dead;
* elites surviving the consequences.

The historical scholarship is more complicated than the caricature.

But popular memory does not need perfect accuracy to carry political force.

The lesson many people inherit is simple:

> ordinary people get sent;
> ordinary people bleed;
> ordinary families bury the dead;
> the people making the decisions often survive the consequences.

That memory can interrupt racist redirection.

The question becomes:

> Is your anger really at desperate people in boats?
> Or is your anger at the insulated political class that keeps creating disasters and asking ordinary people to absorb the consequences?

That is the flip point.

Do not begin by saying:

> do not be angry.

Begin by asking:

> why has your anger been pointed at the people in the boats instead of the people who keep making boats necessary?

## 🌍 The Outsider Frame Keeps Failing

The hostile small-boats narrative depends on a clean outsider frame.

But British imperial and Commonwealth memory makes that frame unstable.

Britain’s war story was never only white, island-British, or domestic.

People from India, Africa, the Caribbean, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and many other parts of the British Empire and Commonwealth served, laboured, supplied, fought, died, sailed, carried, nursed, repaired, farmed, and endured inside Britain’s war machine.

This was not a cosy family story.

It was empire.

It involved coercion, hierarchy, extraction, racism, unequal citizenship, and unequal commemoration.

But it still complicates the outsider frame.

A British person can be racist and still recognise sacrifice.

That does not make the racism harmless.

It makes it unstable.

The contradiction is not:

> Britain is secretly not racist.

The contradiction is:

> Britain is racist toward people it also half-remembers as kin.

That makes “be angry at those people” harder to sustain than propagandists often assume.

The people being cast as strangers may already be inside Britain’s family album.

Brutally.

Unequally.

Without consent.

But inside it all the same.

## ✡️ Too Much Racism Touches the Villain Role

British public memory also carries an anti-Nazi residue.

It is not always deep anti-racism.

Often it is not.

But many people still carry a basic rule:

> too much racism puts you in the Nazi role, and the Nazi role is the wrong side.

That rule is inconsistent, sentimental, and full of loopholes.

But it is still politically useful.

It means that racist narratives can become unstable when pushed too far. People may hold ugly views, but they may also recoil when the frame starts sounding too much like the villain-role Britain was raised to reject.

This is one reason far-right narratives can be louder than they are deep.

They can activate fear, humiliation, resentment, and racial suspicion.

But when pressed into the British wartime frame — boats, sacrifice, Commonwealth service, Nazis as the bad role, people fleeing danger, civilians under force — parts of the narrative start to rub against another inheritance people also believe.

The contradiction can be interrupted.

The question is not always:

> Can this person become an anti-racist philosopher overnight?

Sometimes the question is:

> In the war story you grew up with, which side stops desperate people from getting out?

## 🛥️ Flotillas Make the Varnish Fail Faster

Humanitarian flotillas make the varnish fail faster because they collapse the ambiguity.

Small boats in immigration politics can be fought over.

Are they threat?

Are they rescue?

Are they border crisis?

Are they people in danger?

But a humanitarian flotilla carrying aid toward trapped civilians enters the rescue frame more directly.

The boats are not only crossing water.

They are carrying relief.

They are moving toward people who are visibly trapped.

If military force stops them, the British memory system does not need much help.

It sees the wrong role.

This is why flotilla imagery is so strategically dangerous.

It does not only activate the already sympathetic.

It can reach people who do not think they care.

Even someone who is racist, indifferent, or hostile to Palestinians may still feel the image problem when the scene becomes:

* boats;
* aid;
* civilians;
* military force;
* people taken off vessels;
* rescue interrupted.

They may not say “Dunkirk.”

They may just say:

> That’s not right.

That is enough for the varnish to crack.

## 📺 Media Saturation and Public Instinct

The older grain has been reinforced through decades of media.

Not only formal history.

Everyday cultural weather.

Relevant sources include:

* *Dunkirk* — rescue, little ships, Spitfire guardianship, national pressure chamber;
* *The World at War* — documentary saturation of Second World War memory;
* *Dad’s Army* — invasion anxiety domesticated through comedy;
* *Band of Brothers* — Anglophone war-cinema grammar, moral injury, execution, liberation, and the human mess of war;
* *Downton Abbey* — Somme memory, class command, trauma, and the “blighty wound”;
* *Masters of the Air* / *Masters of Air* — air power, bombing, food drops, and relief from above;
* Churchill clips and reenactments — refusal, crisis rhetoric, “never surrender”;
* TikTok Dunkirk edits — music-coded rescue mythology;
* “I Vow to Thee, My Country,” “Mary on a Cross,” “Bad Things,” and “Way Down We Go” — soundtrack tags for sacrifice, doom, martyrdom, relief, and national feeling.

This means the public does not need a formal political education to react.

It has had a media one.

The boats are already coded.

## 🧨 Blowback: Making Britain Ungovernable Somewhere Else

The hostile small-boats frame does not come free.

Because it has to override rescue memory, it relies heavily on fear, suspicion, racialisation, punitive fantasy, and humiliation.

But once that emotional machinery is built, it does not stay neatly attached to immigration policy.

It spills.

The public becomes more suspicious.

Border anxiety bleeds into institutional distrust.

Security language bleeds into anti-humanitarian reflex.

Suspicion of migrants bleeds into suspicion of courts, charities, lawyers, journalists, local councils, international law, and anyone who appears to defend procedural restraint.

So the state may win a short-term messaging battle while creating a larger governability problem.

It teaches people to feel permanently betrayed, invaded, humiliated, and overruled by hidden forces.

Then it acts surprised when those people become harder to govern, harder to reassure, and harder to bring back into democratic trust.

They tried to govern Britain by overriding the rescue story with a threat story.

The problem is that once you teach the public to live inside the threat story, it does not politely stop at immigration policy.

## 🧭 Working Diagnostic

When assessing a “small boats” narrative, ask:

1. Is the narrative trying to make boats mean threat?
2. What older rescue meanings is it trying to suppress?
3. How much racialisation is required for the frame to work?
4. Is the public grievance actually about migrants, or about broken systems?
5. Who supplied the visible target?
6. What machinery is being protected by the scapegoat?
7. Does Commonwealth memory complicate the outsider frame?
8. Does anti-Nazi memory limit how far racism can comfortably go?
9. Does the narrative become incoherent when questioned?
10. What would happen if the anger were redirected toward the systems that made the grievance real?

If the hostile frame requires constant aggression to remain stable, the varnish is poor.

## 🧿 Analysis

The “small boats as invasion” story can work.

But it is not strong.

It is a political coating over older wood.

The older wood remembers rescue.

It remembers class sacrifice.

It remembers people sent to die by elites.

It remembers Commonwealth service.

It remembers the wrongness of occupation.

It remembers that force is legitimate when it protects rescue, not when it obstructs it.

It remembers that too much racism starts to smell like the villain role.

That does not make Britain pure.

It does not make British people reliably humane.

It does not erase racism, cruelty, border violence, or selective compassion.

But it does mean the hostile narrative has to fight the archive.

And when the right question is asked, the varnish starts to lift.

The core political task is not to tell people their anger is stupid.

It is to distinguish between the real wound and the supplied target.

The wound is sacrifice without return.

The target is the person in the boat.

Those are not the same thing.
