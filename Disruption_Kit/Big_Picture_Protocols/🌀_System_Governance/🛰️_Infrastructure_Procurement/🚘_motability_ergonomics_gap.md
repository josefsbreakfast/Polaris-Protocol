# 🚘 Motability Ergonomics Gap
**First created:** 2026-05-05 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-05  
*Structural access failure when vehicle ergonomics fall outside cost-optimised scheme design.*

---

## Core Claim

A disability mobility scheme cannot be evaluated solely on vehicle provision.

It must be evaluated on **functional usability**.

Where available vehicles cannot be safely entered, exited, or used without pain, the scheme provides mobility in theory but fails to deliver it in practice.

This is the ergonomics gap.

---

## Structural Context

In the UK, Motability functions as the primary—effectively singular—national pathway for disabled vehicle access.

There is no equivalent parallel scheme.

This creates a structural condition:

> When the scheme does not meet a user’s physical requirements, there is no viable alternative route to equivalent support.

The result is not reduced choice.

It is enforced compromise.

---

## The Ergonomic Threshold

For many disabled users, usability depends on non-negotiable physical criteria:

- seat height relative to hip level  
- door aperture and opening angle  
- sill height and step-over requirement  
- transfer geometry (including rotation and load-bearing)  
- boot height and loading access  
- seat structure and support  

These are not preferences.

They are access conditions.

If these thresholds are not met, the vehicle is functionally inaccessible regardless of its availability within the scheme.

---

## Market Drift

The current vehicle market is reducing the availability of ergonomically suitable models:

- decline of MPVs and access-oriented designs  
- upward price movement of mid-height crossovers  
- electric vehicle architectures introducing new access constraints  
- prioritisation of higher-margin vehicle segments by manufacturers  

The scheme does not control these shifts, but it is directly shaped by them.

---

## Cost Optimisation vs Functional Access

Motability Operations Ltd operates under cost constraints tied to fixed benefit levels and fleet risk management.

This produces rational outcomes at system level:

- restriction of vehicle list  
- removal of models with unfavourable cost or resale profiles  
- emphasis on financial sustainability  

However, this optimisation is not neutral.

> It systematically deprioritises edge-case physical requirements in favour of average usability.

For users whose access depends on specific ergonomics, this results in exclusion.

---

## The Private Market Constraint

When suitable vehicles are not available within the scheme, users are directed—implicitly or explicitly—toward private alternatives.

This is not a neutral fallback.

Private access requires:

- substantial upfront capital  
- ongoing insurance, servicing, and breakdown costs  
- independent funding of adaptations  
- assumption of financial and suitability risk  

For individuals on fixed or reduced incomes, this is frequently non-viable.

The result is a de facto paywall around functional mobility.

---

## Failure Mode

The scheme continues to function administratively and financially.

But at the point where ergonomic fit becomes non-negotiable, it produces:

- unsafe transfers  
- increased pain and fatigue  
- reduced independence  
- or complete exclusion from driving  

This is not a marginal inefficiency.

It is a targeted failure at the interface between system design and embodied need.

---

## Correct Framing

This is not a claim that the scheme fails universally.

It is a claim that:

> A system designed for disabled mobility produces structural exclusion when ergonomic suitability is treated as secondary to cost control.

---

## Advocacy Line

> Where no alternative pathway exists, failure to provide ergonomically suitable vehicles is not a limitation of choice but a failure of access.

---

## Ergonomic Reality Check (For Non-Disabled Readers)

This section exists to translate abstract “vehicle choice” into physical experience.

If you can get into almost any car without thinking, you are not using the same criteria.

---

### Step 1: The Sit Test

Stand next to a low car (typical hatchback).

Now:
- keep your knees stiff
- do not use your hands for support
- lower yourself into the seat slowly

Then try to get back out without pushing off the door or steering wheel.

For many disabled users, this is not difficult.

It is impossible, painful, or unsafe.

---

### Step 2: The Height Test

Stand next to a taller vehicle.

Now:
- lift your body up and rotate into the seat
- avoid twisting your knee or hip
- avoid using momentum

If you need to “climb” or pull yourself in, this is not accessible.

---

### Step 3: The Transfer Test

Imagine:
- you cannot put full weight on one leg
- you have limited hip rotation
- you fatigue quickly

Now try:
- turning into the seat
- bringing both legs in
- adjusting position once seated

This is where many “available” cars fail.

---

### Step 4: The Door and Sill Test

Check:
- how wide the door opens  
- how high the sill is  
- how far you must step over  

If you have to:
- lift your leg high  
- twist while stepping  
- or pause mid-transfer  

The design is already working against you.

---

### What This Demonstrates

Vehicle access is not binary.

It is not:
> “can you get in?”

It is:
> “can you get in safely, repeatedly, without pain, and without assistance?”

A car that fails this test is not a viable option, regardless of whether it appears on a scheme list.

---

### Why This Matters

When ergonomically suitable vehicles disappear:

- the number of *listed* cars may remain high  
- but the number of *usable* cars drops sharply  

This is the ergonomics gap in practice.

---

### Translation

If you have never had to think about how to sit in a car:

You are measuring availability.

Disabled users are measuring survival, safety, and independence.

These are not the same metric.
