# 🎯 Single Points of Failure as Authoritarian Easy Targets  
**First created:** 2025-11-05 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-13  
*Why concentrated roles are so attractive to the worst people in the room.*  

---

## 🧭 Orientation  
This node looks at **single points of failure** in democratic systems — especially where one person:

- Holds multiple statutory or decision-making roles.  
- Controls key information flows.  
- Is embedded in both local governance and national-security / resilience networks.

These individuals might be conscientious professionals.  
But structurally, they are **authoritarian targets**: nodes that bad actors (inside or outside the state) can capture, flatter, threaten or reward to redirect an entire system.

---

## 🧩 What Is a Single Point of Failure in Governance?  

Not just “someone important,” but someone who, by design or creep:

- Has **sole sign-off** on legality (Monitoring Officer), finance (s.151), or organisation (Head of Paid Service).  
- Acts as **data gatekeeper** (RIPA approvals, data-sharing agreements, whistle-blowing inboxes).  
- Represents the authority on **multi-agency fora** (LRF, Prevent boards, resilience partnerships).

If they are compromised, overawed, captured or simply overworked, **everything downstream inherits the problem**.

---

## 🎯 Why Authoritarians Love Them  

Whether we’re talking about:

- A power-hungry local operator,  
- A national authoritarian project, or  
- A hostile foreign state looking for leverage,

single points of failure offer:

1. **Efficiency of capture**  
   - One relationship, many levers.  
   - No need to convince committees if one person can pre-filter everything reaching them.

2. **Opacity**  
   - Decisions can be framed as “professional judgement” or “legal advice.”  
   - Paper trails show impeccable procedure while hiding the originating pressure.

3. **Plausible deniability**  
   - If the node fails, blame can be personalised (“a rogue officer”) rather than structural.

4. **Soft leverage**  
   - Flattery, invitations, board seats, conference slots, or “thought-leadership” roles in the resilience/security sector can cultivate loyalty without overt bribery.

---

## 🧠 Pathways of Capture  

| Pathway | Mechanism | Example Pattern |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **Courtier capture** | Ego and status | Officer repeatedly invited to speak on “best practice”, given honorary roles; becomes invested in pleasing that network. |
| **Economic entanglement** | Future job prospects or consultancy | Offers of advisory roles, secondments, or “after you retire” positions. |
| **Security framing** | Appealing to “national security” or “public order” | Officer told dissenters or diaspora groups are “extremism risks”; begins to treat democracy as threat vector. |
| **Information chokehold** | Control of what others see | Officer filters legal or risk advice so elected members never see alternatives. |
| **Fear & dependency** | Isolation and responsibility overload | Overstretched officer convinced only they can hold things together; becomes more pliable to whoever promises support. |

Authoritarian projects rarely begin with tanks.  
They begin with people like this being **overtrusted and under-scrutinised**.

---

## 🧩 Civil Security Implications  

Single points of failure are where:

- Whistle-blowers’ reports go to disappear.  
- Data-sharing and surveillance creep in through “risk” language.  
- Diaspora politics get reclassified as cohesion or extremism problems.  
- Local capture (political or economic) can be scaled rapidly.

From a **civic defence** perspective, they should trigger **red flags**, not reverence.

---

## 🧱 Design Principles for Reducing Target Value  

1. **Role Separation**  
   - One person, one statutory role.  
   - No combination of Monitoring Officer, s.151 Officer, and Head of Paid Service in a single individual.

2. **Deputies and Rotation**  
   - Formal deputies with clear delegated authority.  
   - Time-limited terms or inter-council rotation for key posts.

3. **Transparent Escalation Paths**  
   - Whistle-blowing and integrity routes that **bypass** local IT and go to independent bodies.  
   - Clear signposting of appeal routes beyond the “golden triangle.”

4. **Public Documentation of Powers**  
   - Simple public-facing diagrams of who can sign off what.  
   - LRF and SCG governance charts that show where authority sits.

5. **External Peer Review**  
   - Regular LGA / DLUHC RED / NAO reviews of role concentration, not just finances.  
   - Publish findings in full, not just “assurance summaries.”

---

## 🧩 Authoritarian Target Checklist (Field Use)  

When mapping a council or agency, flag individuals who:

- Hold **2+ statutory or equivalent roles**.  
- Sit on **LRF / Prevent / resilience boards** *and* economic-development or innovation boards.  
- Sign off both **surveillance/data-sharing** and **HR/grievance handling**.  
- Are repeatedly cited in minutes as “the” point of contact for risk, resilience, and legal sign-off.

Those nodes deserve **protective redesign**, not further power.
