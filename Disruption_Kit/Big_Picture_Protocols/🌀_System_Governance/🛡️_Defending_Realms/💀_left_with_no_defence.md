# 💀 Left With No Defence  
First created: 2026-05-31 | Last updated: 2026-05-31**  
Why democratic defence cannot be rebuilt through messaging alone when public consent has already been damaged by legally contested war, managed evidence, and elite misuse of power.

## 🛰️ Orientation

This node examines a recurring failure in British centre-left and left politics: the inability to speak plainly about defence as a democratic public duty.

The issue is not that the left should become militarist. It should not.

The issue is that large parts of the British left have become so shaped by the memory of Iraq, legally contested intervention, and elite consent-management that they struggle to distinguish between:

- defence,
- militarism,
- humanitarian intervention,
- alliance-following,
- peacekeeping,
- anti-atrocity protection,
- and wars of choice.

That confusion leaves a vacuum.

Into that vacuum step harder right actors, defence nostalgists, authoritarian populists, and people quite happy to present themselves as the only adults who still believe protection is real.

That is politically dangerous, strategically unserious, and morally unnecessary.

## 🧿 The Missing Sentence

The sentence should not be difficult:

> A democratic state has a duty to maintain credible defence capacity, under law, with public accountability, because coercive threats do not disappear when decent people dislike militarism.

This is not a right-wing sentence.

It is a democratic sentence.

It belongs to anyone who understands that ordinary people deserve not to be bombed, invaded, coerced, starved, hacked, terrorised, sabotaged, or abandoned while the comfortable debate whether protection is aesthetically embarrassing.

## ⚖️ Defence Is Not Militarism

A serious left position has to distinguish between lawful defence and militarist fantasy.

| Term | Meaning | Democratic left position |
|---|---|---|
| Defence | Protection of civilians, territory, infrastructure, allies, democratic order, and lawful sovereignty | Necessary |
| Deterrence | Credible capacity to prevent attack or coercion | Dangerous but often necessary |
| Peacekeeping | Internationally mandated stabilisation or civilian protection | Legitimate when lawful, accountable, and realistic |
| Anti-atrocity protection | Action to prevent or reduce mass violence | Morally serious, legally constrained |
| Militarism | Fetishising force, hierarchy, enemies, weapons, and patriotic theatre | Reject |
| Wars of choice | Discretionary interventions sold through urgency, alliance loyalty, or moral theatre | Deeply suspect |
| Imperial violence | Coercive force used to dominate, extract, punish, or control | Oppose |

The problem is that British political memory often collapses all of these into one contaminated category: “war.”

That collapse is understandable.

It is also disabling.

## 🩻 The Iraq Injury

For many people alive today, especially on the left, the defining memory of Labour and military force is not defence of the realm.

It is Iraq.

That matters.

The public memory is not simply:

> Labour went to war.

It is closer to:

> Labour went to war through a managed evidentiary atmosphere, with public and parliamentary consent shaped by intelligence claims, urgency, alliance pressure, and later scandal.

This did not merely damage one government.

It damaged the legitimacy architecture around defence itself.

When a political class asks the public to accept military action, and that action later appears to have been justified through overstated certainty, weak scrutiny, poor planning, or legally contested reasoning, the injury spreads beyond that one conflict.

The public learns to hear defence language as sales language.

That is catastrophic.

Because democratic defence depends on consent.

Consent depends on truthfulness.

Truthfulness depends on the state not treating intelligence, humanitarian concern, or emergency rhetoric as consent-production machinery.

## 🕯️ The Forgotten Left Of Armed Resistance

The left has not always been demilitarised in its imagination.

Anti-fascist history includes armed resistance, sabotage, partisan warfare, underground networks, civilian defence, and people who understood that fascism was not defeated by raising awareness.

The sanitised memory of the Second World War often remembers victims, liberators, and moral clarity, but forgets the violent, dangerous, messy reality of resistance.

Jewish partisans existed.

Communist partisans existed.

Socialist, anarchist, nationalist, local and improvised resistance networks existed.

Occupied people fought.

Civilians hid, smuggled, sabotaged, transmitted, armed themselves, protected one another, and sometimes killed.

That does not mean every armed actor calling itself resistance is legitimate.

It means the left should not pretend violence only exists as state violence or fascist violence.

Under conditions of occupation, siege, invasion, genocide, ethnic cleansing, or authoritarian domination, armed resistance can be historically real and morally intelligible.

But that does not give any armed group a blank cheque.

A serious democratic left has to be capable of saying two things at once:

> Resistance to domination can be legitimate.

and

> Legitimacy is not created merely by claiming the word resistance.

That distinction matters for every contemporary conflict where civilians are trapped between state violence, non-state violence, occupation, siege, terrorism, counter-terrorism, proxy warfare, and propaganda.

## 🧨 Hamas, Resistance, And The Failure Of Moral Categories

One reason parts of the left get tangled around Hamas is that they lack a serious grammar for force.

If all violence is either “state violence” or “resistance,” then analysis becomes childish very quickly.

Hamas can be understood as arising within occupation, siege, dispossession, failed diplomacy, political abandonment, and a long history of Palestinian suffering.

That does not require pretending Hamas is simply “the resistance” in a flattened romantic sense.

It does not require ignoring authoritarianism, attacks on civilians, hostage-taking, antisemitism, coercion of Palestinians, or disastrous strategic consequences.

The serious distinction is this:

> Conditions of oppression can explain why armed resistance emerges. They do not automatically legitimate every actor, tactic, target, or political project that emerges from those conditions.

The left should be capable of this distinction.

Too often, it is not.

## 🛡️ Defence As Public Duty

A lawful democratic state has coercive responsibilities.

That is uncomfortable.

It is also true.

A state that cannot defend its people, territory, infrastructure, allies, waters, energy systems, ports, cables, hospitals, communications, food supply, and democratic institutions is not morally pure.

It is vulnerable.

And vulnerability is not evenly distributed.

Comfortable people can experience defence as rhetoric.

Bombed cities cannot.

Port towns cannot.

Service families cannot.

Border communities cannot.

Diaspora communities with memories of invasion, pogrom, partition, fascism, siege, colonial violence, or state collapse cannot.

People from places that got bombed do not necessarily experience defence as a Tory hobby.

They may experience it as the thing that was missing, late, misused, captured, betrayed, or desperately needed.

## 🧱 Working-Class Military Geography

A lot of British working-class geography is military geography.

Naval towns.

Garrison towns.

Dockyards.

Airbases.

Old industrial supply chains.

Veterans’ families.

Cadet units.

Service housing.

Repair yards.

Ports.

Logistics corridors.

Places where the armed forces are not abstract symbols but employers, neighbours, relatives, friends, exes, rugby coaches, landlords, tenants, and people in the queue at Greggs.

When Labour or the wider left sounds embarrassed by defence, these communities hear something sharper than policy caution.

They hear:

> You do not understand our lives.

That is one reason right-populist defence language can land even when it is crude, nostalgic, or dangerous.

It fills an emotional and civic vacancy.

If the left cannot speak seriously about protection, other people will.

## 🌀 The Consent Problem

This is not only a defence problem.

It is a power problem.

British public life keeps producing scandals where the recurring injury is consent being treated as administratively negotiable.

Different domains, same grammar:

- war without properly informed consent;
- safeguarding failures hidden behind institutional reputation;
- sexual abuse enabled by status;
- surveillance expanded through security language;
- policing overreach dressed as order;
- public inquiries delayed until the damage is already historic;
- intelligence and secrecy used to narrow what the public is allowed to know.

The public does not need a PhD in constitutional theory to recognise the pattern.

Someone had power.

Someone else carried the consequence.

The affected person, group, or public was not properly asked, informed, protected, or believed.

That is why consent is not only a sexual ethics concept.

It is a democratic ethics concept.

## 🧯 The Elite Failure Loop

The most corrosive pattern is not individual scandal.

It is recurrence.

The same institutional class repeatedly asks to be trusted on:

- war,
- intelligence,
- safeguarding,
- secrecy,
- policing,
- defence,
- public protection,
- institutional self-review,
- and lessons learned.

Then, when things fail, the defence is often some version of:

> Nobody powerful knew enough, soon enough, clearly enough, to act.

This has become a civic joke because it has become a civic pattern.

The British establishment repeatedly discovers, with theatrical surprise, that consent and accountability are not decorative add-ons.

They are the legitimacy mechanism.

## 🧭 Why Messaging Cannot Fix This

Labour-adjacent politics often treats defence as a messaging hazard before treating it as a governance obligation.

That produces familiar framings:

- defence as jobs;
- defence as patriotism;
- defence as reassurance to swing voters;
- defence as NATO credibility;
- defence as veterans care;
- defence as industrial strategy;
- defence as “national renewal.”

These are not wrong.

They are insufficient.

They do not answer the deeper legitimacy question:

> Why should the public trust the same political class to govern force when previous uses of force damaged public trust so badly?

That cannot be solved by a slogan.

It requires a covenant.

## ⚖️ A Democratic Defence Covenant

A serious left defence position would not begin with military theatre.

It would begin with democratic constraints.

A credible covenant might include:

- no wars of choice without clear lawful basis;
- no intelligence presentation designed to sell a pre-decided policy;
- no open-ended intervention without defined aims, review points, exit conditions, and parliamentary accountability;
- no humanitarian language used as cover for strategic adventurism;
- no blank cheques for allies;
- no arms exports where there is clear risk of atrocity, repression, or unlawful use;
- no hiding civilian harm, mission creep, contractor capture, or dependency on foreign systems;
- no treating Parliament as a theatre for decisions already made elsewhere.

But it would also say yes.

Yes to territorial defence.

Yes to civil resilience.

Yes to support for invaded states.

Yes to lawful deterrence.

Yes to infrastructure protection.

Yes to cyber defence.

Yes to maritime security.

Yes to trained personnel.

Yes to munitions capacity.

Yes to maintenance.

Yes to democratic oversight of force.

Yes to preventing fascists, authoritarians, aggressors, and war criminals from learning that democracies are too embarrassed to defend themselves.

## 🧬 The Serious Left Position

The serious position is not anti-defence.

It is anti-abuse of power.

It says:

> Democratic force must be lawful, accountable, necessary, proportionate, strategically literate, publicly consented, and directed toward protection rather than domination.

That is not weakness.

That is the only version of force a democracy should trust itself with.

It also says:

> The fact that force can be abused does not remove the need for protection. It increases the need for democratic control over protection.

This is where parts of the left have failed.

They remember militarism so vividly that they forget defence.

They remember Iraq so vividly that they struggle to speak about Ukraine.

They remember imperial violence so vividly that they forget civilians also need protection from anti-democratic, fascist, authoritarian, terrorist, or expansionist violence.

That forgetting leaves ordinary people exposed.

It also leaves the political field open to those who are very happy to talk about defence without law, restraint, truthfulness, or care.

## 🛠️ Diagnostic Questions

When assessing any left, Green, Labour, Liberal, conservative, or populist defence position, ask:

1. Does it distinguish defence from militarism?
2. Does it distinguish lawful protection from wars of choice?
3. Does it name the Iraq legitimacy injury honestly?
4. Does it explain when force is legitimate?
5. Does it explain when force is not legitimate?
6. Does it include public and parliamentary consent?
7. Does it include civilian harm tracking?
8. Does it include procurement and contractor accountability?
9. Does it understand working-class military geography?
10. Does it understand bombed-city, diaspora, anti-fascist, and service-family memory?
11. Does it avoid using “jobs” as a substitute for moral legitimacy?
12. Does it avoid using “peace” as a substitute for strategic reality?
13. Does it protect against surveillance creep disguised as resilience?
14. Does it treat consent as structural, not decorative?

If the answer is no, the policy is probably not serious yet.

## 🕊️ The Rebuild

The left does not need to become the right on defence.

It needs to remember its own better traditions:

- anti-fascism;
- trade-union industrial competence;
- international solidarity;
- civilian protection;
- lawful restraint;
- anti-imperial critique;
- democratic accountability;
- public goods;
- care for working people;
- suspicion of elite secrecy;
- resistance to authoritarian domination.

Those traditions can support a serious defence policy.

But only if the left stops treating defence as either embarrassing Tory property or a messaging hazard to be softened through industrial strategy language.

Defence is not optional.

Militarism is not acceptable.

Consent is not decorative.

A democratic society has to be able to say all three.
