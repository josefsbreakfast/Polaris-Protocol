# 🎭 Apparently, This Was Our Idea  
**First created:** 2025-12-23 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-11  
*A satirical note on leadership, consent, institutional drift, and the strange habit of obeying imaginary instructions.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation  

This node is satire.

It is not satire about suffering.  
It is not satire about hunger strikers.  
It is not satire about people facing genuine harm.

People’s lives are on the line, and that is not funny.

What *is* satirically absurd — in the bleak, British sense — is how easily democratic societies can begin treating:
- political pressure as natural law,
- institutional paralysis as inevitability,
- and avoidable decisions as if they arrived from nowhere at all.

This note is aimed primarily at leadership cultures.

But the public is not entirely off the hook either.

---

## 🧠 The Imaginary Memo Problem  

At some point in many legitimacy crises, a strange atmosphere emerges.

A large number of officials, commentators, institutions, and observers begin behaving as if they have all received the same invisible instruction sheet:

> “Due to circumstances that will remain vague,  
> certain conversations are now inappropriate,  
> certain harms are now unavoidable,  
> and certain moral questions are now too complicated to address directly.  
>  
> Please behave as though this conclusion emerged naturally and independently.  
> Thank you.”

And instead of collectively asking:
- who issued the memo,
- where it came from,
- why nobody voted on it,
- or why it sounds like a hostage note written by exhausted consultants,

many people simply nod along as though this is the obvious and mature position.

This is one of the quieter absurdities of modern governance.

---

## 🏛️ Leadership and the Performance of Powerlessness  

There is a particular style of contemporary leadership language built around managed helplessness.

It sounds like:

> “My hands are tied.”

Curiously, this formulation rarely explains:
- who tied them,
- when this occurred,
- whether consent was involved,
- or why holding office apparently did not include resisting the tying process.

Instead, responsibility dissolves into vague abstractions:
- “the climate,”
- “the situation,”
- “the pressures,”
- “security concerns,”
- “market realities,”
- “operational constraints,”
- or “what people will accept.”

These forces are often described as though they were weather systems rather than human-made institutional conditions.

Satirically speaking, it is an impressive achievement:
to occupy positions of authority while narrating oneself primarily as a hostage to events.

---

## 🗳️ The Public Bit (Regretfully)  

The public also participates in this dynamic more often than we admit.

Many people have, at one point or another, thought something like:

- “This feels wrong, but I assume someone important has decided it’s necessary.”
- “I dislike this, but perhaps there are things happening behind the scenes.”
- “I suppose this is just how things work now.”
- “I don’t want to sound naïve or irresponsible.”

Which is a curious conclusion to reach in a democracy,
given that democratic legitimacy is supposedly derived from public participation rather than atmospheric resignation.

It resembles standing in your own living room,
being instructed to sit on the floor,
and deciding:
> “Well. There must be a procedure behind this.”

---

## 🧠 Institutional Drift and Passive Consent  

One of the central mechanisms of democratic erosion is not dramatic authoritarian takeover.

It is passive procedural drift.

Systems gradually normalise:
- silence,
- avoidance,
- managed ambiguity,
- moral deferral,
- and the outsourcing of agency to unnamed external pressures.

Over time:
- intimidation begins to resemble caution,
- helplessness begins to resemble professionalism,
- and public disengagement begins to resemble stability.

This creates environments where:
- nobody explicitly claims responsibility,
while
- harmful outcomes continue anyway.

The resulting atmosphere is politically dangerous because it allows:
- agency to disappear rhetorically before it disappears structurally.

---

## 🎭 The Core Satirical Mechanism  

The core joke — if one has a dark enough sense of humour — is not that pressure exists.

Pressure always exists.

The joke is that democratic societies sometimes behave as though:
- pressure automatically becomes authority,
- silence automatically becomes wisdom,
- and institutional inertia automatically becomes necessity.

As though democracy were a weather condition rather than an active civic process.

At a certain point, governance rhetoric can begin sounding less like leadership and more like:
> “Well, everyone seemed to agree that nobody could do anything, so naturally we continued doing nothing.”

---

## 📺 Respectability as Containment  

A recurring feature of legitimacy crises is the use of “reasonableness” as a containment mechanism.

People are often encouraged to:
- stay calm,
- avoid overreaction,
- trust procedure,
- wait for process,
- and avoid making situations “more difficult.”

Sometimes this is entirely sensible.

Sometimes it becomes a way of endlessly postponing moral clarity while maintaining the appearance of maturity.

This creates a peculiar social atmosphere where:
- visible concern becomes framed as emotional excess,
- direct language becomes framed as irresponsibility,
- and institutional discomfort becomes treated as a higher priority than institutional failure itself.

---

## ⚖️ The Non-Satirical Line  

Here is where the satire stops.

When people place themselves at serious physical risk:
- through hunger strike,
- protest,
- exposure,
- or other acts of desperation,

the relevant question is not:
> “Is this politically awkward?”  
> “Is this inconvenient?”  
> “Will this create reputational pressure?”

The relevant question becomes:
> “Are we genuinely prepared to argue that nobody retained agency here?”

Because that is the point where democratic absurdity stops being comic and becomes morally serious.

---

## 🧩 What This Satire Is Actually For  

This node exists to make one behaviour slightly more embarrassing:

pretending that avoidable political choices are acts of nature.

Its purpose is not outrage theatre.

Its purpose is civic re-humanisation.

A reminder that:
- pressure only functions as authority when treated as authority,
- silence only functions as consent when accepted as consent,
- and “we had no choice” is often something institutions say after choices have already been made.

Satire matters here because humour can sometimes restore perspective faster than formal argument.

Especially in systems where absurdity has become bureaucratically normalised.

---

## 🕯️ Final Grounding  

If you ever catch yourself thinking:
> “Well, I suppose this is simply how things have to be now…”

it is worth asking:

> “According to whom?”  
> “By what mechanism?”  
> “Since when?”  
> “Who benefits from this feeling of inevitability?”  
> “And why am I behaving as though this was my idea all along?”

Democratic decline rarely arrives announcing itself dramatically.

More often, it appears as:
- procedural resignation,
- exhausted politeness,
- managed helplessness,
- and a growing social agreement to stop asking who still has agency.
