### 2. Diagnosis as the pressure point  
Diagnosis is the weak joint the script exploits.

Because diagnosis involves:
- clinical judgement  
- thresholds rather than binaries  
- lived experience  
- contextual interpretation  

it is easy to portray as:
- subjective  
- ideological  
- lax  
- captured by activists  

This framing converts healthcare uncertainty into political suspicion.

### 3. Selective advocacy and regret narratives  
A recurring tactic is the elevation of a narrow set of voices:
- people who regret diagnosis or treatment  
- those harmed by system failure  
- individuals whose stories can be generalised  

These experiences are real.  
Their instrumentalisation is the problem.

They are used to:
- justify restriction for everyone  
- undermine access broadly  
- override the voices of those helped  

This is not safeguarding.  
It is narrative leverage.

- worsening mental health  
- increased crisis presentation  
- preventable suffering  

Protection becomes containment.

### 5. Why these panics surface when they do  
These scripts reliably intensify during periods of:
- economic stress  
- institutional failure  
- declining trust in governance  
- rapid social change  

Moral panic offers:
- a visible target  
- emotional mobilisation  
- distraction from structural causes  

Disability and trans communities are targeted not because they cause instability, but because they are politically exploitable.

### 6. Difference is framed as threat  
Both disabled and trans people disrupt:
- neat productivity models  
- binary norms  
- ideas of bodily control  

Rather than adapting systems, power reframes difference as:
- excess  
- confusion  
- danger  

Classification then becomes a tool of discipline rather than care.

The harm is cumulative, even if each debate is framed as isolated.
