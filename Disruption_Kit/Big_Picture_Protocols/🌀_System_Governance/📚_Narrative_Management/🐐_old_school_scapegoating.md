# 🐐 Old-School Scapegoating  
**First created:** 2025-12-17 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-09  
*Why modern institutions still perform an ancient ritual — just without naming it.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation  
This node compares **historical scapegoating rituals** with **modern institutional scapegoating**, focusing on function rather than morality.

The aim is not to condemn ritual, but to show how its *mechanics* persist — stripped of ethical framing, transparency, and collective responsibility.  

(I am not advocating we start slaughtering goats annually again; we are discussing communal emotional processing.)  

---

## ✨ Key Features  
- Compares ritual scapegoating with contemporary institutional practice  
- Explains why scapegoating appears during liability collapse  
- Distinguishes containment from accountability  
- Clarifies why “truth” often emerges suddenly and partially  

---

## 🐐 The Original Ritual (Briefly, Without Mythologising)

In the ancient Jewish Yom Kippur ritual (*se’ir la-Azazel*):

- Harm and wrongdoing were **acknowledged as collective**
- Responsibility was **named without being individualised**
- A single vessel carried the symbolic burden
- The vessel was expelled
- Social order was restored

Crucially:
- The scapegoat was not “guilty”
- The ritual was explicit, bounded, and understood as symbolic
- The community knew this was **containment**, not truth-finding

The ritual was honest about what it was doing.

---

## 🏛️ Modern Institutional Scapegoating  

In modern institutions facing severe harm or criminal exposure:

- Harm is acknowledged only once denial becomes untenable  
- Responsibility is rapidly narrowed  
- A single individual is framed as the problem  
- That individual is removed, disciplined, or prosecuted  
- The system declares itself corrected  

Unlike the ritual:
- The symbolism is denied  
- The process is framed as justice or discovery  
- Structural responsibility is minimised or erased  

The function, however, is the same:  
**liability containment through concentration of blame**.

| Ancient Ritual | Modern Institution |

| Burden symbolically concentrated | Responsibility individualised |
| Ritual explicitly named | Ritual denied as such |
| Community knows it’s symbolic | Framed as full accountability |

## ⚖️ Containment vs Accountability  

Scapegoating — ancient or modern — is about **containment**, not accountability.

Containment answers:
- “How do we survive this?”
- “How do we restore order quickly?”
- “How do we limit spread?”

Accountability would require:
- Cross-context verification  
- Structural self-examination  
- Distributed responsibility  
- Ongoing repair rather than closure  

Most institutions choose containment when survival is at stake.

---

## 🔥 Why Collapse Feels Sudden  

When one party moves first (self-reports, leaks, cooperates):

- The incentive structure flips  
- Silence becomes liability  
- Distance becomes survival  

This produces:
- Rapid scapegoat selection  
- Sudden cooperation  
- Accelerated narrative finalisation  

What looks like moral awakening is usually **risk re-optimisation**.

---

## 🧠 Why This Matters for Interpretation  

Understanding scapegoating as ritual explains why:
- Institutions deny harm for long periods, then act overnight  
- “Truth” emerges late and incompletely  
- Survivors feel both vindicated and erased  
- Lessons learned are narrow and procedural  
- The same patterns repeat elsewhere  

The ritual works.  
That is why it persists.
