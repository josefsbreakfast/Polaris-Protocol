# 🥔 Potatoes Not Bombs  
**First created:** 2026-05-29 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-29  
*An anti-war-consent node on material repair, classed sacrifice, bombardment inheritance, and why people with bombing memory may not be an easy audience for bombing campaigns.*

---

## 🧭 Orientation  

This node examines a failure in war-consent messaging: the assumption that people with inherited memories of bombardment, rationing, labour extraction, underprotection, and postwar neglect will naturally identify with later appeals for military escalation.

The argument is simple:

> People whose families were bombed, under-sheltered, rationed, worked, and then told to call survival “character” may not respond to bombing campaigns with patriotic consent.

They may respond with suspicion.

They may hear the appeal as:

> “Here, let us reenact how your grannies were left trying not to die. You like this, right?”

This is not a failure of empathy. It may be empathy operating in the wrong direction for the comms strategy.

The response is not:

> “Bomb harder.”

It is:

> “Feed people. House people. Heat people. Repair the damage. Stop asking the sacrifice class to bless another bill being sent to someone else’s body.”

Potatoes, not bombs.

---

## 🥔 Core Thesis  

War-consent messaging often assumes that civilian suffering can be used to authorise military violence. But for communities with inherited bombardment memory, civilian suffering may do the opposite.

It may not produce consent.

It may produce refusal.

The sacrifice class does not need another speech about necessary violence. It needs food, shelter, heating, trauma care, reconstruction, public services, and honest accounting for who is expected to absorb the consequences of state decisions.

The value of a life is not declared in speeches.  
It is revealed in infrastructure.

---

## 💣 The Bombardment Inheritance  

Bombardment does not end when the sirens stop.

It continues through:

- housing damage,
- rationing,
- industrial exposure,
- family silence,
- regional underinvestment,
- nervous-system inheritance,
- women’s unpaid stabilising labour,
- men returning changed,
- children learning war vocabulary before ordinary childhood language,
- and generations being taught not to make a fuss.

The public story may become “resilience.”

The lived inheritance may be:

> “Do not collapse where the system can see you.”

This node treats that inheritance as politically relevant. It shapes how later appeals to bombing, sheltering, sacrifice, and civilian fear are received.

---

## 🏚️ Shelter Was Not Safety  

Air-raid shelters were not proof that civilians were safe.

They were proof that civilian life had been reorganised around managed survivability.

Some people slept in shelters pre-emptively for nights at a time.  
Some people refused to leave their houses during raids.  
Some people knew shelters could be flattened.  
Some workers could plausibly feel safer underground than above it.

When all options are unsafe, people choose the danger they can bear.

This complicates later imagery of civilians in shelters. A shelter is not automatically evidence of adequate protection. It can also be evidence that ordinary life has been forced to live around the expectation of violence.  

This can become a, "fact of life", and swiftly normalised.

## 🧱 Classed Protection  

The home front was not equally sheltered.

Some families had:

- cellars,
- rural evacuation options,
- second homes,
- money,
- mobility,
- influence,
- better housing,
- and more distance from strategic targets.

Others lived near:

- shipyards,
- docks,
- coalfields,
- steelworks,
- rail infrastructure,
- factories,
- ports,
- munitions production,
- anti-invasion coastlines,
- and other wartime targets.

The question is not only:

> “Who was bombed?”

It is also:

> “Who was expected to keep producing while being bombed?”

And:

> “How much did the state value your family’s life over mine?”

## 🫖 The Convince Class And The Sacrifice Class  

War is often narrated by the convince class and carried by the sacrifice class.

The convince class says:

> “This is regrettable but necessary.”  
> “This is about security.”  
> “This is about values.”  
> “Think of the women.”  
> “Think of the civilians.”  
> “Support the difficult decision.”

The sacrifice class hears:

> “Whose street?”  
> “Whose child?”  
> “Whose lungs?”  
> “Whose housing?”  
> “Whose nervous system?”  
> “Whose public services later?”  
> “Whose family gets told to be proud instead of repaired?”

The convince class mistakes sacrifice for a resource it can narrate.

The sacrifice class knows sacrifice as a bill that gets sent to the wrong address.

---

## 🕳️ The Misread  

The comms failure is not that people lack compassion.

It is that they may have too much historically informed compassion for the wrong part of the state’s argument.

They empathise with:

- civilians under bombs,
- people in shelters,
- families sleeping underground,
- migrants housed in emergency infrastructure,
- workers told to keep going,
- soldiers fed into traumatic systems,
- women expected to hold households together,
- and regions left with the afterlife of violence.

They do not necessarily empathise with:

- the state asking to bomb,
- the lobby asking for consent,
- the official insisting this is necessary,
- or the media class turning civilian fear into moral leverage.

This is why “but foreigners” does not solve the problem.

Bombing civilians abroad does not stop looking like bombing civilians because the civilians are abroad.

---

## 🏚️ Recycled Underprotection  

The afterlife of wartime infrastructure matters.

Air-raid shelters, emergency housing, cold temporary accommodation, postwar scarcity, and underbuilt welcome systems reveal a recurring governance pattern:

> expose people, underprovide, then narrate endurance as character.

This applies differently across different histories and should not flatten distinct harms. But the pattern recurs often enough to matter.

Emergency survivability infrastructure can become peacetime housing.  
Temporary underprotection can become normalised settlement.  
A lack of dignity can be reframed as resilience.

This is not repair.

It is exposure management.

## 🧿 Diagnostic Questions  

Ask:

- Who is being asked to consent?
- What is their inherited relationship to bombing?
- What did their family or region carry last time?
- Was their sacrifice repaired or mythologised?
- Who got shelter, mobility, protection, and rebuilding?
- Who got character instead?
- Is civilian fear being used to protect civilians, or to authorise more violence?
- Who benefits from turning empathy into military consent?
- Who will carry the afterlife of the bombs?
- What would material care look like instead?

## 🥔 Potatoes  

“Potatoes” here means the unglamorous material basis of care.

Food.  
Heating.  
Housing.  
Shelter.  
Reconstruction.  
Asylum.  
Medical care.  
Trauma support.  
Public services.  
Legal accountability.  
Ordinary repair.

Potatoes are not sentimental.

They are the refusal of grand violence as moral theatre.

They ask:

> What would happen if the same urgency used to justify bombs were used to keep people alive, housed, fed, warm, and repaired?

---

## ⚖️ Boundary Note  

This node does not claim that all wartime experiences are equivalent.

It does not collapse the Blitz, the Holocaust, Gaza, Iraq, Afghanistan, Ukraine, the Windrush generation, Irish migration, mining communities, or regional British underinvestment into one identical category.

The point is not equivalence.

The point is pattern recognition:

> people who inherit histories of exposure, underprotection, and state-managed sacrifice may not be easy to persuade with narratives that ask them to bless someone else’s exposure, underprotection, and sacrifice.
