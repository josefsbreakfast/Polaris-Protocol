## Summary

This node explains how contemporary UK policy-making increasingly operates on **curated representations of reality** rather than direct, contestable engagement with the world.

Dashboards, analytics platforms, AI-assisted briefings, and algorithmic media feeds do not merely summarise information.  
They **prioritise, filter, and frame** what decision-makers believe is happening â€” and therefore what they believe must be done.

---

## From Evidence to Feed

Historically, policy formation relied on:
- reports
- deliberation
- civil service synthesis
- external expertise
- political judgement

Today, this process is increasingly mediated by:
- dashboards
- risk summaries
- automated alerts
- AI-assisted briefings
- engagement-driven media aggregation

The result is not more knowledge, but **compressed knowledge**.

What survives compression appears urgent.  
What does not, disappears.

---

## How Curation Works

Curated policy reality is shaped by systems that:
- rank issues by salience
- privilege measurable indicators
- reward escalation
- collapse uncertainty into signals
- filter out low-visibility harm

These systems answer implicit questions such as:
- What needs attention *now*?
- What is worsening?
- What looks risky?
- What is politically legible?

They do not ask:
- What is missing?
- Who is unseen?
- What harm is slow or cumulative?
- What would restraint look like?

## Media Feedback Loops

The problem is reinforced by changes in media production.

As news organisations rely more heavily on:
- algorithmic aggregation
- engagement optimisation
- AI-assisted summarisation

media outputs increasingly mirror the same signals policymakers see.

This creates a loop:
- policy feeds influence media narratives
- media narratives reinforce policy feeds
- both drift away from lived experience

Reality becomes self-referential.

---

## Migration, Security, and Threat Saturation

Curated reality is especially powerful in domains framed as security issues:
- migration
- borders
- foreign interference
- protest
- cyber threats

In these areas:
- risk signals are amplified
- absence is treated as suspicion
- uncertainty is framed as danger
- dissent is reframed as noise

Policy responses become **reactive to representations**, not conditions.

---

## Why This Environment Is Easily Manipulated

Curated systems are vulnerable because:
- they prioritise speed over verification
- they rely on upstream data integrity
- they reward emotionally salient signals
- they lack transparency and contestability

Cyber disruption, misinformation, and partial data can:
- distort perception
- escalate false urgency
- suppress countervailing evidence

No hacking is required to shape outcomes.  
Influencing the feed is often enough.

---

## The Illusion of Responsiveness

Curated reality gives the impression of:
- attentiveness
- responsiveness
- decisiveness

But it often produces:
- policy churn
- misaligned priorities
- performative action
- neglect of slow-burn harms

What looks like competence can mask **epistemic closure**.

---

## Why This Matters for Democracy

Democratic governance depends on:
- shared reality
- contestable evidence
- plural perspectives
- tolerance for uncertainty

When reality is mediated through opaque systems:
- disagreement appears irrational
- critique appears obstructive
- rights appear inconvenient
- alternatives appear invisible

Democracy degrades not through censorship, but through **over-curation**.
