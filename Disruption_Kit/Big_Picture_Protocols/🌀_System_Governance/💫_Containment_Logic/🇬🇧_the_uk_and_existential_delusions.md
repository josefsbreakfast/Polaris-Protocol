### What â€œExistentialâ€ Should Mean  
In constitutional and international law, existential threat is narrow:
- Imminent threat to territorial integrity  
- Collapse of constitutional order  
- Mass civilian danger that cannot be mitigated by ordinary means  

Most alliance disputes, diplomatic breaks, or policy divergences do **not** meet this threshold.

---

### Existential Misclassification  
Existential misclassification occurs when a state internally redefines:
- Alliance friction  
- Intelligence dependency  
- Reputational risk  
- Political embarrassment  

as threats to *state survival*.

Once this happens:
- Safeguards are reframed as luxuries  
- Oversight becomes obstruction  
- Harm is discounted as unavoidable  
- Dissent is treated as danger  

This produces rigidity rather than resilience.

---

### The UK Pattern  
In recent governance contexts, the UK appears to:
- Treat alignment with the US as non-deviable  
- Treat deviation on Israel/Palestine as reputationally catastrophic  
- Treat security posture as identity, not policy  

This framing creates a false binary:
> *Hold the line at any cost*  
> or  
> *Lose credibility altogether*

That binary is not imposed by law. It is **institutionally generated**.

---

### Domestic Spillover  
Existential framing rarely stays confined to foreign policy. It bleeds into:
- Policing and protest management  
- Prison governance  
- Medical override decisions  
- Oversight refusal  

Where life, care, and rights become subordinate to posture, the state signals that some harms are now tolerable.

---

### Why This Is Dangerous  
States that misclassify threats tend to:
- Overestimate the cost of restraint  
- Underestimate long-term damage  
- Confuse loyalty with legitimacy  
- Manage fallout rather than prevent harm  

History shows this pattern precedes:
- Rights erosion  
- Loss of moral authority  
- International credibility decline  
- Internal radicalisation, not stability  

---

Labeling a policy position â€œexistentialâ€ often functions to:
