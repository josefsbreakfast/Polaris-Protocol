# 🛰️ Orientation  

*🍪 Biscuit Translation Table + Field Pronunciation Guide* satirises:
- bureaucratic multilingualism,
- international coordination failures,
- translation ambiguity,
- and the absurd consequences of applying security logic to culturally variable food terminology.

Presented as a mock operational language guide, the node exaggerates the genuine linguistic complexity surrounding:
- biscuits,
- cookies,
- crackers,
- sponge cakes,
- and related baked classifications
across multiple cultures and languages.

The humour emerges from the idea that:
> international snack diplomacy repeatedly collapses because nobody fully agrees what a biscuit actually is.

At the same time, the piece lightly reflects real linguistic phenomena including:
- false friends,
- semantic drift,
- loanwords,
- regional food classification,
- and culturally specific culinary categories.

Within the ABDD setting, these ambiguities become operational hazards capable of triggering:
- diplomatic incidents,
- algorithmic misclassification,
- or accidental counter-snack escalation.

# ⚠️ False Friends Appendix  

## 🇺🇸 United States  

- “Biscuit” = savoury bread roll, not sweet biscuit.  

---

## 🇬🇧 United Kingdom  

- “Biscuit” = sweet baked item equivalent to the American “cookie.”  

---

## 🇫🇷 French  

- “Biscuit” may refer to:
  - cookie,
  - cracker,
  - or sponge cake,
depending on context.  

---

## 🇩🇪 German  

- “Biskuit” refers to sponge cake rather than cookie.  

---

## 🇪🇸 Spanish  

- *Galleta salada* indicates savoury crackers rather than sweet biscuits.  

---

## 🇬🇷 Greek  

- *Κουλουράκι (koulouráki)* refers to a traditional ring biscuit rather than generic *μπισκότο*.  

---

## 🇮🇱 Hebrew  

- ביסקוויט (*biskvit*) = sponge cake.  
- Actual cookie/biscuit = עוגייה (*ugiyah*).  

---

## 🕎 Yiddish  

- ביסקויט (*biskvit*) = sponge cake.  
- Cookie/pastry = קיכל (*kikhl*).
