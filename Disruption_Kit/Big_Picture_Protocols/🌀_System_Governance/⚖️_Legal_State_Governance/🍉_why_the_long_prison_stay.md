## ‚öñÔ∏è Bail Is The Legal Baseline  
Under the **Bail Act 1976**, the default position is release on bail unless the prosecution can demonstrate substantial grounds for detention.

In protest-related and political cases, courts routinely use:
- strict bail conditions,
- protest bans,
- non-association orders,
- curfews,
- reporting requirements,
- electronic monitoring.

Custody is meant to be **exceptional**, not routine.

---

## üß± Why Remand Becomes Prolonged  
In cases charged under **counter-terrorism legislation**, several factors combine to extend remand:

- complex evidence and disclosure processes  
- digital forensics backlogs  
- risk-averse prosecutorial strategy  
- repeated extensions of custody time limits  
- institutional reluctance to be seen as ‚Äútaking risk‚Äù  

Each extension may be lawful in isolation.  
Together, they can produce **de facto punishment before trial**.

---

## ‚è≥ How Unusual Is Long-Term Remand?  
While short remand periods are common in serious cases, **remand extending toward or beyond 18‚Äì24 months** is:

- legally sensitive,  
- increasingly difficult to justify, and  
- closely scrutinised under Article 5 ECHR (right to liberty).

At this point, the burden on the state is high.

## üí∞ What Detention Costs The Taxpayer  
Detention is expensive ‚Äî especially under enhanced security.

Indicative figures (rounded, conservative estimates):

- **Standard prison place:** ~¬£50,000 per person per year  
- **High-security / specialist regimes:** ¬£60,000‚Äì¬£80,000+ per year  
- **Additional costs:**  
  - prison healthcare  
  - mental health services  
  - hunger strike monitoring  
  - legal oversight and reviews  

A prolonged remand of two years can easily exceed **¬£120,000‚Äì¬£160,000 per person**, before trial.

## üè∑Ô∏è What Bail Costs The Taxpayer  
By contrast, bail with conditions typically involves:

- probation or police supervision  
- electronic monitoring (where used)  
- reporting requirements  

Indicative annual costs:

- **Standard bail supervision:** low thousands per year  
- **Electronic tagging:** ~¬£3,000‚Äì¬£5,000 per year  

Even at the upper end, bail costs are **a fraction of custodial detention**.

## üß† Incentives And Risk Aversion  
Why, then, does detention persist?

Because:
- **institutional risk aversion** favours containment over discretion,  
- no single actor bears the full cost of delay,  
- and political sensitivity discourages early release.

The taxpayer absorbs the cost.  
The detainee absorbs the harm.

---

## üö® Why Hunger Strikes Emerge Here  
Hunger strikes often arise when:
- bail applications fail repeatedly,  
- timelines stretch indefinitely,  
- and remand begins to feel like a sentence.

They are not a shortcut.  
They are a **signal of procedural exhaustion**.

---

## üß© What This Reveals  
Prolonged remand is not:
- cheaper,
- safer,
- or more efficient.

It is:
- fiscally costly,  
- legally fragile,  
- and ethically corrosive.

When bail could achieve the same risk management at a fraction of the cost, extended detention becomes a **governance choice**, not a necessity.

## üó£Ô∏è Narrative Distortion, Security Failure, And Collective Punishment

A recurring pattern in press and political discourse has been the treatment of a **single extreme incident** ‚Äî the unauthorised entry onto an RAF base and the spraying of paint into an aircraft engine ‚Äî as if it were **representative of all Palestine Action cases**.

This is factually misleading.

In reality, the majority of cases involving Palestine Action relate to:
- damage to **private property**,
- symbolic or disruptive acts at **arms factories or corporate premises**,
- actions such as removing roof tiles, occupying buildings, or blocking access.

These actions are **not equivalent**, legally or practically, to breaching a military installation.

---

## üêç Worst-Case Generalisation As Narrative Strategy

The RAF base incident has been repeatedly foregrounded to:
- elevate perceived threat levels across the entire group,
- justify terrorism framing and proscription,
- normalise prolonged remand and denial of bail,
- and flatten hundreds of distinct cases into a single extreme archetype.

This is a classic **worst-case generalisation** tactic:
one exceptional event is used to define all related activity.

Historically, this approach has been used against:
- suffragettes,
- trade unionists,
- Irish activists,
- and environmental movements.

The effect is always the same:  
**erase scale, erase diversity, erase proportionality**.

---

## ‚úàÔ∏è The Missing Question: Security Failure

If activists were able to:
- access a RAF base,
- enter restricted areas,
- and interfere with military equipment,

then this represents a **serious security failure**.

In any other national security context, the primary public questions would be:
- How was access gained?
- Why was the perimeter not secure?
- Why was detection delayed or absent?
- What remedial action has been taken?
- Has the vulnerability been fixed?

Instead, public discourse has focused almost exclusively on:
- punishment,
- sentencing,
- proscription,
- and extended detention.

The absence of parallel discussion about **security remediation** is striking.

---

## üî• Punishment As Embarrassment Management

Even for those who support proscription, it is notable that:
- no sustained public reassurance has been offered that the security failure has been addressed,
- no transparent account has been given of how the breach occurred,
- and accountability has been displaced entirely onto protesters.

This suggests that punishment is being used to:
- redirect attention away from institutional failure,
- reassert control symbolically,
- and close down scrutiny.

Punishment manages embarrassment more effectively than it improves security.

---

## üß® Why This Matters For Remand And Hunger Strikes

This narrative compression directly feeds into:
- elevated threat perception,
- prolonged remand,
- denial of bail,
- and public tolerance for extreme measures.

If the public is encouraged to believe that:
‚ÄúAll cases are equivalent to the RAF base incident,‚Äù

then:
- exceptional detention appears justified,
- nuance disappears,
- and hunger strikes can be dismissed as the irrational actions of ‚Äúextremists‚Äù.

This framing does not reflect the factual diversity of cases.
It serves administrative and political convenience.

---

## ü™º The Legitimate Public Interest Question

A democratic and security-literate response would ask:

If a military base was breached,  
**why are we not publicly accounting for how that failure occurred and how it has been fixed?**

That question is:
- non-ideological,
- fiscally responsible,
- and squarely in the public interest.

Its absence should concern anyone serious about security, proportionality, and governance.

---

## üè• Austerity And NHS Underfunding: Opportunity Cost

This level of expenditure sits uncomfortably alongside persistent claims of **public-sector scarcity**, including:
- NHS funding shortfalls,
- long waiting lists,
- delayed access to mental health services,
- and underfunded community care.

Using the most conservative estimate:
- **¬£400,000 per year** for eight people on remand,
- **¬£800,000** if remand extends to two years,

this spending represents **real opportunity cost**, not abstract accounting.

For example, comparable sums could fund:
- multiple full-time nurses or paramedics for a year,
- community mental health practitioners,
- early-intervention services that reduce A&E admissions,
- or backlog-reduction capacity within the Crown Court system itself.

Instead, public money is being absorbed by:
- prolonged pre-trial detention,
- enhanced security regimes,
- prison healthcare escalation,
- and repeated legal reviews ‚Äî

all without accelerating trial timelines or improving public safety.

---

## üîÑ Scarcity Is A Political Choice, Not A Natural Law

The repeated claim that ‚Äúthere is no money‚Äù for:
- healthcare,
- courts,
- social care,
- or community services,

sits uneasily beside the willingness to spend hundreds of thousands of pounds on **extended remand for unconvicted individuals**.

This is not fiscal restraint.
It is **selective allocation**.

Scarcity is being imposed downstream, while upstream inefficiency is tolerated.

---

## üßæ What The Taxpayer Actually Gets

From the taxpayer‚Äôs perspective, this expenditure delivers:
- no faster justice,
- no reduction in court backlogs,
- no resolution of the underlying case,
- and increasing legal and humanitarian risk.

The public pays more ‚Äî and gets less.

That contradiction is especially stark during a cost-of-living crisis, where the same public is being told that:
- NHS care must be rationed,
- public-sector pay must be restrained,
- and delays are unavoidable.

---

## üß† Why This Matters

When the state chooses expensive containment over cheaper, lawful alternatives such as bail with conditions, it sends a clear signal:

> money is available for punishment and delay,  
> but not for care, speed, or repair.

That signal does not remain confined to prisons.
It shapes public trust across the entire system.
