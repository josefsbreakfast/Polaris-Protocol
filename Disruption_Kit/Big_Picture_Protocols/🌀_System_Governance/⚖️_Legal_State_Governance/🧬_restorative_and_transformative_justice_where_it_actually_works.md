## ðŸ§  First Principle: Justice Models Must Match Risk Profiles

Restorative and transformative justice (RJ/TJ) are **risk-sensitive tools**.

They are effective when:
- harm is bounded
- power asymmetry is manageable
- denial is absent or declining
- ongoing threat can be contained

They are dangerous when:
- harm is severe and ongoing
- offenders are predatory or compulsive
- power imbalance is extreme
- participation is coerced
- safety cannot be guaranteed

Using RJ/TJ where it does not fit is not compassion.
It is negligence.

## ðŸ§  Transformative Justice: Narrower, Deeper, Riskier

Transformative justice goes further:
- it targets underlying conditions
- it involves social and relational change
- it rejects state monopoly on justice

TJ can work in:
- tightly bounded communities
- highly resourced settings
- contexts with shared norms
- environments with real exit options for participants

It fails when:
- communities are coercive
- silence is culturally enforced
- power is concentrated
- harm threatens life or liberty

TJ is not scalable by default.
Pretending it is undermines trust.

## âš ï¸ Why CSA Requires Extreme Caution

In child sexual abuse (CSA) contexts:
- power asymmetry is structural
- consent is impossible
- harm is profound and long-term
- compulsion and escalation risk is high

RJ/TJ **cannot** replace:
- criminal accountability
- safeguarding restrictions
- monitoring and treatment
- state protection functions

Any RJ/TJ use in CSA contexts must be:
- survivor-led
- optional
- post-safety
- post-accountability
- professionally supervised

Anything else is abuse repackaged as healing.

## ðŸ§  The Consent Problem

RJ/TJ fails instantly if:
- participation is pressured
- outcomes affect sentencing
- survivors fear retaliation
- refusal is penalised socially

Consent must be:
- informed
- reversible
- consequence-free

Otherwise the process reproduces harm under a different name.

## ðŸ”„ The Accountability Requirement

RJ/TJ without accountability:
- rewards minimisation
- incentivises denial
- retraumatises survivors

Effective models require:

- acceptance of responsibility
- consequences for non-compliance
- independent facilitation
- clear exit criteria

Healing does not require punishment.
But it does require **truth and constraint**.
