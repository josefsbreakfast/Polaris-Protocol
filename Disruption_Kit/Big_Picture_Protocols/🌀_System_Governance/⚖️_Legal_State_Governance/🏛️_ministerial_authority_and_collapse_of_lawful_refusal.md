## üèõÔ∏è Ministerial authority (plain language)
In the UK system:

- Ministers decide policy.
- Civil servants and the armed forces **implement** it.
- Responsibility flows **upward**.
- Obedience flows **downward**.

This arrangement underpins:
- civilian control of the military,
- parliamentary accountability,
- and executive coherence.

It also means that **once a decision is cleared ministerially**, downstream actors lose lawful discretion.

## ‚öñÔ∏è The ‚Äúmanifestly unlawful‚Äù threshold
UK doctrine permits refusal **only** where an order is *manifestly unlawful* ‚Äî meaning:

- illegal on its face,
- obvious to a non-lawyer,
- requiring no contextual legal judgement.

Examples:
- orders to kill civilians directly,
- orders to commit torture.

What does *not* meet this threshold:
- intelligence sharing,
- ISR collection,
- arms export processing,
- dual-use licensing,
- policy communications,
- omission or delay.

Most genocide-prevention failures therefore **never appear as refusible acts** to implementers.

### Government legal advice
- Lawyers advise; ministers choose.
- Advice is confidential and non-binding.
- Override leaves no downstream protection.

**Result:** legality speaks, but cannot act.
