## ğŸ§® Trade-Offs in the Current System

The authorisation framework is optimised for particular risks â€” and makes trade-offs in doing so.

### What the System Does Well
- Enables rapid authorisation in high-risk contexts  
- Distributes decision-making authority  
- Creates a formal audit trail  

### What the System Trades Away
- End-to-end responsibility for outcomes  
- Visibility of cumulative or cross-agency harm  
- Clear ownership when downstream harm occurs  

### Who Benefits
- Ministers protected by procedural compliance  
- Agencies shielded from sole liability  
- Courts reviewing narrow questions of legality  

### Who Carries the Cost
- Individuals subject to opaque or long-term surveillance  
- Communities experiencing disproportionate scrutiny  
- Public trust in oversight as meaningful restraint  

These trade-offs are structural, not accidental.

## ğŸ”® Legitimacy, Expectation, and Duration

Public tolerance of surveillance has historically depended on an exchange:  
intrusion in return for intelligible protection.

That tolerance collapses when surveillance:
- intrudes into intimate or associative life,
- persists for years or decades,
- or exceeds what people reasonably expected to be monitored for.

Across multiple inquiries, surveillance is criticised less for initiation than for duration.

Duration itself becomes escalation.  
What once appeared proportionate becomes illegitimate through persistence alone.

## ğŸ«¥ Policing by Consent

UK policing rests on a foundational doctrine: **policing by consent**.

Consent is not approval.  
It is contextual, revisable, and dependent on intelligibility.

Surveillance challenges this doctrine uniquely:  
it is invisible at the point of use, disclosed retrospectively, and difficult to contest in real time.

As a result, many members of the public are now asking a simple question:

**Do we consent to this use of power?**

## ğŸŒ‹ Authorisation Structures and Incentivised Drift
