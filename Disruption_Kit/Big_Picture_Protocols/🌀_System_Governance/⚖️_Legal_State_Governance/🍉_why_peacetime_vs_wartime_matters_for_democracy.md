- which rights apply,

## üß≠ The Core Democratic Distinction  

### üïäÔ∏è Peacetime Democracy  
In peacetime, citizens may lawfully:
- hold and express controversial political views,
- criticise allied or adversarial states,
- rely on international law (including genocide assessments),
- engage in journalism, OSINT, research, and analysis,
- organise, protest, and persuade.

The **burden of justification** lies with the state to restrict these rights.

### ü™ñ Wartime Posture  
In wartime (formally declared or lawfully activated):
- speech may be restricted,
- information may be classified or criminalised,
- alignment may be enforced,
- dissent may be curtailed under emergency powers.

Crucially: **those powers must be declared, bounded, and reviewable**.

---

## üß® The Democratic Hazard: Interpretive Drift  
A central danger arises when:
- institutions *behave* as if wartime applies,
- while law *remains* peacetime.

This produces:
- inconsistent enforcement,
- unclear thresholds,
- self-censorship without legal basis,
- and fear driven by interpretation rather than statute.

Citizens are left asking:
> ‚ÄúAm I being judged by rules that do not officially exist?‚Äù

## üçâ Frame Clash: War vs Credible Genocide  
A specific fault line intensifies this ambiguity:

- **War framing** emphasises:
  - alignment,
  - security,
  - information control,
  - adversarial narratives.
- **Credible genocide framing** (grounded in international legal scrutiny):
  - demands scrutiny,
  - insists on accountability,
  - treats silence as complicity.

Both frames cannot govern civic space simultaneously without conflict.

---

## üß© Category Confusion (Where Harm Emerges)  
Under interpretive drift, boundaries blur between:
- journalism and intelligence,
- OSINT and espionage,
- protest and extremism,
- political organisation and terrorism.

Without formal wartime declaration, these confusions are **not resolved by law**, but by discretion ‚Äî which is dangerous for democracy.

---

## üß† Psychological & Civic Impact  
When citizens cannot tell:
- what is lawful,
- what is merely disfavoured,
- and what is truly prohibited,

they experience:
- heightened vigilance,
- moral injury,
- withdrawal from participation,
- erosion of trust in institutions.

Democracy weakens not through repression alone, but through **uncertainty**.

## üßØ Governance Principles That Protect Democracy

- **Restrictions must be explicit, not implied.**
- **Peacetime rights cannot be eroded by narrative pressure.**
- **If wartime powers are needed, they must be declared and accountable.**

Anything else is governance by fog.
