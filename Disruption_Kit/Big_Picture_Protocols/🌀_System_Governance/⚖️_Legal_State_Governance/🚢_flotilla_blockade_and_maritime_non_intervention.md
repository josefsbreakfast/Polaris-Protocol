## ‚öì Blockade law (plain language)
A naval blockade is only lawful if:

- it is proportionate,
- it allows humanitarian access,
- and it does not cause starvation of civilians.

Once starvation risk is foreseeable, **third states acquire duties**:
- not to assist the blockade‚Äôs unlawful effects,
- and to facilitate relief where capacity exists.

Blockade legality is **conditional**, not permanent.

By early 2024:

- famine risk was publicly identified,

- docking was refused by the blockading power.

At the same time:
- allied states maintained maritime and aerial ISR presence,
- described explicitly as **non-SAR and non-humanitarian**.

This is the moment that matters.

## üõ∞Ô∏è ISR-only presence is not neutral
Maintaining ISR-only maritime assets communicates:

- awareness of conditions,
- capacity to observe and verify,
- and deliberate abstention from facilitation.

Once harm is foreseeable, observation without action becomes **acquiescence**.

This is not ignorance.
This is **choice under visibility**.

## üö´ Docking refusal + observation = acceptance
The combination of:

- blocked humanitarian docking,
- known starvation risk,
- and continued third-state observation,

creates a closed loop:

1. Harm is visible.
2. Access is denied.
3. Capacity to assist exists.
4. Assistance is withheld.

At that point, non-intervention is no longer passive.

Maritime non-intervention strengthens claims of:

- failure to prevent prohibited outcomes,
- aiding by omission,
- and tolerance of unlawful blockade effects.

Unlike arms transfers or intelligence sharing, this case does not rely on inference.

The harm, capacity, and refusal are **co-located in time and space**.
