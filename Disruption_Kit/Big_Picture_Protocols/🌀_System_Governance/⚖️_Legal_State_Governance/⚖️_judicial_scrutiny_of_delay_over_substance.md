## üßø Analysis  

### 1. Why courts focus on delay  

Judges are tasked with evaluating:
- reasonableness  
- proportionality  
- procedural fairness  

Delay directly implicates all three.

Unlike motive or intent, delay is:
- timestamped  
- documentable  
- uncontested as a fact  
- resistant to reinterpretation  

This makes it a **clean judicial signal**.

---

### 2. Delay as a proxy for fairness  

Courts frequently use delay to infer:
- seriousness of response  
- respect for obligations  
- regard for the affected party  
- quality of governance  

A slow or inconsistent response suggests that:
> **the matter was not treated with appropriate weight at the time.**

This inference can be more damaging than the underlying mistake.

---

### 3. Delay shifts the evidentiary burden  

Once delay is established, the burden moves:

- the claimant no longer needs to prove urgency  
- the respondent must justify why action was not taken sooner  

This inversion significantly alters the legal balance.

---

### 4. Harm caused by process  

Courts recognise that delay can cause:
- loss of evidence  
- prolonged uncertainty  
- procedural disadvantage  
- escalation of harm  

As a result, judicial focus often centres on:
> **what the delay did**, not just what originally happened.

---

### 5. Why institutions misjudge this risk  

Institutions often assume:
> ‚ÄúIf the underlying issue is defensible, delay will not matter.‚Äù

Judicial reasoning frequently proves the opposite.

A defensible issue paired with unjustified delay  
is often viewed more negatively than a flawed issue addressed promptly.
