This node is not laughing at Anguilla.

The place is complicated.

That is the point.

Anguilla is not a punchline.

Anguilla is stop one on the Flag It Yourself postcard route.

Contestants may arrive believing Anguilla is:

## First Postcard: Where Actually Is It?

Anguilla is one of the most northerly of the Leeward Islands in the Lesser Antilles.

It lies east of Puerto Rico and the Virgin Islands, and directly north of Saint Martin.

The territory includes:

- the main island of Anguilla;
- several smaller islands and cays with no permanent population;
- the capital, The Valley;
- a total land area of around 35 square miles / 91 km²;
- and a population of around 15,753 in 2021.

The main island is approximately 16 miles / 26 km long and about 3 miles / 5 km wide at its widest point.

So yes, contestants may feel tempted to say “small.”

They may not say “simple.”

Small is not simple.

Small is often where administrative arrogance goes to embarrass itself in public.

## Motto And Dolphins

Anguilla’s motto is:

> Unity, Strength and Endurance

Production suggests contestants read this before assuming Anguilla’s constitutional history is a passive little tale of British tidiness.

Anguilla’s coat of arms includes three dolphins.

This matters.

Not because contestants may ask the dolphins for constitutional guidance.

They may not.

The dolphins are part of Anguilla’s own symbolic language. Contestants should notice that the island has its own self-representation, not just a British label placed over it from far away.

The cocktail flag is not the only symbol in the room.

It may not even be in the top three.

## Name Note: Eel Island

The name Anguilla is usually linked to the Italian anguilla, meaning “eel,” from Latin roots associated with “snake,” referring to the island’s long, narrow shape.

Anguillan tradition holds that Christopher Columbus named the island.

Contestants should be careful here.

The naming story is not permission to begin an entire speech about European discovery while ignoring the people already present, the people later enslaved, or the people now living there.

The island had a life before the European naming story.

Write that down.

## Before Empire: Indigenous Presence

Anguilla was first settled by Indigenous American peoples who migrated from South America.

Archaeological evidence points to a long pre-European history, including early artefacts and later settlement remains.

There are known petroglyph sites, including Big Spring and Fountain Cavern. Big Spring includes many petroglyphs, with rock ledges marked by face-like indentations.

This matters because contestants may arrive with a map that begins when Europeans start naming things.

The island did not begin when Europeans learned to spell eel.

## Colonial History And Enslavement

European contact and control were contested and uneven.

There are accounts of Dutch, French, Spanish, and English involvement or attack at different points. Traditional accounts say English settlers from Saint Kitts colonised Anguilla from around 1650.

European settlers grew tobacco and cotton, and sugar later became more important.

Enslaved Africans were brought into the region and to Anguilla during the colonial period. Over time, enslaved Africans and their descendants came to outnumber white settlers. Their forced labour, survival, culture, resistance, family formation, and descendants are part of Anguilla’s history.

Slavery was abolished across the British Empire in 1834, but abolition did not erase the consequences of slavery, plantation history, race, land, labour, poverty, migration, or political inequality.

Contestants must not talk about Anguilla as if its history is only constitutional paperwork.

The island’s story includes Indigenous presence, European colonial contest, plantation economies, enslaved African labour, emancipation, economic hardship, migration, and local political struggle.

This is not:

> “Nice beach, British-ish.”

This is history.

Behave.

## How It Became An Overseas Territory

Anguilla’s modern constitutional story cannot be understood by saying “it was British” and stopping there.

For much of the colonial period, Anguilla was administratively tied to other islands. In 1825, it was placed under the administrative control of nearby Saint Kitts. In 1882, Anguilla was federated with Saint Kitts and Nevis, against the wishes of many Anguillans.

That resentment mattered.

Economic stagnation, droughts, and the Great Depression pushed many Anguillans to emigrate for better prospects elsewhere. But the key constitutional grievance remained: many Anguillans did not want to be dominated from Saint Kitts.

Full adult suffrage came in 1952.

After the short-lived West Indies Federation, Anguilla became part of the associated state of Saint Kitts-Nevis-Anguilla in 1967.

Many Anguillans rejected that arrangement.

On 30 May 1967, Anguillans ejected the St Kitts police force from the island. After a referendum, they declared independence from St Kitts. This became known as the Anguillan Revolution.

The goal was not independence from Britain in the usual anti-colonial direction.

The goal was separation from Saint Kitts and Nevis, and a return to a direct British relationship rather than remaining in the associated state.

This is the bit contestants must understand before saying anything smug about “decolonisation” as if every territory’s politics points in one identical direction.

Sometimes local preference does not fit the neat slogan.

That is why you have to ask the people there.

A second referendum confirmed the desire for separation from St Kitts. The short-lived Republic of Anguilla was declared, with Ronald Webster as president. Britain restored authority in 1969 after sending troops, and Anguilla was later formally separated from Saint Kitts and Nevis in 1980, becoming a separate British Crown colony, now a British Overseas Territory.

So no: Anguilla is not a passive dot.

Anguilla has already had a revolution about being treated as an administrative attachment.

Contestants should take the hint.

## What The Craic Is Now

Anguilla is a British Overseas Territory.

The monarch is head of state and is represented by the Governor. Anguilla also has its own elected local government, including a Premier, Executive Council, and House of Assembly.

In ordinary terms:

- Anguilla is not independent;
- Anguilla is not part of the United Kingdom;
- Anguilla has local self-government;
- the UK retains responsibilities in areas such as defence, external affairs, and aspects of good governance;
- the constitutional relationship is real, but not the same thing as “Britain owns a beach.”

Contestants must not arrive and behave as if London is the only political actor.

Local government exists.

Local identity exists.

Local consent exists.

The cocktail flag will need to cope.

## People Are Not Scenery

Anguilla is a Caribbean island society, not a decorative beach extension of someone else’s nostalgia.

Contestants should avoid the following errors:

- assuming “British” means culturally identical to Britain;
- treating Anguillians as background characters in a British constitutional sketch;
- confusing tourism branding with local life;
- treating the island’s relationship with Britain as passive;
- using “small island” as code for “politically simple”;
- assuming warm weather makes constitutional questions less serious;
- forgetting that Anguilla has already objected very firmly to being governed through arrangements it did not want.

Anguillians are not supporting actors in a British map drama.

The contestant’s job is to learn enough not to be embarrassing.

This may take some time.

## Wildlife / Habitat Postcard

Anguilla is not just beaches.

Although, yes, there are beaches, and production accepts that some contestants may need a few minutes to recover from this discovery.

A respectful wildlife / habitat postcard should notice:

- sea turtles;
- nesting beaches;
- seabirds;
- salt ponds;
- coral reefs;
- marine habitats;
- coastal scrub and dry vegetation;
- limestone landscapes;
- fragile island ecosystems;
- conservation work by local organisations.

The Anguilla National Trust is a key local conservation body. Its work includes sea turtle monitoring, bird counts, habitat and species monitoring, public education, and protection of natural and cultural heritage.

Contestants are reminded:

> A beach is not empty because you personally do not understand ecology.

Sand may contain nests.

Water may contain reefs.

Salt ponds may contain birds.

Scrub may contain more life than your entire argument.

Please observe accordingly.

## Cocktail Flag Warning

The cocktail flag may not be placed:

- in turtle nesting areas;
- in coral;
- in protected habitat;
- in salt pond areas;
- in conservation sites without permission;
- in someone’s garden;
- in someone’s lunch;
- in sand where local rules say do not disturb;
- on or near petroglyphs;
- in Wallblake House;
- anywhere that makes Anguillians wonder whether Britain has sent its least prepared adult.

The flag is not a treaty instrument.

It is cake furniture.

Anguilla is not obliged to be impressed.
