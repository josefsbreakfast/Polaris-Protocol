# 📍 Postcard List  
**SATIRE. SATIRE. SATIRE.**  
*Because “British Overseas Territory” is not one place, one people, one history, one habitat, or one convenient little dot for men with map feelings.*

---

## Welcome To The Postcard List

This folder sits inside **🛶 Flag It Yourself**.

It exists because contestants may arrive believing that “British Overseas Territories” is one tidy category.

It is not.

It is an administrative umbrella covering places with very different histories, people, cultures, legal arrangements, ecosystems, disputes, economies, vulnerabilities, and relationships with Britain.

Some are Caribbean societies.

Some are South Atlantic islands.

Some are military base areas.

Some have permanent civilian populations.

Some do not.

Some carry displacement histories.

Some carry major biodiversity responsibilities.

Some have active sovereignty disputes.

Some are remote enough that “just pop over there and stick a flag in it” should result in immediate referral to the remedial module:

> **Maps Are Smaller Than Oceans, Colin.**

---

## Purpose Of This Folder

Each node in this folder is a postcard stop.

A postcard stop should make the contestant encounter the territory as a place, not a symbol.

Each postcard asks:

- Who lives here?
- How did this become an Overseas Territory?
- What is the current constitutional / political arrangement?
- What does local identity look like?
- What should outsiders not flatten?
- What wildlife, plants, habitats, or conservation issues matter?
- What would a respectful visitor need to know?
- What does this stop teach about the difference between map-colour and reality?

The joke is the cocktail flag.

The serious bit is that the places are real.

---

## Core Rule

Do not flatten the territories.

The phrase **British Overseas Territories** may be administratively useful, but it does not mean these places are culturally, historically, legally, ecologically, or politically interchangeable.

Contestants may not say:

- “they’re all basically the same”;
- “it’s ours, isn’t it?”;
- “I don’t see why this is complicated”;
- “local consent is a distraction”;
- “the map is clear”;
- “the Navy will sort it”;
- “the people there should understand British interests”;
- “but historically—”

At that point, production may gently confiscate the cocktail flags.

---

## The Postcard Method

Each territory node should contain five basic postcard layers.

### 1. The Human Postcard

Who lives here?

What languages, identities, cultures, histories, and local institutions matter?

What should an outsider not assume?

The contestant must not treat residents as background scenery, grateful extras, local colour, or supporting characters in a British sovereignty drama.

### 2. The History Postcard

How did Britain become involved?

Was this a settlement, conquest, treaty, base arrangement, colonial administration, strategic claim, constitutional continuation, or something messier?

The point is not to write a complete history book.

The point is to stop people saying “just keep it” without knowing what “it” even is.

### 3. The Current Crack

What is the current status?

Who governs locally?

What does the UK retain responsibility for?

Are there sovereignty disputes, decolonisation questions, military arrangements, environmental obligations, constitutional sensitivities, or local political issues?

This is the section where contestants discover that governance is not activated by cake furniture.

### 4. The Habitat Postcard

What lives here?

What ecosystems matter?

What animals, plants, reefs, cliffs, wetlands, forests, volcanic landscapes, turtle beaches, seabird colonies, penguin zones, monkey zones, or protected habitats need noticing?

Contestants must understand:

> The map is not the habitat.

### 5. The Cocktail Flag Warning

Where must the contestant absolutely not put the flag?

Examples:

- not in coral;
- not in a nesting site;
- not in protected habitat;
- not in someone’s garden;
- not at customs;
- not in a memorial;
- not near annoyed residents;
- not in a macaque;
- not in a penguin;
- not anywhere that requires the sentence “technically I thought this would be fine.”

## Research Standard

Each postcard node should aim for:

- one reliable source on the territory’s current constitutional status;
- one reliable source on local history;
- one reliable source on local people / culture / identity;
- one reliable source on wildlife, biodiversity, or conservation;
- one local or territory-specific source where possible.

Do not rely only on London-facing summaries.

If the point of the folder is to stop flattening places into imperial map-dots, then the research should not flatten them either.

---

## Style Note

The tone can be satirical.

The facts should not be sloppy.

Jokes may carry the reader in.

Care should keep them there.

A good postcard node should be funny enough to be readable and careful enough not to become the exact thing it is mocking.

---

## Working Principle

The postcard method turns a territory from a dot into a place.

A place has:

- people;
- history;
- law;
- politics;
- weather;
- animals;
- plants;
- habitats;
- memory;
- disagreement;
- consent;
- and its own current reality.

The contestant may still hold a view after learning these things.

But it should be a smaller, quieter, better view.
