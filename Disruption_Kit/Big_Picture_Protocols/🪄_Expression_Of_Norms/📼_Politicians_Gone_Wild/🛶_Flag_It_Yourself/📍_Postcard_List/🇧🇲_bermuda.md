## First Postcard: 181 Islands In The North Atlantic

Bermuda is a British Overseas Territory in the North Atlantic.

It is not in the Caribbean, despite many people mentally filing it there because the weather is nicer than Grimsby.

Bermuda is an archipelago of around 181 islands and islets. The larger islands are connected in ways that can make Bermuda feel like one main landmass, but “one island” is still the sort of thing a contestant says before being handed a remedial map.

The capital is Hamilton.

Contestants should note:

> If your entire Bermuda knowledge is “triangle,” you have not yet begun.

The triangle is not governance.

The triangle is not local history.

The triangle is not a conservation plan.

The triangle will not validate your cocktail flag.

## Before English Settlement: Reefs, Birds, Hogs, And The Isle Of Devils

Bermuda was known to Europeans before English settlement.

It is associated with the Spanish explorer Juan de Bermúdez, after whom the islands were named. Spanish and Portuguese ships used the islands as a place to replenish water and fresh meat, but neither Spain nor Portugal settled the archipelago.

The islands also developed a reputation as the Isle of Devils.

This was probably not because Satan had submitted a planning application.

More likely, the reputation came from:

- storm-racked conditions;
- dangerous reefs;
- frightening night sounds;
- the calls of birds such as the Bermuda petrel, or cahow;
- and introduced wild hogs making the kind of noises that encourage sailors to become religious very quickly.

This matters because Bermuda’s European story does not begin as tidy imperial possession.

It begins with sea, hazard, noise, reef, fear, and practical use by ships.

Contestants may wish to update their spreadsheet.

## How It Became An Overseas Territory

Bermuda’s English settlement is tied to the Sea Venture, the flagship of a fleet sent in 1609 to support Jamestown, Virginia.

A storm scattered the fleet. The Sea Venture was driven onto Bermuda’s reef to prevent it sinking, and its passengers and crew survived.

This is not a minor detail.

Bermuda’s English settlement story is not:

> “Britain looked at a nice island and filed the paperwork.”

It is:

> “A shipwreck happened, everyone survived, Jamestown sounded dreadful, some people wanted to stay, and then empire did what empire does.”

The survivors built two ships, the Deliverance and the Patience, to continue to Virginia.

Production notes that Deliverance and Patience are also useful qualities for contestants attempting the Overseas Territory itinerary, though neither can be issued in the standard kit.

English settlement began formally in 1612. St George’s became an early colonial capital and one of the oldest continuously inhabited English towns in the Americas. Bermuda’s House of Assembly dates from 1620, giving the territory a very long parliamentary history.

Contestants must therefore not assume that “Overseas Territory” means a politically blank place waiting for Britain to speak.

Bermuda has had institutions for a very long time.

It has not spent four centuries waiting for a think-tank man to arrive with a cocktail flag and explain governance.

## Slavery, Native American Enslavement, And Black Bermudian History

Bermuda’s story includes slavery and racial hierarchy.

That cannot be treated as a footnote to the shipwreck.

The first enslaved people recorded in Bermuda included a Black person and a Native American person brought from the West Indies in the early seventeenth century. Over time, more enslaved Black people were trafficked to the island from the Americas and the Caribbean.

Bermuda’s slavery history also included enslaved Native Americans, including people from Indigenous nations in New England who were exiled and sold into slavery after colonial wars.

As the Black population grew, white settlers feared insurrection. Bermuda saw repeated unrest, conspiracies, and rebellions among enslaved people. Laws were passed to control Black economic and social life.

This is not decorative history.

This is the labour, coercion, resistance, racial order, ancestry, and survival history underneath the postcard.

Contestants must not do the classic imperial shuffle:

> Shipwreck. Parliament. Navy. Pink sand. Shorts. Done.

No.

The Bermuda postcard must include the people whose forced labour, resistance, culture, and survival formed the island.

If contestants want “king and country,” they also get “history and consequence.”

No refunds.

## A Small Archipelago Can Still Have Dramatic Politics

Bermuda’s history does not become simple after settlement.

The English Civil War reached Bermuda, where many colonists were strongly royalist. Bermuda’s position during the American Revolution was also complicated by trade, hunger, family connections, Atlantic routes, and survival. Bermudians were involved in the 1775 removal of gunpowder from the Bermudian magazine, which was sent to support the American rebellion.

Later, Bermuda became much more important to British naval power. The Royal Naval Dockyard on Ireland Island became a major base guarding western Atlantic shipping lanes. Bermuda was connected to the War of 1812, American Civil War blockade running, Boer War prisoners, twentieth-century military installations, wartime censorship, intelligence work, tourism, aviation, finance, political unrest, and modern self-government.

So yes.

Some shit has gone down in Bermuda.

But this section is not here to turn Bermuda into a list of imperial trivia.

It is here to stop contestants using “small island” as code for “historically simple.”

Small places can have dense histories.

Please learn this before touching the cocktail flags.

## What The Crack Is Now

Bermuda is a British Overseas Territory.

It has a high degree of internal self-government. The monarch is head of state and is represented by the Governor. Bermuda has its own elected government and Parliament, including a Premier.

The UK retains responsibilities including areas such as external affairs and defence, but Bermuda is not just a small administrative outpost. It has its own government, political parties, economy, institutions, and public debates about constitutional development and self-government.

Contestants must not arrive and say:

> “Well, London decides.”

That is a sign they have confused constitutional ultimate responsibility with everyday political reality.

The cocktail flag will be placed back in the packet until they calm down.

If the King can turn up and look at the place as a real place, contestants can also try.

Bermuda is not a stage set for British Atlantic nostalgia.

Contestants should avoid the following errors:

- assuming royal visit equals no politics;
- forgetting that people living on islands generally notice when outsiders use them as metaphors.

Bermudians are not supporting actors in a British map drama.

The contestant’s job is to learn enough not to be embarrassing.

This may take some time.

## Weather Is Also Part Of The Map

Contestants are reminded that Bermuda’s geography includes weather.

Bermuda is prone to severe weather from recurving tropical cyclones. Its coral reef and its position north of the main tropical cyclone development region can offer some protection, limiting the direction and severity of some approaching storms.

This does not mean Bermuda is “safe from weather.”

It means geography, reefs, storm tracks, ocean temperature, exposure, infrastructure, and preparedness all matter.

Contestants may not say:

> “It’s just an island.”

It is 181 islands and islets in the North Atlantic with hurricanes, reefs, local infrastructure, insurance risk, coastal exposure, and people who have to live there after the dramatic weather has finished being a satellite image.

The reef is not a decorative border.

The reef is habitat, protection, vulnerability, and living infrastructure.

Please stop treating it like the blue bit around the map.

## Wildlife / Habitat Postcard

Bermuda is ecologically distinctive.

Contestants should notice:

- the Bermuda petrel, or cahow;
- the Bermuda skink;
- Bermuda cedar and palmetto;
- endemic snails and other invertebrates;
- coral reefs;
- turtles;
- seabirds;
- coastal habitats;
- caves;
- Nonsuch Island conservation work;
- and the fact that small islands can hold globally significant biodiversity.

The cahow and Bermuda skink are especially important reminders that “small island” does not mean “minor ecology.”

Some species found in Bermuda are endemic. If they disappear there, they disappear from the world.

This should make contestants quieter.

Production welcomes this development.
