# 🛶 Flag It Yourself  
**SATIRE. SATIRE. SATIRE.**  
*For people who believe “just keep the island” is a naval strategy, a legal position, and a substitute for understanding boats.*

---

## Welcome Aboard

**Flag It Yourself** is the first flagship programme inside **📼 Politicians Gone Wild**.

It is for politicians, pundits, think-tank lads, retired men with maps, and any public figure who believes that distant islands, bases, rocks, former imperial nodes, or suspiciously pink historical map-dots can be retained forever by saying “British interests” in a serious voice.

The format is simple.

A contestant declares that Britain must keep something.

We issue them the official kit.

Then we invite them to go and stick the flag in it themselves.

This is satire.

Do not put actual politicians in dinghies.  
Do not push anyone into the sea.  
Do not mistake a joke for a maritime plan, unless you are writing certain op-eds, in which case apparently crack on.

---

## Official Contestant Kit

Each contestant is issued with:

- one inflatable dinghy;
- one life vest;
- one laminated map;
- one compass;
- one GPS tracker;
- one packet of emergency calorie biscuits that taste like compressed Cold War shelving;
- one flask of tea;
- twelve tiny Union Jack cocktail flags, of the kind normally seen in Jubilee cupcakes, royal wedding traybakes, village fêtes, school halls, and suspiciously dry sponge;
- one clipboard;
- one pencil attached to string;
- one waterproof pouch labelled **“Please Do Not Tweet During The Maritime Incident.”**

The flags are not optional.

Contestants who have spent several years insisting that distant territories must remain British for reasons of map, destiny, vibes, inherited blazer energy, or “geostrategy” will be expected to plant the flags personally.

Photographic proof is required.

Each successful flag placement must include:

- visible flag;
- visible contestant;
- visible exhaustion;
- GPS confirmation;
- timestamp;
- short explanation of what legal, logistical, diplomatic, military, and human problem has supposedly been solved by sticking a cocktail flag into something damp.

No luxury transport is permitted.

No business class.  
No private jet.  
No naval escort.  
No “my office will handle the details.”  
No social media posting while underway.  
No Truth Social.  
No Mastodon.  
No dramatic Telegraph column dictated from the dinghy.  
No livestream called **“Sovereignty At Sea.”**

The contestant may briefly activate the GPS tracker for check-in purposes.

They may not use the device to complain that the sea is large.

Telegram access may be granted only for emergency production updates, biscuit accountability, and confirmation that the contestant has not attempted to annex a buoy.

The flask contains tea because this is Britain.

Is tea a sensible primary hydration plan for a maritime expedition?  
No.

Will the contestant have previously claimed to understand national resilience?  
Almost certainly.

This is therefore considered educational.

Contestants are reminded that rowing requires hands. Hands cannot simultaneously operate a dinghy, wave a cocktail flag, write a column, and blame lawyers for the tide.

Production staff reserve the right to shout:

> Less tweeting, more rowing.

## The Premise

Some people love a map more than they love a supply chain.

They look at an island and see destiny.

They look at a base and see prestige.

They look at a territorial dispute and see a chance to say “geostrategic” while avoiding all questions about fuel, law, consent, maintenance, jurisdiction, local people, diplomacy, regional politics, repair, crew rotation, or whether anyone has packed enough pants.

**Flag It Yourself** exists to help.

It asks:

> If this territory is so essential to your vision of Britain, can you personally explain how it works once the sea stops being a metaphor?

---

## Core Rule

A flag is not a logistics plan.

A map is not a navy.

A base is not useful because it gives someone in a blazer feelings.

A ship is not fuelled by nostalgia.

A sailor cannot eat sovereignty.

## Episode One — Admiral Spreadsheet Goes To Sea

The contestant begins in Dover.

They are given their dinghy, biscuits, tiny flags, flask of tea, GPS tracker, and a strong belief that Britain was better when more things were coloured pink.

They must then personally travel to every island, base, maritime route, strategic rock, and imperial leftover they believe Britain must retain forever.

Luxury travel is forbidden.

No business class.  
No aircraft carrier.  
No staffer carrying the snacks.  
No friendly superpower quietly doing the expensive bit.  
No “my office will send the details.”  
No saying “project power” until you have successfully inflated the dinghy.

The contestant must also complete the following tasks:

1. identify the nearest refuelling point;
2. explain who maintains the port;
3. explain who pays for it;
4. explain who lives there;
5. explain who was removed from there;
6. explain who wants sovereignty;
7. explain who actually uses the base;
8. explain what the treaty says;
9. explain what happens if the weather turns;
10. explain why the Navy should listen to someone whose maritime strategy is mostly colouring-in.

Bonus points if the contestant can define “logistics” without blaming a lawyer.

## Episode Two — Big Boat Can Get More Petrol Now

In this episode, contestants are introduced to a difficult modern concept:

> Boats changed.

This may be upsetting for some viewers.

There was a time when maritime power depended more heavily on direct chains of imperial ports, coaling stations, local dominance, and large amounts of Britain behaving as if the ocean had been invented for its convenience.

That time has passed.

Modern ships are not wooden tubs of scurvy and cannon panic.  
They are bigger, more complex, more capable, more fuel-hungry in some ways, more sustainable in others, and part of a logistics system involving replenishment, allies, bases, agreements, ports, repair, crews, fuel, food, water, sanitation, communications, satellites, intelligence, industrial capacity, and people with clipboards who know why the clipboard matters.

This means:

> Big boat can get more petrol now.

It also means:

> Big boat still needs a plan.

The contestant must therefore explain why sovereign possession of a dot is better than lawful, reliable, treaty-secured, alliance-backed, operationally usable access.

They may not answer:

- “because empire”;
- “because China”;
- “because Starmer”;
- “because the map”;
- “because the Navy will sort it”;
- “because I have strong feelings about the Indian Ocean.”

## Episode Three — The Map Is Not The Territory, Colin

Some contestants struggle with the difference between a map and reality.

This episode offers practical support.

The contestant is shown:

- a map;
- the sea;
- a boat;
- a human being;
- a maintenance bill;
- a treaty;
- a port;
- a storm;
- a fuel requirement;
- a local population;
- a displaced population;
- an international court;
- and a naval engineer who has had enough.

They must then sort these objects into two piles:

### Pile One: Things That Exist Physically

Examples:

- water;
- ships;
- ports;
- fuel;
- weather;
- people;
- hunger;
- distance;
- maintenance;
- the consequences of decisions.

### Pile Two: Things That May Not Solve The Problem Alone

Examples:

- vibes;
- flags;
- old maps;
- saying “sovereignty” loudly;
- calling something “woke”;
- being cross that lawyers exist;
- assuming Britain is still in 1897;
- describing Mauritius as if it is a suspicious intern.

If the contestant successfully completes this task, they may proceed to the next round.

If not, they are given a biscuit and returned gently to the studio.

## Episode Four — Chagos: The Cursed Map-Dot Special

In this special episode, contestants discuss the Chagos Archipelago without pretending it is exactly the same as Gibraltar, the Falklands, Israel-Palestine, the British Raj, Brexit, the EU, the Norman Conquest, or whatever else has fallen out of the filing cabinet.

They must say, clearly and separately:

- what Britain wants;
- what the United States wants;
- what Mauritius wants;
- what Chagossians want;
- what India may care about;
- what China may seek to exploit;
- what the base needs operationally;
- what the law says;
- what the treaty says;
- what was done to the people removed from the islands;
- and why “keep it because it looks powerful” may not be the same as strategy.

They may not use Chagossians as a moral garnish.

They may not use Mauritius as a cardboard cut-out labelled “China?”

They may not say “the Falklands” unless they can explain why a territory with a present local population that has repeatedly expressed a preference is not automatically the same as a territory whose people were removed.

They may not mention “soft power is for the birds” unless they first identify the bird, its habitat, and whether it has been consulted.

---

## Episode Five — Sirens, Scurvy, And Select Committees

For historical balance, contestants are reminded that old maritime power was not just nice uniforms and heroic paintings.

It also involved:

- disease;
- hunger;
- press-gangs;
- class violence;
- shipboard surgery;
- amputations;
- storms;
- boredom;
- fear;
- rats;
- terrible toilets;
- and heritage museums showing children wax sailors screaming while someone removes a leg.

Contestants who romanticise the old maritime age must sit through the full diorama.

They must then write 500 words on the following question:

> Was empire actually glamorous, or did someone just edit out the screaming?

Refreshments will be available in the gift shop.

---

## Episode Six — Foursquare For Imperial Feelings

In this episode, contestants must check in at every site they claim is essential to British destiny.

The check-in must include:

- GPS proof;
- timestamp;
- photograph of the tiny cocktail flag;
- declaration of what has allegedly been secured;
- list of all support vessels, treaties, maintenance arrangements, medical supplies, port permissions, and snacks required to make the claim remotely meaningful.

Contestants may not claim sovereignty over anything merely because they got damp near it.

Contestants may not check in at a buoy and call it “forward posture.”

Contestants may not become emotionally attached to a sandbank and demand a select committee.

## What The Show Tests

**Flag It Yourself** tests whether a political claim survives contact with:

- water;
- distance;
- fuel;
- law;
- repair;
- weather;
- local consent;
- displacement;
- treaties;
- food;
- sanitation;
- allied access;
- human bodies;
- communications discipline;
- and the fact that the Navy is not powered by newspaper columns.

It does not ask whether Britain should have maritime interests.

Of course Britain has maritime interests.

It asks whether the speaker understands those interests well enough to stop confusing them with old map feelings.

## Production Notes

This node is satire.

It is not a proposal to endanger anyone.  
It is not a plan to put politicians in boats.  
It is not an incitement to harm, abandon, drown, harass, stalk, threaten, dox, or physically interfere with public figures.

The joke is the gap between rhetoric and reality.

The civic point is simple:

> If your argument only works while someone else absorbs the risk, your argument is not finished.

---

## Working Principle

Maritime strategy is not map nostalgia.

A serious maritime argument should be able to explain:

- access;
- sustainment;
- refuelling;
- repair;
- personnel;
- port rights;
- allied agreements;
- local politics;
- legal authority;
- regional diplomacy;
- vulnerability;
- cost;
- and what happens when conditions change.

An unserious maritime argument points at a map and gets emotional.

**Flag It Yourself** is for the second one.
