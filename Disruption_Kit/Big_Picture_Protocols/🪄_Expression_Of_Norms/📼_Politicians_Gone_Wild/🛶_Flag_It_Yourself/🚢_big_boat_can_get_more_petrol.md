# 🚢 Big Boat Can Get More Petrol  
SATIRE. SATIRE. SATIRE.  
Introductory maritime education for contestants whose naval strategy begins and ends with pointing at a map.

---

## Welcome To The Boat Lesson

Contestants on Flag It Yourself may arrive with a romantic understanding of British sea power.

This understanding may include:

- old maps;
- flags;
- Nelson;
- “Global Britain”;
- large paintings of ships doing emotionally persuasive things;
- and a vague belief that if Britain once had a port somewhere, the Royal Navy probably still needs it for reasons of destiny.

This node offers remedial support.

The lesson is simple:

> Big boat can get more petrol now.

This does not mean big boat needs no plan.

It means the plan has changed.

---

## The Historical Boat Problem

Historically, ships were limited by very physical things.

Not vibes.

Things.

Ships needed:

- food;
- water;
- repair;
- crew;
- shelter;
- medical care;
- supplies;
- fuel or wind;
- safe harbours;
- local knowledge;
- and somewhere to stop before everyone became ill, hungry, lost, mutinous, or extremely dead.

Older maritime power depended heavily on controlled ports, coaling stations, victualling yards, dockyards, fortified harbours, imperial routes, and a great deal of Britain behaving as if the ocean had been arranged for its convenience.

This is not because old sailors were silly.

It is because ships were physical objects operated by human bodies in hostile conditions.

Wooden ships did not run on speeches.

Neither do steel ones.

---

## The Empire Map Was Also A Logistics Map

The old imperial map was not just decorative.

It was also a system of:

- stops;
- stores;
- repairs;
- resupply;
- labour;
- law;
- coercion;
- customs control;
- military access;
- trade protection;
- communication routes;
- and local domination dressed up as global order.

That is why old maps can be emotionally misleading.

They make empire look like colour.

But much of it was actually infrastructure.

And much of that infrastructure depended on other people being made to carry the cost.

So when someone says, “Why don’t we just keep it?” the first answer is:

> Keep what, exactly? The flag? The port? The runway? The repair yard? The legal right? The local consent? The fuel? The people? The ecological responsibility? The alliance arrangement? The memory? The myth?

A serious maritime argument knows which one it means.

An unserious maritime argument says “sovereignty” and hopes nobody asks about the plumbing.

---

## The Modern Boat Situation

Modern ships are not wooden tubs of scurvy and cannon panic.

They are bigger, more complex, more capable, more electronically dependent, more maintenance-intensive, and part of a wider system.

Modern maritime power may involve:

- replenishment at sea;
- allied ports;
- treaty access;
- aircraft;
- satellites;
- undersea surveillance;
- communications networks;
- submarines;
- drones;
- naval auxiliaries;
- repair contracts;
- fuel logistics;
- interoperability;
- intelligence sharing;
- legal permissions;
- and people who know what the clipboard is for.

This means a modern navy does not simply ask:

> Do we own the dot?

It asks:

> Can we use the place lawfully, reliably, safely, defensibly, affordably, and without turning the surrounding region into a permanent diplomatic migraine?

Those are different questions.

Please stop confusing them.

## Big Boat Can Get More Petrol

The phrase “big boat can get more petrol” is not a complete naval doctrine.

It is a corrective.

It reminds contestants that modern maritime endurance, range, and reach are not the same as eighteenth- or nineteenth-century maritime dependency.

A modern naval force can be sustained through systems that do not always require direct sovereign possession of every stop along the way.

This may include:

- refuelling;
- resupply;
- allied basing;
- port visits;
- logistics ships;
- air support;
- repair access;
- pre-positioned stores;
- treaty arrangements;
- and boring agreements that never appear in heroic paintings because everyone is wearing a lanyard.

The boring agreements matter.

The lanyard people may be saving your boat.

Show respect.

---

## But Big Boat Still Needs A Plan

This is where contestants often become confused.

The fact that big boat can get more petrol does not mean big boat can go anywhere, do anything, ignore law, upset every local community, annoy all wildlife, and still arrive in triumph because someone said “Britannia.”

Big boat still needs:

- crew;
- fuel;
- food;
- maintenance;
- spare parts;
- communications;
- medical cover;
- ports;
- repair options;
- legal authority;
- weather planning;
- regional diplomacy;
- political consent;
- and people who understand that morale cannot be stored in a cocktail flag.

A ship is a machine.

A navy is a system.

A maritime strategy is not a feeling about where the pink bits used to be.

---

## The Current Point Of A Navy

The modern point of a navy is not to recreate the empire map with better Wi-Fi.

A navy may exist to:

- defend the country;
- protect sea lanes;
- support allies;
- deter attacks;
- protect undersea infrastructure;
- support humanitarian response;
- maintain nuclear deterrence;
- conduct surveillance;
- secure maritime trade;
- evacuate civilians;
- enforce sanctions or embargoes where lawful;
- support international obligations;
- and help ensure that hostile actors cannot simply use the sea as a free operating space.

That is serious work.

It deserves better than nostalgia.

If someone says they support the Navy, they should support:

- enough sailors;
- enough maintenance;
- enough sleep;
- enough pay;
- enough housing;
- enough repair capacity;
- enough shipbuilding capacity;
- enough fuel planning;
- enough industrial resilience;
- enough honesty about commitments;
- and fewer speeches written by men who think ports are decorative.

---

## History Is Not Useless

History matters.

Old maritime history can teach:

- why logistics win wars;
- why disease can break fleets;
- why supply chains matter;
- why sea lanes shape power;
- why ports become strategic;
- why sailors are workers, not symbols;
- why imperial routes were never neutral;
- why maps can conceal violence;
- why local populations are not background scenery;
- and why pretending the world has not changed is a reliable way to lose money, lives, and credibility.

The problem is not remembering history.

The problem is using history as a costume.

---

## The Map-Romance Error

The map-romance error occurs when a contestant believes that retaining a territorial colour on a map is the same thing as possessing usable power.

Symptoms include:

- pointing at islands;
- saying “geostrategic” too often;
- assuming the Navy agrees with them;
- treating lawyers as a weather event;
- confusing local consent with weakness;
- invoking the Falklands during unrelated disputes;
- describing any decolonisation process as surrender;
- and assuming that if Britain once controlled a place, Britain must still need it for reasons too grand to explain.

Treatment:

- ask where the fuel comes from;
- ask who lives there;
- ask what the treaty says;
- ask who maintains the port;
- ask what the local government wants;
- ask what the Navy actually needs;
- ask what happens if the weather turns;
- ask whether the argument survives without old maps.

Side effects may include strategic literacy.

## Chagos As A Boat Lesson

Chagos and Diego Garcia are useful for this node because they expose the difference between:

> “Britain must own the dot.”

and:

> “Britain and its allies require reliable operational access under a legal and diplomatic arrangement that can actually last.”

Those are not the same sentence.

A serious Chagos argument should be able to discuss:

- the displaced Chagossians;
- Mauritius;
- the United Kingdom;
- the United States;
- Diego Garcia;
- regional states;
- India;
- China;
- legal rulings;
- leaseback arrangements;
- base access;
- legitimacy;
- cost;
- and long-term operational stability.

An unserious Chagos argument says:

> “But the map.”

Then it reaches for a cocktail flag.

## Things The Navy Is Not

The Navy is not:

- a nostalgia delivery service;
- a floating correction for bad columns;
- a magic fix for imperial grief;
- a substitute for diplomacy;
- a ceremonial map-colouring agency;
- a proof that Britain never has to update its assumptions;
- or a group of people whose job is to make retired men feel better about the Indian Ocean.

The Navy is made of people, machines, systems, maintenance, logistics, discipline, skill, risk, and consequence.

If your opinion makes their job harder while claiming to honour them, reconsider the opinion.

---

## Questions Before Publication

Before publishing a dramatic claim about maritime power, contestants must answer:

1. What specific naval function are you talking about?
2. Is this about access, sovereignty, prestige, deterrence, logistics, law, or memory?
3. What does the place actually provide?
4. Can that function be secured by treaty rather than possession?
5. Who lives there?
6. Who was displaced?
7. What does local consent look like?
8. What does the Navy actually need?
9. What does the route cost?
10. Who repairs the ship?
11. Where does the fuel come from?
12. What happens if the arrangement becomes diplomatically toxic?
13. Are you defending maritime capability, or are you defending an old emotional map?

If the answer to question thirteen is “both,” please sit down and separate them.

---

## Working Principle

The historical point of sea power was often access, supply, coercion, trade protection, and imperial reach.

The current point of naval power is still access and security, but the systems, ethics, laws, technologies, alliances, and political conditions have changed.

Therefore:

> History can inform maritime strategy.  
> History cannot be allowed to cosplay as maritime strategy.

Big boat can get more petrol now.

Big boat still needs a plan.

The map is not the plan.
