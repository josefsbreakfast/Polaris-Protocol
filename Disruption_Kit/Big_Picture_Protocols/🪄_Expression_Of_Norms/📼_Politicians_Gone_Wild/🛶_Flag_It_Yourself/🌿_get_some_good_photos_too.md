# 🌿 Get Some Good Photos Too  
SATIRE. SATIRE. SATIRE.  
Because if you are going all that way for king, country, cocktail flags, and unresolved map feelings, you may as well notice the wildlife.

---

## Welcome To The Nature Requirement

Contestants on Flag It Yourself are reminded that territories are not just dots on a map.

They are places.

Places contain:

- people;
- laws;
- weather;
- plants;
- animals;
- habitats;
- conservation rules;
- things that bite;
- things that migrate;
- things that nest;
- things that are protected;
- and things that were there before the opinion column.

This node sets out the Crown Naturalist Obligation.

If contestants are travelling around the Overseas Territories for reasons of king, country, sovereignty, destiny, blazer memory, or “geostrategy,” they must also bring back some decent nature notes.

Not because they are suddenly David Attenborough.

Because if you claim to love the territory, you should be able to notice what lives there.

---

## Core Rule

> The map is not the habitat.

A territory is not just:

- a flag;
- a base;
- a rock;
- a route;
- a tax arrangement;
- a strategic position;
- a historical grievance;
- a place to stand while singing the national anthem badly into the wind.

It is also an ecosystem.

Contestants who fail to notice the living world will be referred to the remedial module:

> “You Went All That Way And Photographed Only Your Own Forehead?”

---

## Crown Naturalist Obligation

At every stop, contestants must provide:

- one photograph of local wildlife, where lawful and respectful;
- one photograph of plant life, habitat, landscape, coastline, reef, wetland, forest, grassland, volcanic terrain, cliff, beach, or other locally relevant environment;
- one note on a conservation rule they followed;
- one note on what they did not touch, feed, chase, annoy, trample, salute, annex, or make pose with a cocktail flag;
- one sentence explaining how the place is more than a map-dot.

Contestants must not disturb wildlife to complete the task.

No photo is better than a bad photo obtained by annoying a penguin.

This should not need saying, but production has read the comments section.

## Note On Gibraltar Monkeys

The monkey references in this folder refer specifically to Gibraltar’s Barbary macaques.

They are real.

They are local.

They are not props.

They are not to be fed emergency biscuits.

They are not required to recognise the contestant’s cocktail flag.

If a macaque steals the flag, that is not a constitutional crisis.

That is a macaque.

Please update your notes.

## Note On Penguins

Penguins appear in this folder because several Overseas Territory stops involve penguin-relevant geography, especially the Falklands, South Georgia, and Antarctic territories.

Penguins are not:

- mascots for imperial confidence;
- tiny naval officers;
- soft-power ambassadors;
- available for patriotic content capture;
- required to stand near a cocktail flag;
- or legally competent to validate your position on sovereignty.

If a penguin walks away from you, that may be the strongest diplomatic signal you receive all week.

Respect it.

## Good Photo Rules

A good photo is:

- taken from a respectful distance;
- lawful under local rules;
- not obtained by feeding or disturbing wildlife;
- not staged by interfering with habitat;
- not centred entirely on the contestant’s face;
- not captioned “mine”;
- not used to imply local consent where none was granted.

A good photo shows that the contestant noticed the place.

A bad photo shows that the contestant visited a living ecosystem and still managed to behave like the main character in a map.

---

## Things You May Not Photograph Into Submission

Contestants may not harass the following for content:

- penguins;
- monkeys;
- turtles;
- seals;
- seabirds;
- iguanas;
- dolphins;
- crabs;
- nesting birds;
- protected plants;
- coral;
- local residents;
- customs officers;
- port workers;
- anyone eating lunch in peace.

The phrase “for king and country” is not a filming permit.

## The Habitat Question

At each stop, contestants must answer:

> What lives here that would be harmed if I treated this place only as a strategic object?

This is the question.

Not:

> How do I make this look good with a cocktail flag?

Not:

> Can I get a TikTok out of this?

Not:

> Does this bird support my interpretation of British interests?

The habitat question is there to interrupt map-brain.

A place is not only useful because humans have a dispute about it.

A place is alive before it is useful.

## Optional Bonus Task — Respectful Postcard

Contestants may submit one postcard-style note from each territory.

The postcard must include:

- one local wildlife observation;
- one local habitat observation;
- one local rule they followed;
- one thing they learned that was not visible on the map;
- one sentence beginning: “I previously failed to consider…”

Example:

> I previously failed to consider that a territory can be strategically discussed for years by people who have never once noticed the birds.

That is growth.

---

## Automatic Fail Conditions

The contestant fails this module if they:

- feed wildlife;
- touch protected species;
- stand on coral;
- disturb nests;
- block nesting beaches;
- chase animals for photos;
- use flash where prohibited;
- pose wildlife with flags;
- ask monkeys to hold political materials;
- ask penguins to endorse maritime policy;
- describe biodiversity as “a distraction”;
- leave litter;
- or return with 400 selfies and no evidence that the territory contains anything except their own head.

## Working Principle

A territory is not a postcard because it is pretty.

It is a postcard because it proves the contestant encountered a place.

The point is not tourism.

The point is attention.

If you can notice:

- the people;
- the law;
- the weather;
- the wildlife;
- the plants;
- the habitat;
- the vulnerability;
- the distance;
- the maintenance;
- and the fact that the place existed before your opinion;

then you are slightly less likely to mistake a map for reality.

Only slightly.

Production remains cautious.
