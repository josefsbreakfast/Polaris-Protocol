### 1. High Court as Panic Move
Judicial review in the High Court is:
- prohibitively expensive,
- slow by design,
- reputationally risky,
- and precedent-sensitive.

Even when fast-tracked or supported by pro bono counsel, this route signals **serious backing, serious fear, or everything to lose** â€” often all three.

No newsroom confident in its regulatory footing chooses this path over a classification issue unless the classification threatens its operating model.

---

### 2. Eternal Losses, Infinite Resolve
Sustained financial losses do not indicate weakness here â€” they indicate **non-market funding tolerance**.

Loss-making media entities that continue to litigate at this level are not behaving commercially.  
They are behaving **ideologically**.

Litigation becomes infrastructure:
- a legitimacy-laundering exercise,
- a precedent firewall,
- and a warning shot to regulators.

---

### 3. Sacred Territory Defence
The intensity of the response reveals the shrine.

In this case, the sacred ground is:
- the blurred boundary between *news*, *current affairs*, and *opinion*,
- particularly when occupied by sitting or former politicians.

This ambiguity allows:
- authority to be worn without accountability,
- power to speak as neutrality,
- and influence to masquerade as journalism.

Once a regulator names that trick, even imperfectly, panic follows.

---

### 4. Overreaction as Disclosure
The public statement style matters.

Indicators of panic include:
- excessive legal framing,
- anticipatory rebuttals to criticisms not yet made,
- oscillation between victimhood and triumph,
- and language aimed as much at regulators as at audiences.

This is not messaging for celebration.
It is messaging for **containment**.

---

### 5. Why This Is Not a Loss
Transparency advocates often misread these moments as defeat.

In reality:
- the pressure point has been mapped,
- the escalation threshold exposed,
- and the dependency publicly demonstrated.

The regulator may lose on procedure.
The system gains coordinates.
