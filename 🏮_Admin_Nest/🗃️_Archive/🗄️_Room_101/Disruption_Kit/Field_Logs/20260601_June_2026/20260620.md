Make Russia’s war-machine physically, financially, and diplomatically non-viable faster than it can regenerate; make Ukraine harder to break than Russia is willing or able to pay for; then give Moscow an off-ramp that is visibly a defeat but can be sold internally as “avoiding worse.”

The core logic

Russia’s war depends on three external lifelines:

1. Money: oil, gas, shadow-fleet shipping, illicit finance, insurance, and non-Western buyers.
2. Inputs: imported electronics, machine tools, drone parts, optics, bearings, chemicals, vehicles, and other dual-use components.
3. Strategic permissiveness: Western-aligned states repeatedly doing things elsewhere that fracture legitimacy, distract attention, raise oil prices, or give Moscow sovereignty-propaganda ammunition.

The question is not whether sanctions exist. They do.

The question is whether allies enforce them as a strategic campaign rather than as paperwork.

What “full-force compliance” would actually mean

1. Shut the shadow fleet as a war-finance system

Russia’s shadow fleet exists to move sanctioned oil and keep war revenue flowing. SWP’s May 2026 analysis says the shadow fleet primarily serves to evade oil sanctions and finance the war, while also creating defence, espionage, sabotage and environmental risks for Europe.  

A serious allied posture would mean:

* detain vessels with false insurance, unsafe ownership chains, spoofed tracking, or sanctions-linked cargoes;
* deny port services, bunkering, classification, insurance and brokering;
* sanction beneficial owners, managers, insurers, flag registries, cargo buyers and banks;
* treat shadow-fleet operations as maritime-security threats, not merely trade irregularities;
* confiscate cargo where legally available and use proceeds for Ukraine.

The UK has already shown the shape of this. AP reported that British forces detained the Smyrtos in the English Channel on 14 June 2026, a Cameroon-flagged tanker believed linked to Russia’s shadow fleet.  

That matters because every detained or uninsurable vessel raises Russia’s transaction cost. It does not need to stop every barrel. It needs to turn each barrel into a more discounted, delayed, risky, legally contaminated barrel.

* impose strict end-use checks on high-risk exports going through Türkiye, the Gulf, Central Asia, China/Hong Kong, India, Thailand and the Caucasus;
* sanction repeat transshipment firms, freight forwarders, customs brokers and banks;
* require manufacturers to monitor serial numbers and abnormal demand spikes;
* criminally pursue “knowable blindness,” not only proven intent;
* cut access to Western markets for firms that repeatedly feed Russian military supply chains.

* no elective escalations that raise oil prices without strategic necessity;
* no Syria/Iraq/Red Sea/Libya moves that create easy Russian propaganda unless the legal and coalition basis is watertight;
* no public splits between the US, UK, EU, Türkiye, Israel and Gulf partners that let Russia arbitrage the dispute;
* no half-measures where Western partners announce sanctions while quietly tolerating evasion by politically convenient intermediaries.

This is the “stop scoring own goals” layer.

A separate report citing Ukraine’s defence ministry says Ukraine could produce up to 20 million drones annually and thousands of missiles if partner funding is available.  

That is the pivot.

Western allies should not only send what they have. They should buy what Ukraine can build.

The practical posture is:

* multi-year contracts with Ukrainian producers;
* rapid co-production inside Ukraine and in neighbouring NATO states;
* guaranteed purchases for interceptors, drones, EW, counter-drone systems, artillery ammunition and repair capacity;
* fast financing rather than slow grant theatre;
* insurance and redundancy for factories exposed to Russian strikes.

This creates the correct asymmetry:

Russia gets slower, more expensive, more sanctioned imports.
Ukraine gets faster, cheaper, locally adapted production.

That is how the battlefield cost curve changes.

Russia’s strategy depends on exhausting Ukraine’s cities, grid, factories, ports and civilians. Therefore Ukraine’s defensive position has to be built around air defence, counter-drone coverage, radar, repair capacity and energy resilience.

That is the right shape, but it has to be scaled and accelerated.

Pressure	Effect on Russia

1. War costs remain high.
2. Revenue becomes less reliable.
3. Military replacement becomes slower and more expensive.

* Russia drops demands for Ukrainian demilitarisation or regime change.
* Russia accepts monitored withdrawal from some occupied areas as part of sanctions relief.

* Moscow seeks negotiations because it cannot generate enough pressure to improve its position.

That is still defeat in strategic terms if Russia’s original war aims were to subjugate Ukraine, break its Western alignment, and prove that NATO support would fail.

The positioning formula

The allied line should be:

Ukraine receives enough defensive depth to make conquest impossible. Russia loses enough external supply and oil revenue to make continued offensive war irrational. Sanctions relief is offered only for verifiable withdrawal, reparations mechanisms, prisoner returns, child returns, and durable security guarantees.

That is not appeasement. That is coercive diplomacy from a position of strength.

Before winter 2026:
The offer becomes: withdraw/settle under verification and recover limited, staged economic relief — or continue into another winter with shrinking revenue, rising interdiction, and a Ukraine that is harder to break than before.

That is the path.

The hard truth

This only works if NATO members and close partners stop behaving as if Ukraine policy is separate from:

* Türkiye trade routes;

Russia’s war is integrated. The response has to be integrated too.

The plain version:

Cut the money. Cut the components. Cut the loopholes. Stop gifting Russia chaos. Fund Ukraine’s own production. Protect Ukraine’s sky and grid. Then make Moscow choose between a bad settlement now and a worse war later.

That is how Ukraine can be positioned to defend its territory while Russia is pushed toward conceding strategic defeat within the year.
