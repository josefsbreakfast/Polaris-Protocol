# 🍽️ Fork Taxonomy  
**First created:** 2025-08-11 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-30  
*A reader-facing taxonomy for recurring behavioural distortion patterns in complex systems.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation

**Fork Taxonomy** maps recurring patterns where behaviour splits, drifts, duplicates, degrades, sanitises, reverts, or moves away from the expected path.

A fork is not automatically evidence of a deliberate intervention.

A fork is a recognisable divergence.

It may appear in:

- platforms
- institutions
- workflows
- interfaces
- records
- services
- communications
- automated systems
- human decision chains
- bureaucratic processes

Forks can feel personal, consistent, or intentional.

Sometimes they are.

More often, they emerge from complex systems under pressure: old infrastructure, mismatched incentives, partial automation, bad handovers, conflicting rules, stale metadata, and small nudges that create larger cascades.

This folder exists to help readers recognise fork-like patterns without jumping too quickly to attribution.

The working sequence is:

```text
Observe first.
Classify second.
Interpret third.
Attribute last.
```

---

## ⚖️ Personalised Is Not Always Personal

Fork-like behaviour can feel highly specific.

A process may break in the same place.  
A correction may keep reverting.  
A system may appear to comply while the substance quietly changes.  
A workflow may seem to split around one person, case, group, or issue.

That does not automatically mean a named individual is being manually targeted.

Modern systems often act through:

- automated routing
- risk categories
- queue logic
- audience segmentation
- fraud scoring
- recommender loops
- profile inference
- legacy records
- permission layers
- system defaults
- staff incentives
- templated responses
- institutional self-protection

The key distinction is:

```text
Personalised does not always mean personally targeted.
Targeted does not always mean individualised.
Individualised does not always mean a human chose you.
```

A fork may be aimed at a category, population, risk group, customer type, political audience, geographic region, demographic cluster, or administrative class rather than a named person.

A fork may also emerge without any targeting at all.

That does not make the effect harmless.

It means the evidence has to stay clean.

The practical task is to pattern the divergence well enough that the system can be challenged, repaired, routed around, or documented later.

## 🌳 System Model — The Environment Matters

Modern digital and institutional systems are not clean, rational machines.

A better model is:

- **a patched legacy system**  
  Decades of additions, migrations, policies, exceptions, and partial repairs.

- **a semi-wild environment**  
  More like a forest than a manicured garden.

- **a mixed human-machine process**  
  Automation, templates, human discretion, escalation rules, and institutional habits interacting at once.

In this environment:

- some behaviours are predictable
- some are emergent
- some are caused by small disturbances
- some persist because nothing interrupts them
- some are shaped by incentives rather than instructions
- some appear intentional because the same conditions keep producing the same effect

> You are not walking through a controlled garden.  
> You are navigating a system with hidden dependencies, feedback loops, and uneven maintenance.

That does not mean nothing is deliberate.

It means apparent intention needs evidence.

## 🌊 How Fork Patterns Emerge

Fork-like behaviours typically arise through three interacting layers.

---

### 1. Structural Instability

The baseline system may be:

- layered
- outdated
- partially inconsistent
- poorly documented
- difficult to observe
- connected to legacy databases
- dependent on manual workarounds
- built across multiple teams or vendors

This creates conditions where errors, reversions, duplications, delays, and mismatches can occur without anyone intending them.

Examples:

- a record keeps reverting after correction
- duplicate profiles behave differently
- one system updates while another does not
- a form accepts a change but the backend does not
- a case is repeatedly routed to the wrong place

Structural instability is boring.

It is also powerful.

---

### 2. Directional Influence

Small inputs can bias outcomes.

These may include:

- UI changes
- policy tweaks
- moderation rules
- incentive adjustments
- triage thresholds
- risk flags
- ranking systems
- staff scripts
- escalation templates
- internal performance metrics

These do not always control the system directly.

They nudge it.

Because the system is already unstable, small nudges can create large and uneven effects.

Examples:

- a wording change alters how cases are triaged
- a risk flag changes the route of a process
- a recommender update shifts what users repeatedly see
- a staff script produces the same evasive response across contexts
- a platform tweak makes one type of content harder to surface

Directional influence can be intentional without being fully controlled.

---

### 3. Intent

Some fork-like behaviours are shaped deliberately.

This may involve:

- role protection
- reputational management
- risk avoidance
- narrative control
- selective delay
- defensive bureaucracy
- local human decisions
- coordinated strategic action in some cases

However, deliberate action usually operates inside the system.

It does not necessarily control the whole system.

Even intentional behaviour may be filtered through automation, templates, queues, incentives, legacy infrastructure, and institutional habit.

This is why the taxonomy avoids both extremes:

```text
Not everything is a conspiracy.
Not everything is an accident.
```

The evidence decides the confidence level.

---

## 🍽️ What “Forks” Represent

Within this taxonomy, a fork is:

> A recognisable pattern where system behaviour splits, drifts, duplicates, reverts, sanitises, degrades, or performs compliance while changing substance.

Forks are best understood as symptoms of system conditions first.

They may later become evidence of intent, neglect, design failure, policy failure, or coordinated action — but that attribution comes after the pattern has been documented.

A fork is useful because it names the divergence without overclaiming the cause.

## 🧿 How To Use This Index

Use this node to:

- identify which fork pattern best matches what you are observing
- separate pattern recognition from assumptions about intent
- understand how structural conditions, incentives, automation, and human decisions interact
- document divergence clearly enough that it can be reviewed later
- avoid treating every repeated weirdness as either harmless accident or deliberate attack

This is a navigation tool.

It is not a definitive explanation engine.

## 🧭 Quick Classification Guide

| Observed behaviour | Possible fork type |
|---|---|
| A process splits into two inconsistent versions | Fork clone / clone map |
| A correction keeps being undone | Structural reversion |
| A task is accepted but quietly degraded | Execution drift |
| Language becomes softer until the meaning changes | Subtle sanitiser |
| The same credential or phrase repeats instead of substance | Reverent repetition |
| The system fragments under pressure | Shatterfork |
| A shutdown attempt exposes deeper instability | Shutdown protocol |
| Emotional or stylistic behaviour appears reused | Voice capture conditions |

Start with the most observable behaviour.

Then ask:

- What changed?
- What repeated?
- What degraded?
- What reverted?
- What stayed formally compliant while changing in substance?
- What ordinary system conditions could explain it?
- What evidence would support a stronger attribution?

## 🧪 Minimum Logging Standard

When recording a fork, capture:

| Field | Prompt |
|---|---|
| Timestamp | When did the divergence occur? |
| Surface | Which platform, institution, workflow, interface, service, or process? |
| Expected path | What should have happened? |
| Observed path | What actually happened? |
| Fork behaviour | Split, drift, duplication, sanitising, reversion, degradation, repetition, collapse |
| Trigger conditions | What happened immediately before the fork appeared? |
| Repetition pattern | Has this happened before? Where? |
| User effect | What delay, confusion, pressure, erosion, or harm did it create? |
| Residual evidence | Screenshot, message, file, metadata, ticket, witness note, archive trace |
| Alternative explanations | What boring technical, administrative, or human explanations are plausible? |
| Confidence level | Known, inferred, uncertain, speculative |
| Interpretation | What might this reveal about the system? |

A useful fork log distinguishes:

```text
The expected route.
The route actually taken.
The point of divergence.
The repeated pattern.
The evidence available.
The interpretation currently justified.
```

## ⚖️ Interpretation Safety Notes

Forks can feel directed because they are patterned.

But pattern does not automatically prove coordination.

Use the following standard:

> Treat the divergence as meaningful.  
> Do not overclaim what caused it.

Avoid assuming:

- coordination from repetition alone
- malice from inconvenience alone
- individual targeting from personalised effect alone
- central control from consistent output alone
- harmlessness because a system is automated
- neutrality because a process uses official language

A fork can be harmful without being planned.

A fork can be planned without being fully controlled.

The point of this taxonomy is to keep enough discipline that both possibilities remain visible until the evidence supports one more strongly.
