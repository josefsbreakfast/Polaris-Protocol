## 🛰️ Orientation

**Glitchy Ghosties** is the apparitional branch for ghost-like behaviour in digital, bureaucratic, archival, or algorithmic systems.

A “ghost” in Polaris terms is not supernatural.

It is what happens when a system behaves as if it remembers, recognises, follows, repeats, or misreads something — especially when the mechanism is unclear to the person experiencing it.

Ghosts can emerge when systems:

- misremember
- misinfer
- miscategorise
- resurface old material
- connect unrelated records
- overfit to behaviour
- construct phantom associations
- interpret a person through partial or stale data

The important point is this:

> A system can feel personal without being personally directed.

This folder exists to help readers classify ghost-like behaviour carefully, keep evidence clean, and avoid jumping too quickly from “this feels targeted” to “this must have been manually targeted.”

The goal is not dismissal.

The goal is grounded pattern recognition.

---

## ⚖️ Personalised Is Not Always Personal

Modern systems can produce highly specific-feeling outputs without anyone sitting there choosing one named individual.

A recommendation, advert, interface change, resurfaced memory, or strange recurrence may feel intimate because it is based on:

- behavioural clustering
- inferred interests
- audience segmentation
- recommender feedback loops
- automated profiling
- lookalike modelling
- stale metadata
- incorrect metadata
- previous engagement patterns
- population-level targeting
- badly maintained system memory

This matters because grounding protects the analysis.

If every strange output is treated as deliberate individual targeting, the pattern becomes harder to study. The user becomes more frightened, the evidence becomes noisier, and the system behaviour becomes harder to map.

The safer working distinction is:

```text
Personalised does not always mean personally targeted.
Targeted does not always mean individualised.
Individualised does not always mean a human chose you.
```

This does not mean targeted action is impossible.

It means attribution should come last.

More often, ghost-like behaviour is produced by automated systems acting on groups, categories, probabilities, inferred traits, or behavioural patterns. Even where targeting exists, it may be aimed at a population, cohort, region, identity category, consumer segment, political audience, or behavioural type rather than a named individual.

The practical task is to stay grounded enough to pattern the behaviour.

Ask:

- What exactly appeared?
- What category might the system have inferred?
- What behaviour might have triggered it?
- Is this happening to other people in the same group?
- Is there evidence of individual selection, or only personalised output?
- What would a boring technical explanation look like?
- What evidence would change the confidence level?

The goal is not to minimise the experience.

The goal is to keep the record clear enough that something useful can be done with it later.

## 🧿 The Four Ghost Families

Glitchy Ghosties uses four main ghost families.

Choose the primary mechanism first.

Do not classify by vibe alone.

---

### 🍄 1. Memetic Ghosts

**Memetic Ghosts** are behaviour-triggered narrative entities.

They arise when recommender systems, feeds, adverts, search results, prompts, memes, or content patterns seem to “speak back” to the user.

They may feel:

- oddly timed
- second-person
- emotionally pointed
- uncannily relevant
- as if a system has picked up a theme and is now repeating it back

Common causes may include:

- recent searches
- adjacent content engagement
- audience modelling
- shared cultural templates
- algorithmic clustering
- trend-based recommendation
- repeated interaction with similar material

Use this category when the main issue is:

```text
The system appears to be producing a narrative that feels directed at the user.
```

Useful question:

> Is this a content pattern, recommendation pattern, or repeated narrative motif?

---

### 👾 2. Metadata Ghosts

**Metadata Ghosts** are data errors that behave like entities.

They appear when records, tags, labels, permissions, account states, names, dates, attachments, locations, or identifiers become inconsistent, stale, orphaned, duplicated, or contradictory.

They may feel:

- impossible to correct
- administratively sticky
- weirdly persistent
- like a shadow record is following the user
- like one system knows something another system denies

Common causes may include:

- stale records
- duplicate accounts
- mismatched identifiers
- cached information
- broken synchronisation
- partial deletion
- legacy database fields
- migration errors
- inconsistent permissions
- automated enrichment or tagging

Use this category when the main issue is:

```text
The system’s records do not line up, and the error behaves as if it has a life of its own.
```

Useful question:

> Is this primarily a record problem?

---

### 🪦 3. Haunting Ghosts

**Haunting Ghosts** are old material behaving like it is still present.

They appear when past content, earlier states, old searches, previous identities, legacy accounts, historical errors, unresolved flags, or outdated associations keep resurfacing.

They may feel like:

- algorithmic déjà vu
- the past looping
- an old profile still active
- previous behaviour shaping current treatment
- a correction that never fully took
- a system refusing to update its image of the user

Common causes may include:

- old engagement signals
- cached content
- historical records
- archived data
- unresolved account links
- model memory at population level
- third-party data persistence
- recommendation loops
- delayed system updates

Use this category when the main issue is:

```text
Something from the past keeps returning as if it is still current.
```

Useful question:

> Is the system treating old information as present-tense information?

---

### 🪹 4. Network Ghosts

**Network Ghosts** are phantom social graphs.

They appear when a system infers relationships, communities, affiliations, locations, tastes, risks, identities, or social proximity that the user has not explicitly declared.

They may feel like:

- being placed in the wrong group
- being associated with people or themes by correlation
- seeing content meant for a nearby audience
- a system imagining a community around the user
- a platform drawing conclusions from proximity rather than consent

Common causes may include:

- shared devices
- shared networks
- location signals
- contact graph inference
- similar browsing behaviour
- demographic clustering
- audience expansion
- lookalike modelling
- engagement with adjacent material
- inferred community membership

Use this category when the main issue is:

```text
The system appears to be guessing the user’s relationships, group, affiliation, or community.
```

Useful question:

> Is this a phantom network, inferred category, or imagined association?

## 🧭 How To Classify A Ghost

Start with the mechanism.

| Question | Likely ghost type |
|---|---|
| Is it mainly a content pattern that feels like it is speaking back? | 🍄 Memetic Ghost |
| Is it mainly a broken, stale, contradictory, or sticky record? | 👾 Metadata Ghost |
| Is it mainly old material resurfacing as if current? | 🪦 Haunting Ghost |
| Is it mainly an inferred relationship, group, or affiliation? | 🪹 Network Ghost |

If more than one applies, choose the primary mechanism and cross-reference the secondary one.

Example:

```text
An old search topic keeps reappearing in recommendations after a platform update.
```

Possible classification:

- Primary: 🪦 Haunting Ghost, because old material is resurfacing.
- Secondary: 🍄 Memetic Ghost, because the recurrence appears through content and narrative.
- Possible secondary: 🪹 Network Ghost, if the content appears tied to an inferred audience or group.

Do not force the cleanest label if the system itself is messy.

The point is not to make the weirdness neat.

The point is to make the record usable.

## 📘 How To Log A Ghost

Every ghost entry should include:

| Field | Prompt |
|---|---|
| Timestamp | When did it happen? |
| Surface | Which platform, database, system, app, institution, feed, or interface? |
| Ghost type | Memetic, metadata, haunting, network, or mixed |
| Trigger conditions | What happened immediately before it appeared? |
| Manifestation | What appeared, repeated, changed, resurfaced, or misrecognised? |
| User effect | What confusion, friction, fear, erosion, pressure, or delay did it create? |
| Residual evidence | Screenshot, quote, file, metadata, link, archive trace, witness note |
| Alternative explanations | What ordinary explanations are plausible? |
| Interpretation | What might this reveal about the system? |
| Confidence level | Known, inferred, uncertain, speculative |

A good ghost log separates:

```text
What happened.
What it felt like.
What evidence remains.
What interpretation is justified.
What remains uncertain.
```

That separation is protective.

It keeps the record useful under pressure.

## ⚖️ Interpretation Safety Notes

Ghost-like behaviour can feel intensely personal.

That does not mean it is always personally directed.

Use the following standard:

> Treat the anomaly as worth recording.  
> Do not overclaim what caused it.

Avoid assuming:

- intent from weirdness alone
- surveillance from relevance alone
- coordination from repetition alone
- malice from friction alone
- innocence from automation alone
- neutrality from institutional language alone

A system may cause real harm without conscious malice.

A deliberate action may also hide inside ordinary system behaviour.

The task is to hold both possibilities without collapsing into either panic or dismissal.
