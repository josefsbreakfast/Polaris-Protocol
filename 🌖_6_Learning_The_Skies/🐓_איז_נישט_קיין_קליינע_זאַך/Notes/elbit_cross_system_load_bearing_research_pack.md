# Elbit as Cross-System Load-Bearing Node - Research Checkpoint Pack
**Date:** 2026-05-26  
**Status:** Research notes for writer / briefing use. Not legal advice.  
**Scope:** Public-domain sourcing plus clearly labelled analysis developed from the conversation.  

---

## 1. Executive thesis

- **Core working thesis:** Elbit Systems is useful as a bridge-point case study because the same corporate name carries different legal, strategic, economic and symbolic weight across different jurisdictions.
- **Safer phrasing:** Elbit may be a *cross-system load-bearing node* rather than a single uniform object.
- **Even safer phrasing:** Elbit can be analysed as a *defence-sector stress case* showing how modern defence, tech, procurement, protest and international-law systems behave under prolonged atrocity-risk conditions.
- **Do not overclaim:** The public sources do not prove that any state is protecting Elbit because of one named contract, nor do they prove corrupt coordination. They do support the more careful claim that Elbit is materially embedded in several allied defence ecosystems.
- **Writing north star:** The present aim is not to settle every ideological question about Israel/Palestine, Zionism, anti-Zionism, defence procurement or direct action. The immediate legal/humanitarian anchor is the ICJ provisional-measures framework and the protection of Palestinians from genocide-risk and related prohibited acts. [S1-S5]

## 2. Public-domain factual anchors

### 2.1 ICJ / Genocide Convention anchor

- The ICJ case is **Application of the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in the Gaza Strip (South Africa v. Israel)**. [S1]
- On **26 January 2024**, the ICJ issued provisional measures. The order did not make a final genocide finding. It did find that at least some rights claimed under the Genocide Convention were plausible and ordered measures including prevention of Article II acts, prevention and punishment of direct/public incitement, humanitarian assistance, evidence preservation and reporting. [S2]
- On **28 March 2024**, the ICJ issued further provisional measures / modifications connected to deteriorating humanitarian conditions. [S3]
- On **24 May 2024**, the ICJ reaffirmed earlier measures and issued further measures concerning Rafah and humanitarian access. [S4, S10]
- The Genocide Convention requires prevention and punishment; Article III also includes complicity, attempt and direct/public incitement, and Article IV applies to constitutionally responsible rulers, public officials and private individuals. [S5]
- Research note: for briefing purposes, use “ICJ provisional measures / genocide-risk legal atmosphere” rather than “ICJ proved genocide” unless discussing later legal findings from a separately sourced body.

### 2.2 Elbit corporate / macroeconomic anchor

- Elbit reported **$7.9386bn revenue for 2025** and **$28.1bn order backlog at 31 Dec 2025**. About 72% of backlog was attributable to orders from outside Israel. [S11]
- Reuters reported on **26 May 2026** that Elbit Q1 2026 revenue rose to about **$2.2bn** and backlog reached **$30.2bn**, with 71% of orders from outside Israel. [S12]
- Reuters reported Israel defence exports reached nearly **$15bn in 2024**, up 13%, with Europe accounting for 54% and the US 9%. [S13]
- The Bank of Israel Annual Report 2025 describes a fiscal environment shaped by high defence expenditure, debt/deficit management and measures aimed at market confidence. [S14]
- Analytical implication: Elbit is not only a defence company; in Israel's wider economic frame it sits inside a defence-export and war-economy ecosystem with macroeconomic relevance. Treat that as structural context, not a claim that the Bank of Israel directs corporate decisions. [S11-S14]

## 3. Jurisdiction-specific load-bearing profiles

### 3.1 Israel: domestic strategic infrastructure

- Public sources identify Elbit as Israel's largest defence company and a major exporter; company financials and Reuters reporting show a very large backlog and significant international demand. [S11-S13]
- The Israel profile is the most direct: defence-industrial capacity, wartime production, skilled employment, export revenue, military-tech development and domestic strategic capability.
- For Israel, harm to Elbit can plausibly be framed by state/security actors as harm to defence readiness, wartime production and strategic export capacity.
- Analytical caution: “load-bearing” here does not mean Elbit is the only load-bearing defence actor. Rafael, Israel Aerospace Industries and other firms also matter. Elbit is the useful bridge-point case because it is visible, transnational and repeatedly targeted in public activism.

### 3.2 UK: visible programme/site contractor and protest flashpoint

- A UK government FOI response states that Thales UK was prime contractor for Watchkeeper and that UAV Tactical Systems Ltd., an Elbit/Thales joint venture, supplied the Watchkeeper air vehicle under subcontract. [S15]
- Watchkeeper is a UK drone/ISTAR case that made Elbit comparatively publicly legible in the UK as a UAV-related actor. [S15-S17]
- Later reporting describes Watchkeeper as troubled and slated for early retirement, with subsequent reporting indicating service extension to March 2027. [S16-S17]
- Public-order/legal-risk context: UK prosecutions connected to actions at Elbit sites are in the public domain. Reuters reported February 2026 acquittals on aggravated burglary and other outcomes, then May 2026 criminal-damage convictions for four activists in relation to a 2024 Elbit facility raid. [S31-S32]
- The UK government also announced the proscription of Palestine Action under the Terrorism Act 2000; that official source should be cited as the government's position, while critiques should be separately sourced if used. [S30]
- Analytical implication: the UK profile is highly visible because company/site/protest/procurement narratives collide in one public space. The risk calculation is not only “brand reputation” but also defence procurement, site security, public order, protest law and state credibility.

### 3.3 US: distributed subsystem supplier, not one obvious drone programme

- Pre-2026 public sources show Elbit America embedded in US Army/USAF capability areas: mortar fire-control, mortar systems, F-16 missile warning and F-16 head-up display replacement. [S18-S23]
- In 2017 Elbit America announced a US Army ID/IQ contract up to **$102m** for mortar fire-control systems; Reuters independently reported the deal. [S18-S19]
- In 2022 Elbit Systems announced an approximately **$49m** ID/IQ contract for 120mm mortar systems for the US Army. [S20]
- In 2020 Elbit America announced a US Air Force contract to supply pylon-based infrared missile-warning systems for F-16s; the wider reported ceiling was **$471.6m**. [S21]
- In 2024 Elbit America announced a USAF F-16 Block 40/42 Wide-Angle Conventional Head-Up Display replacement ID/IQ ceiling up to **$89m**, with a first delivery order over **$57.5m**. [S23]
- In December 2025 a US government contract notice listed **$49.9m** for Elbit America Heads Up displays with estimated completion in 2030. [S22]
- Post-2026 culmination: the Soldier Borne Mission Command award and next-generation binocular night vision are strong evidence of the direction of travel into battlefield-awareness/soldier-systems architecture, but they should not be used as pre-2026 evidence. [S24-S25]
- Analytical implication: the US load-bearing profile is **distributed**: soldier systems, vision, displays, platform survivability, fire control and C2-adjacent technologies. It is less like the UK Watchkeeper story and more like an embedded supplier/subsystem mesh.

### 3.4 Germany: rearmament / NATO-readiness partner profile

- Elbit Systems Germany publicly describes Bundeswehr-relevant work in drone defence, communication, C2, reconnaissance and target designation, with E-LynX radios produced in Ulm for the DLBO digitisation programme and already in Bundeswehr use. [S26]
- Elbit announced a **$260m** contract to supply DIRCM self-protection systems for Germany's A400M fleet. [S27]
- Reuters reported Lufthansa Technik would work with Elbit on a German Navy Hermes 900 Starliner military drone project, with Lufthansa Technik handling maintenance/training and Elbit producing drones; the project sits in Germany's post-2022 defence-spending environment. [S28]
- Reuters also reported wider European demand for Elbit PULS/advanced rocket systems. [S29]
- Analytical implication: Germany's profile is shaped by Bundeswehr modernisation, NATO readiness, aircraft self-protection, soldier radios, drone capabilities, long-range fires and Germany's distinct Israel-policy/legal-memory environment.

## 4. Cross-system analysis from the conversation

### 4.1 “Jurisdictionally polymorphic” company object

- The same company can be viewed differently depending on who is looking:
  - Israel: domestic defence-industrial infrastructure.
  - UK: visible foreign-linked contractor and public-order flashpoint.
  - US: US subsidiary embedded across multiple capability stacks.
  - Germany: supplier inside accelerated rearmament and NATO-readiness architecture.
  - Public activists: symbol of Israeli military technology and alleged complicity.
  - Investors/banks/insurers: financial, legal, reputational and continuity risk.
- Research use: this framing explains why apparently similar Western democracies may respond differently to protest/divestment/procurement scrutiny.

### 4.2 Compliance architecture can become containment architecture

- Defence procurement is often opaque for ordinary reasons: classification, export controls, security, commercial confidentiality, sensitive technology and sovereign procurement discretion.
- Under normal conditions, subsidiaries, local counsel, compliance teams and procurement segmentation can be read as strong governance design.
- Under post-October 2023 / ICJ-provisional-measures conditions, the same structures can appear to the public as opacity, exceptionalism, special treatment or accountability avoidance.
- Research use: this lets the writer avoid an over-simple “conspiracy” frame while still explaining why public suspicion is rationally produced.

### 4.3 Competent silos can still produce global under-modelling

- Each jurisdiction can reasonably model its own legal risk and avoid pretending to be expert in another jurisdiction.
- That is competent professional boundary-keeping, not inherently evasive.
- But when the issue crosses international humanitarian law, genocide-prevention duties, defence procurement, corporate governance, investor risk, protest law and diaspora grief, no single silo fully owns the climate system.
- Strong line for later drafting: **Each node models its own legal weather; nobody fully owns the climate system.**

### 4.4 Why Elbit becomes a “precedent-risk” object

- Elbit is not necessarily the only company with this profile. It is a conspicuous stress case.
- If legal theories, divestment campaigns, protest defences, procurement reviews or reputational pressure succeed against Elbit, other defence-tech, AI, surveillance, battlefield-awareness and dual-use firms may read the case as precedent risk for themselves.
- This could produce defensive behaviour from adjacent sectors without requiring a single coordinated conspiracy.
- Strong line: **Elbit may become the condensation point for wider defence-tech panic.**

### 4.5 Why the public reads opacity as authoritarian drift

- European publics bring multiple twentieth-century and colonial memory stacks to the field: fascism, collaboration, occupation, colonial violence, Algeria, Ireland, the Balkans, the Holocaust, Cold War securitisation, Berlin Wall memory, emergency powers, counterterrorism after 9/11, Iraq, Afghanistan and migration securitisation.
- Israeli and North American institutions may read European protest through other alarms: antisemitism risk, October 7 trauma, hostage grief, national security, anti-Israel mobilisation, foreign interference or extremism.
- The result is mutual misread: each party sees its own inherited catastrophe clearly and the other party's catastrophe as exaggerated, manipulative or hostile.

## 5. Legal/humanitarian discipline for later writing

### 5.1 Main harm first

- The briefing's centre of gravity should be explicit: protection of Palestinians under current atrocity-risk conditions and practical upholding of the ICJ provisional measures. [S1-S5]
- This does not require ideological closure on Israel's long-term constitutional future, anti-Zionism/Zionism, statehood models, reparations, return, security architecture or decolonial theory.
- It does require tactical discipline: do not let every adjacent legal/procurement/reputational side-front consume the anti-genocide objective.
- Strong line: **No genocide prevention while everyone is busy defending their own side-quest.**

### 5.2 De-escalation is not neutrality

- De-escalation here means lowering the ambient threat level so people and institutions can remain legally, morally and cognitively competent.
- It does not mean abandoning accountability, erasing Palestinian harm, abandoning Israeli hostage families, ignoring antisemitism, ignoring anti-Muslim racism or pretending defence infrastructure is apolitical.
- Strong line: **De-escalation is not moral neutrality; it is the precondition for lawful judgment and practical protection.**

### 5.3 Humanisation without exoneration

- ICRC sources support the proposition that dehumanization increases conflict and abuse risk and makes legal protections easier to set aside. [S7-S8]
- Therefore, refusing dehumanisation is not sentimental niceness. It is anti-escalation infrastructure.
- Humanisation does not require exoneration: a person, company, official, activist or soldier can be human and still legally or morally accountable.
- Strong line: **Rehumanisation is not absolution. It is anti-escalation infrastructure.**

### 5.4 Universal humanity; unequal exposure

- A careful note must not collapse power asymmetry. Palestinians in Gaza, the West Bank and East Jerusalem face a different order of immediate material danger than most diaspora actors or corporate/state actors.
- Better framing: many groups carry real historical fear and present insecurity, but they are not equally situated and do not wield equal coercive power.
- Strong line: **Universal humanity; unequal exposure.**

## 6. Strategic notes for a writer

### 6.1 What the writer can safely say

- Elbit is materially embedded in multiple defence ecosystems, but differently in each jurisdiction.
- Public procurement and corporate sources support a “distributed load-bearing” analysis in the US and a “visible programme/site contractor” analysis in the UK.
- German public sources support a Bundeswehr-modernisation/NATO-readiness profile involving radios, aircraft protection and drone-related work.
- Israeli financial/export sources support a macroeconomic/defence-export relevance frame.
- The ICJ orders create a concrete legal anchor for atrocity-risk and preventive-measures discussion.
- Modern defence compliance architecture can be strong in normal conditions and still fail to manage cross-jurisdictional atrocity-risk perception.

### 6.2 What the writer should not say without more evidence

- Do not say Elbit is the single reason states are policing protests in a particular way.
- Do not say all Western defence/tech firms are secretly coordinating to protect Elbit.
- Do not say the ICJ has made a final finding that Israel committed genocide unless using a later, specific source that says so and clearly distinguishes it from the ICJ merits case.
- Do not say all activists are irrational or all institutional responses are purely neutral.
- Do not say all affected groups are equally oppressed. Use “universal humanity; unequal exposure.”
- Do not use loose “8200 network everywhere” language. Use “dense military, intelligence, cyber and private-sector crossover in Israel's defence-tech ecosystem” only if later sourced.

### 6.3 Low-cost de-escalation measures worth developing later

- Public-facing explanation of what can/cannot be disclosed in defence procurement, with plain-English reasons.
- Independent review channels for arms/export/procurement risk that do not require citizens to infer everything from silence.
- Human-rights due-diligence escalation routes for companies in conflict-affected contexts. [S9]
- Community safety language that recognises antisemitism, anti-Muslim racism and Palestinian material exposure without treating them as interchangeable.
- Tactical discipline for activists: separate expressive action from effective harm-reduction action.
- Tactical discipline for institutions: avoid converting ordinary moral alarm into security threat language unless the facts truly require it.
- Rehumanisation protocols: prohibit exterminatory/dehumanising language across all sides while preserving accountability language.
