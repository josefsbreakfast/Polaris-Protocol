# ⚖️ When Everyone Was Just Doing Their Job  
**First created:** 2026-05-27 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-27  
*Systems-accountability node on distributed responsibility, institutional fragmentation, and the legitimacy cliff.*

## 🛰️ Orientation

Some harms are produced by villains.

Some harms are produced by systems in which everyone can explain their own limited role.

This node is about the second pattern.

The public does not need to believe in a conspiracy to reject a system. It only needs to see that everyone with power can explain their own role while nobody can explain why the harm was allowed to continue.

## 🧩 Limited Intention

Modern governance often divides responsibility into narrow roles.

One person drafts the policy.

One person checks the legal risk.

One person signs the contract.

One person enforces the rule.

One person handles communications.

One person manages reputational exposure.

One person processes the complaint.

One person says the matter sits elsewhere.

Each role may be limited.

Each actor may sincerely believe they are not the author of the harm.

That may even be true.

It may still not be enough.

## ⚖️ Distributed Responsibility

Distributed responsibility is not automatically bad.

Complex societies need delegation. Legal systems, public administration, procurement, universities, companies, charities, courts, police, and media bodies cannot operate without role division.

The problem begins when distributed responsibility becomes distributed moral insulation.

Each actor says:

- I did not make the whole decision.
- I only followed the relevant procedure.
- I acted within my remit.
- Other bodies are responsible for the wider question.
- This is not the forum for that issue.
- The legal position is complicated.
- We cannot comment on ongoing matters.

Individually, these statements may be reasonable.

Together, they can become a wall.

## 🕳️ Distributed Irresponsibility

Distributed irresponsibility appears when every actor can justify their part, but the system cannot justify the outcome.

This is especially dangerous where harm is:

- slow;
- cumulative;
- legally fragmented;
- reputationally managed;
- borne by exposed communities;
- difficult to attribute to a single decision-maker;
- protected by procedural complexity.

The result is not necessarily unlawful conduct by each actor.

The result is a legitimacy problem for the whole architecture.

## 📉 The Legitimacy Cliff

A legitimacy cliff appears when the public stops being reassured by process.

Before the cliff, institutions can say:

- there are procedures;
- the matter is under review;
- powers are being used lawfully;
- all relevant guidance has been followed;
- this is complex;
- we must be careful.

After the cliff, those same phrases stop reassuring people.

They begin to sound like evasion.

That is the moment when institutional language becomes evidence against itself.

## 🌫️ Why “It’s Complicated” Stops Working

“It’s complicated” is often true.

But when people are being harmed, complexity cannot be the final answer.

Complexity must produce better responsibility, not less responsibility.

If complexity only produces delay, fragmentation, and plausible deniability, then the public learns a brutal lesson:

the system is intelligent enough to protect itself, but not coherent enough to protect people.

That is where legitimacy breaks.

## 🛠️ Better Checks

A system trying to avoid distributed irresponsibility should ask:

- Who is carrying the consequence?
- Who has authority to reduce harm?
- Who benefits from delay?
- Who can say no?
- Who can stop the transfer of pressure?
- Who is being asked to absorb risk because other actors have cleaner paperwork?
- Where does local legality produce global incoherence?

These are not decorative ethics questions.

They are structural safety checks.
