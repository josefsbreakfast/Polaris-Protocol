# 🪬 Golem Ethics For AI  
**First created:** 2026-05-27 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-27  
*A Jewish question-space for thinking about AI, created systems, instruction, responsibility, and moral obligation.*

---

## 🛰️ Orientation  

This folder uses **golem ethics** as a Jewish question-space for AI and automated systems.

The golem is not used here as a one-to-one metaphor for AI.

This folder does not claim that contemporary AI systems reproduce the ethical problems of the Golem of Prague, or any other specific golem tradition, in a direct or literal way.

The comparison is more modest, and more useful.

The golem offers a Jewish symbolic doorway into questions that feel newly technological but are not entirely new:

- created force,
- instruction,
- obedience,
- protection,
- speech,
- responsibility,
- animation,
- restraint,
- and the danger of systems that act beyond ordinary human scale.

In that sense, the golem is not the answer to AI ethics.

It is a way of making the question recognisable.

---

## 🪬 Core Framing  

This folder uses golem ethics as artistic and ethical licence.

It does not flatten Jewish folklore, halachic reasoning, mysticism, or communal tradition into a simple technology metaphor.

It asks instead:

> What older Jewish question-spaces become useful when human beings create systems that obey, amplify, classify, protect, misread, or harm without being able to carry moral obligation?

This folder therefore invites correction, expansion, and redirection from people with deeper learning.

A rabbi, scholar, teacher, or fluent tradition-bearer may rightly say:

> The golem is not the best source here.  
> The better question is over there.

That would not be a failure of the folder.

That would be the bridge working.

---

## 🔁 The Inversion  

There is also an inversion here.

In many golem stories, the concern is that a made being may act too strongly, too literally, or beyond the maker’s control.

In contemporary AI systems, the danger may appear reversed.

The machine is not alive.  
The machine is not morally conscious.  
The machine is not obligated.  
The machine cannot repent.  
The machine cannot answer for harm.

And yet human systems may begin treating its outputs as if they can carry judgment, authority, responsibility, or permission.

The problem is not that AI has become a golem in any simple sense.

The problem is that human beings may begin handing moral weight to a made thing that cannot answer for harm.

## 🤖 Why AI Belongs In This Cluster  

The wider `🐓 איז נישט קיין קליינע זאַך` cluster is concerned with people operating inside systems where AI, defence technology, law, finance, public pressure, communal fear, and international crisis are interacting at once.

These systems can process information at great speed.

They can classify.  
They can rank.  
They can predict.  
They can optimise.  
They can recommend.  
They can model risk.  
They can make human beings appear as variables, targets, liabilities, datasets, stakeholders, or threats.

But they cannot carry moral obligation.

That matters because some people inside these systems may be tempted, trained, or pressured to treat technical output as if it settles the human question.

It does not.

AI may assist judgment.

It cannot replace the human responsibility to ask:

- who is harmed?
- who benefits?
- who is erased?
- who is misrecognised?
- who is being abstracted?
- who can appeal?
- who can correct the system?
- who answers if the system harms someone?

This folder exists to help make those questions harder to avoid.

## 🧠 Created Systems And Human Responsibility  

Created systems do not remove responsibility from their creators, operators, funders, deployers, supervisors, or beneficiaries.

A system may be automated and still humanly authorised.

A model may be complex and still humanly governed.

A decision pipeline may be distributed and still morally accountable.

A machine may act quickly, but speed does not erase obligation.

This folder asks what kinds of responsibility remain when human action becomes mediated through systems that appear intelligent but cannot be morally answerable.

It also asks what kinds of human beings those systems assume.

Do they assume people are:

- risks,
- souls,
- users,
- citizens,
- targets,
- patients,
- civilians,
- enemies,
- customers,
- obligations,
- or data points?

The answer matters.

A system’s anthropology becomes part of its ethics.

## 🪬 Questions For Counsel  

This folder brings AI ethics into questions of creation, instruction, speech, responsibility, and restraint.

- Who is responsible for the action of a made thing?
- What happens when a created system obeys too well?
- Can instruction replace judgment?
- Who may write the words that animate the system?
- Who may erase, correct, restrain, or deactivate it?
- What must never be delegated to a system that cannot carry moral obligation?
- When does technical obedience become moral danger?
- What kind of human being does the system assume?
- What happens when protection becomes harm?
- How should a person act when the system gives an answer but the human question remains open?

## 🌉 Relationship To The Wider Cluster  

The wider cluster asks religious scholars and trusted communal figures to help people under pressure recover steadiness, proportion, and moral courage.

This folder explains why AI makes that need more urgent.

AI systems can intensify the exact failures the wider cluster is concerned with:

- abstraction,
- speed,
- scale,
- delegated judgment,
- false neutrality,
- bureaucratic distance,
- risk conversion,
- dehumanising classification,
- and the disappearance of the person inside the model.

Golem ethics does not solve those problems.

It gives the conversation a doorway.

It says:

> human beings have asked before what happens when we create force, give it instruction, and then discover that obedience is not the same as wisdom.

That question belongs here.

---

## 🧭 Practical Use  

This folder should be useful for two audiences at once.

For people working in AI, defence technology, law, finance, modelling, procurement, or public systems, it offers a way to ask:

> What am I delegating, and should it be delegated?

For religious scholars, teachers, and communal figures, it offers a way to recognise that the technical problem may be carrying older ethical questions in unfamiliar clothing.

The goal is not to make AI mystical.

The goal is to keep AI from making human responsibility disappear.
